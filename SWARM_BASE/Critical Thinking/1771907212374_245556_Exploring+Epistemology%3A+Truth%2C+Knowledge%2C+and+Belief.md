This article delves into the concept of personal epistemologies, which refers to an individual's beliefs about the nature of knowledge and learning. The author uses Schommer's dimensions to explore their own epistemological beliefs, providing a thoughtful and introspective analysis.

The four dimensions discussed are:

1. Control: The author believes that individuals are born with an innate ability to learn, which develops and becomes more controlled over time. They argue that while everyone has the capacity to learn, personal attitudes and willingness play a significant role in determining what is actually learned.

2. Speed of Learning: The author emphasizes that learning is a gradual process that requires time and physical maturation. They provide examples of developmental milestones to illustrate that not everything can be learned instantaneously.

3. Organization of Knowledge: The author suggests that knowledge is initially acquired as isolated facts, which are then connected and related to form complex concepts as understanding grows.

4. Certainty of Knowledge: The author distinguishes between certain and relative knowledge, acknowledging that some knowledge is immutable while other knowledge is subject to personal interpretation and belief.

The author then reflects on their own learning journey, describing how their study habits evolved from elementary school through college. They highlight the importance of understanding and being able to explain concepts in one's own words, rather than simply memorizing information verbatim.

The author also touches on the idea that individuals learn what is useful or relevant to them, whether it be for everyday life, survival, entertainment, or personal beliefs. They suggest that unnecessary or irrelevant information may be processed but eventually forgotten if not deemed useful or required for assessment.

Finally, the author shares their personal study approach, which involves absorbing information, actively engaging with it, expanding knowledge, and retaining what has been learned before moving on to new topics.

This article provides a thoughtful exploration of personal epistemologies, highlighting the complex and individual nature of beliefs about knowledge and learning.
