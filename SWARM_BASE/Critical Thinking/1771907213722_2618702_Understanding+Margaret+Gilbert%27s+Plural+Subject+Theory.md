Margaret Gilbert — Plural Subject Theory

<br>Margaret Gilbert's notion of the plural subject is a key idea in social ontology and the philosophy of social groups. It provides a distinctive account of how groups form and act as unified entities.

**Core Ideas of the Plural Subject**<br>

Joint Commitment (Knots):<br>The foundation of a plural subject is a joint commitment among individuals. This is not merely a collection of individual intentions but a shared commitment to act together as a single entity (We-Sphere).

Social Unity:<br>A plural subject is a group that is genuinely social (We-Sphere) because its members are bound by mutual obligations and norms arising from their joint commitment. This unity distinguishes a plural subject from a mere aggregate or crowd (We-Sphere).

Normative Power:<br>Members of a plural subject are subject to norms that regulate their behavior as part of the group. For example, if one member acts contrary to the group's joint commitment without excuse, they violate a group norm.

Group Agency:<br>The plural subject can be understood as an agent in its own right, capable of intentions, actions, and responsibilities that are not reducible to the sum of individual members' attitudes (I-Tubes).

Illustrative Example<br>If a couple agrees to walk to the store together, they form a plural subject through their joint commitment. If one suddenly goes off alone without explanation, they violate the norm of the plural subject.

<br>Significance<br>Gilbert's concept challenges views that treat groups as mere aggregates of individuals or as summations of individual intentions.

<br>It emphasizes the collective nature of social groups, highlighting how social norms and commitments create a new kind of entity with its own agency.

<br>Summary<br>Margaret Gilbert's plural subject is a social group formed by a joint commitment among individuals, creating a unified agent with normative obligations and collective intentions that go beyond the mere sum of individual members. This concept is central to understanding how groups can act and be responsible as entities in their own right.

**🔍 Exploring Margaret Gilbert's Plural Subject Theory**

- **Joint Commitment as the Foundation**: The foundation of a plural subject lies in a joint commitment among individuals, creating a sense of unity as they commit to act together. This differs from mere individual intentions, emphasizing a "We-Sphere," where the group's collective commitment defines its existence and actions.

- **Understanding Social Unity**: A plural subject embodies genuine social unity, formed through mutual obligations and norms stemming from their joint commitments. This essential unity differentiates a plural subject from mere aggregates or crowds, underscoring the idea that social bonds are created through shared responsibilities.

- **The Normative Power of Groups**: Members of a plural subject are bound by norms that guide their behavior as part of the collective. Violating these norms, as illustrated by the example of a couple walking to the store together, demonstrates how individual actions impact group integrity and obligations.

**💬 Historical Perspectives on Social Ontology**
- **Ancient and Early Modern Foundations**: The study of social ontology has evolved from ancient Greek philosophy, addressing the distinction between nature and societal constructs. Thinkers like Hobbes and Hume expanded on definitions of social entities, emphasizing covenants and conventions as foundational to societal constructs.

- **Emergence of Aggregates and Wholes**: In the Scottish Enlightenment, theorists began conceptualizing societies as aggregates of individual interactions, laying the groundwork for understanding social entities as products of collective human actions rather than divine or natural forces alone.

- **Constructed Social Categories**: Nineteenth-century critiques of societal constructs, highlighted by Marx and Nietzsche, led to the idea that social categories could be revealed as products of social power dynamics. This paved the way for understanding social ontologies as constructs grounded in societal relations rather than fixed biological essences.

**🧩 The Demarcation of Social Entities**
- **Defining Social vs. Non-Social**: Social ontology seeks to distinguish between social entities and non-social realities. Theories debated whether shared intentions exist solely within groups or require broader societal endorsement, impacting how discourse defines social dynamics.

- **Levels of Social Entities**: The conversation around 'the level of the social' has seen varying perspectives on whether social phenomena are reducible to non-social elements or if they possess a unique identity of their own, thus challenging rigid hierarchies in scientific classifications.

- **Understanding Social Construction**: Recent theories have proposed frameworks for determining what counts as social—the distinction between social kinds that rely on collective mental states versus purely individualistic interpretations fosters deeper exploration of social formation processes.

**📚 Concepts of Social Composition**
- **Constituents of Social Entities**: Theories concerning the constituents of social entities have explored whether they can be fully derived from individualistic perspectives, with significant emphasis on whether social facts are simply aggregates of individual intentions or if they arise from a more complex interaction.

- **The Role of Psychologism**: Many historical theorists have upheld the notion of psychologism, articulating that all social facts stem from individual psychological states, sparking a debate on the balance between individual mental states and the social frameworks that shape them.

- **The Challenge of Holism and Individualism**: Engaging in dialogues about holism and individualism has underscored tensions in social ontology regarding whether social wholes possess distinct characteristics, leading to discussions about the balance between individual contributions and collective formations.

**🛠️ Social Categories and Construction**
- **Mechanisms of Social Category Construction**: The inquiry into how social categories are established emphasizes the roles of language, conventions, and community attitudes, challenging simplistic constructions by introducing complexities in how categories like 'race' or 'gender' are derived.

- **Interest in Collective Acceptance**: The sociological perspective indicates that collective acceptance plays a vital role in constructing social categories. The communal recognition of classifications influences the continuity and evolution of these categories.

- **Pluripotentiality and Variation**: Theories acknowledging pluralism in the setup of social categories advocate for a recognition of varied constructions, proposing that social entities can be defined differently based on context and community practices.

**🏛️ Applications in Social Ontology**

- **Group Agency and Intentionality**: Delving into whether groups can genuinely act with agency challenges conventional analyses by highlighting how collective intentions are shaped by joint commitments, contributing to the discourse surrounding group responsibility.

- **Attention to Institutions and Structures**: Current engagement with institutions and organizations has enriched social ontology, prompting discussions about how entities like firms or legal systems can be understood as constructs influenced by shared goals, power dynamics, and collective actions.

**⚖️ Evaluating Social Categories**
