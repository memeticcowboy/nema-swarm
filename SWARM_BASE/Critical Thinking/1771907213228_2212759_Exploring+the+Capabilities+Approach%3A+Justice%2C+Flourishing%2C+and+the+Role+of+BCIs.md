### Capabilities Approach

#### Definitions and Emphasis

Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum’s capabilities approach is about what people can actually do and be, not just what they have. Sen focuses on freedom, saying it’s about removing barriers like poverty so people can live lives they value. Nussbaum adds that it’s about human dignity, listing 10 key capabilities like health and education needed for a good life. Both see justice as making sure everyone has these chances to flourish, not just survive.

#### Extensions to Non-Human or Hybrid Agents

Nussbaum has extended this to animals, saying they deserve justice too, not just kindness, focusing on their ability to flourish. For hybrid agents, like brain-computer interfaces (BCIs), it’s less clear but research suggests we can look at how these technologies affect human capabilities, like helping disabled people communicate 

[](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13347-022-00597-1)

.

#### Evaluating Brain-Computer Interfaces and Neuroenhancement

Looking at BCIs or neuroenhancement, we’d ask: do they help people do what they value, like talk or learn better? For example, BCIs can help paralyzed people communicate, which is crucial for flourishing. But we also check if they create unfair advantages or risks, like privacy issues. It’s complex, with debates on balancing benefits and ethics.

#### Ethical Access to Cognitive Augmentation Tools

In education or jobs, the approach can help ensure everyone gets a fair shot at using tools like memory enhancers, especially if they’re needed to compete. It’s about making sure no one’s left behind, but there’s controversy over whether this creates new divides between rich and poor, and how to avoid pressure to use them.
