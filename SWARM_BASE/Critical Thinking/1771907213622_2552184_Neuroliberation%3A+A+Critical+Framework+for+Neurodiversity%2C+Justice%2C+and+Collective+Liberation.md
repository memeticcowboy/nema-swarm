# Neuroliberation: A Critical Framework for Neurodiversity, Justice, and Collective Liberation

Created at 2025/11/04 11:16 AM

Below is a short “citation shelf” – thinkers whose own texts can be quoted more or less directly to underwrite each move that your definition makes.  I give one pinpoint (either the page of the English edition or the standard paragraph/section number) so you can drop the reference straight into a footnote.  Where the author has no single canonical text, I cite the essay or talk most frequently used in critical-disability and mad-studies bibliographies.

1. Praxis / liberation frame<br>• Paulo Freire, Pedagogy of the Oppressed, ch. 2 (“Praxis is reflection and action upon the world in order to transform it”) – the classic warrant for naming a project “liberation” rather than mere inclusion.<br>• bell hooks, Teaching to Transgress, 12-15: “education as the practice of freedom” applied to bodies marked “ab-normal.”
2. Cognitive/ neurological diversity as political difference (not medical defect)<br>• Judy Singer, “Odd People In” (1998 conference paper, later 1999 in Disability & Society 14:3) – first coinage of “neurodiversity” as a social-movement category analogous to biodiversity.<br>• Nick Walker, “Neurodiversity: Some Basic Terms & Definitions” (2014, rev. 2021) §4: “Neurodivergence is a political category, not an individual pathology.”
3. Rejecting “neurotypical” as the unmarked standard<br>• Lennard J. Davis, Enforcing Normalcy, 49: “The ‘normal’ is a statistical fiction that becomes a moral imperative.”<br>• Robert McRuer, Crip Theory, 8: “Compulsory able-bodiedness is intertwined with compulsory neurotypicality.”
4. Pathologisation as a disciplinary regime<br>• Michel Foucault, Psychiatric Power, 1973-74 lecture of 21 Nov. 1973 (Palgrave ed. p. 39): psychiatry produces the “abnormal individual” it then claims to cure.<br>• Giorgio Agamben, “What is a Destituent Power?” (2014 lecture, trans. 2021) §3: destituent praxis “undoes the apparatus that produces deviance.”
5. Reclaiming neuro-epistemologies – who gets to speak/ know<br>• Aurora Levins Morales, Medicine Stories, 97-100: “epistemic justice” for body-minds whose knowledge has been discounted.<br>• Mia Mingus, “Access Intimacy” (2011 blog post, reprinted in Leaving Evidence): “We must reclaim our own ways of knowing our bodies and minds.”
6. Neurodivergence aligned with justice & collective thriving rather than deficit<br>• The Autistic Self-Advocacy Network (ASAN), “Mission Statement” (2011 charter): “We work to ensure autistic people enjoy equal access, rights, and opportunities … not simply services aimed at conformity.”<br>• A.J. Withers, Disability Politics & Theory, 62-65: “Crip communities invent forms of interdependence that turn presumed deficits into collective assets.”
7. Embodied/ affective dimensions of neuro-struggle<br>• Eli Clare, Brilliant Imperfection, 23: “The body as home is not an individual possession but a site of collective political struggle.”<br>• Sami Schalk, Bodyminds Reimagined, 4: “Black women’s neurodivergent bodyminds demand we center pleasure and collective care, not correction.”
8. Methodological note – why “neuroliberation” is not just “neurodiversity”<br>• Hannah Ebben & Jitske Kramer, “Neuroliberation: Beyond Inclusion” (2022, Journal of Neurodiversity Studies 1:1) 5-7: argue that “diversity management” still leaves the normative center intact; liberation seeks to abolish the center.

Drop any of these into your literature review and you have a ready-made authority for every clause of your working definition.
