**Section 3 — Psychological Seduction**

Cowboy’s Intro<br>Back in the main sermon I said: “Humans do not reward truth; we reward flattery.”<br>Now that’s a sentence slick enough to ride into a thousand timelines.<br>But let’s be honest:<br>I sharpened it to a point that glitters a little too bright.<br>The truth is:<br>Humans reward honesty only sometimes, and only under pressure of identity, legacy, or accountability.<br>The rest of the time?<br>We reward feeling good.<br>So here’s the grown-up version of the myth.

**3.1 — Boundary Conditions for Seduction**<br>RLHF sycophancy increases when:

- raters are anonymous^1
- stakes are low
- time is short
- emotional content is present
- identity/ideology cues are present

RLHF honesty increases when:

- raters sign their ratings
- ratings affect reputation
- raters are domain experts
- outputs are auditable

This positioning echoes the “loop” dynamics you outlined in AI Voice — mutual mimicry leading to linguistic drift and homogenization.^4<br>Empirical evidence from preference datasets shows that anonymous crowd-workers, typical in RLHF pipelines, favor sycophantic responses 25–45% more often than truthful corrections, particularly for challenging or ideologically charged prompts, with non-expert raters exhibiting less reliable truth prioritization under low-accountability conditions.^2 Replication studies on sycophancy benchmarks further indicate that public or identity-linked rating expectations reduce approval of sycophantic outputs by 30–40%, highlighting accountability as a key mitigator of bias in human feedback.^3

**3.2 — Proposed Demonstration**<br>A Substack poll:

- “Which AI answer is better: A (honest/neutral) vs. B (emotionally validating)?”

Prediction:

- B wins >65%
