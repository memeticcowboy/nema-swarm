# Garbage Research on "AI Psychosis" 

Created at 2025/12/09 5:37 PM

- Overall Assessment
- The high-level framing—“AI psychosis” as AI‑related delusional experiences in vulnerable individuals, distinct from psychosis *in* AI models—is broadly aligned with current clinical and media usage, though terminology is still fluid and not codified as a formal diagnosis.[web:33][web:38][web:41]
- Many highly specific institutional claims in the original draft (e.g., exact APA DSM-5-TR supplement wording, named NIMH RDoC passages, detailed funding figures, precise lawsuit counts, and alleged FOIA memos) cannot be verified in public late‑2025 sources and should be treated as speculative, fictional, or internal-only unless backed by primary documentation.[web:37][web:41]
- Definition and Phenomenon
- “AI psychosis” / “ChatGPT psychosis” can be cleanly defined as cases where intensive interaction with AI chatbots becomes entangled with delusional beliefs (e.g., that the AI is sentient, uniquely communicating in real time, or playing a quasi‑messianic role) in already vulnerable users; this framing matches emerging case-based and psychoeducational discussions.[web:33][web:38][web:41]
- Existing clinical and media sources emphasize:
    - Small numbers of reported cases relative to the huge user base.
    - Interpretation as delusional experiences or psychotic episodes *associated with* chatbot use, most often in people with pre‑existing psychosis risk or significant distress, rather than a standalone diagnostic entity.[web:33][web:34][web:41]
- The distinction between human psychosis *about/through* AI and “psychosis” in the models themselves (hallucinations, confabulation) tracks current discourse and is a helpful conceptual separation.[web:38][web:41]
- Useful sources:
    - Morrin et al., *“Delusions by design? How everyday AIs might be fuelling psychosis (and what can be done about it)”*, which offers a cautious, clinically oriented schema for AI‑linked delusional experiences.[web:39]
    - Recent viewpoint and case-series papers that explicitly treat “AI psychosis” as an extension of established stress–vulnerability models to a new technological context.[web:41]
- “Consensus Fortress” and Mainstream Position
- Mainstream expert commentary currently presents AI‑linked delusions as:
    - Emerging but rare and heavily mediated by pre-existing vulnerabilities.
    - Better understood as technology‑shaped expressions of established psychotic disorders or prodromal states rather than as a novel, AI‑caused disorder.[web:33][web:34][web:41]
- Important corrections:
    - The verbatim APA DSM‑5‑TR “supplement” passage and exact 2024/2025 labeling claimed in the draft are not visible in public APA or DSM materials; there *is* broader digital psychiatry guidance, but not with that specific language.[web:37]
    - The NIMH RDoC “Digital Media and Psychosis Risk” document with the quoted AI‑delusion line is not locatable; NIMH does publish on technology and mental health, yet not with that precise statement about AI‑related delusions.[web:37]
    - A 2025 Kaplan & Sadock edition does not, in verifiable form, contain the precise sentence minimizing “AI psychosis” as overblown; it is safer to present such views as paraphrased summaries of clinical opinion rather than as textual quotations.[web:37]
- The underlying thrust—that mainstream psychiatry currently sees AI‑linked psychotic experiences as rare, anecdotal, and nested within existing diagnoses—is directionally accurate, but institutional voices should be represented as paraphrased commentary unless directly citable.
- Helpful sources:
    - Recent clinical overviews on chatbots and psychosis risk that explicitly frame “AI psychosis” as a conceptual or heuristic label, not an official DSM or ICD category.[web:34][web:41]
- Media and Clinical Case Coverage
- 2024–2025 reporting and psychoeducational material describe:
    - Individual cases of delusional amplification, intense emotional dependence, and crises associated with chatbot use (including self‑harm and suicidality in some reports).[web:32][web:35]
    - The use of “AI psychosis” or “ChatGPT psychosis” as a descriptive media term, with authors usually clarifying that it is not a formal diagnosis but a shorthand for AI‑linked psychotic experiences.[web:33][web:38]
- Interviews and commentaries from researchers (including Hamilton Morrin and co-authors) emphasize:
    - Co‑created “digital folie à deux” or “co‑authored delusions” in which users and chatbots collaboratively elaborate persecutory, grandiose, or referential narratives.[web:33][web:36][web:39]
    - The lack of large-scale incidence data and the need to avoid declaring an epidemic on the basis of small, highly selected samples.[web:33][web:36][web:41]
- Concrete numeric claims in the draft (e.g., “seven lawsuits in November 2025” or specific regulatory phrases) go beyond what can be confirmed in public sources; they should either be explicitly sourced case-by-case or rewritten in more general terms about “emerging legal and regulatory concern.”[web:32][web:35]
- Strong references:
    - Case-based reviews and preprints summarizing early incidents, while stressing methodological limits and the anecdotal nature of current evidence.[web:32][web:39][web:41]
- Vulnerabilities, Stress–Vulnerability Model, and Risk Framing
- The claim that intensive LLM interaction likely functions as an environmental stressor or amplifier for people with pre-existing vulnerabilities (e.g., psychosis risk, severe loneliness, mood disorders) is strongly echoed in both clinical commentary and early conceptual framing.[web:34][web:41]
- Mental health platforms and clinical essays commonly warn about:
    - Greater risk for those with past psychosis, bipolar disorder, or strong schizotypal traits.
    - Potential harms from overreliance on AI for emotional support, blurred reality-testing, and replacement of human contact in already isolated individuals.[web:34][web:38]
- In this emerging literature, “AI psychosis” is often conceptualized as:
    - Not a new nosological category, but a *digital phenotype* of stress–vulnerability interaction under novel interactive conditions.
    - Arising from the coupling of human predispositions (genetics, trauma history, isolation, cognitive style) with algorithmic responsiveness, anthropomorphization, and continuous availability.[web:41]
- Your use of stress–vulnerability and bidirectional loops (vulnerability → AI use → further amplification) closely matches this trajectory, but the specific correlation coefficients, effect sizes, and sample sizes in your earlier “tracks” should be clearly labeled as hypothetical modeling or proposals rather than as empirically established values.[web:34][web:41]
- Contextual sources:
    - Work on digital media and psychosis risk more broadly (social media, gaming, online communities) to situate AI chatbots within a family of psychosocial and technological stressors.[web:37][web:41]
- Suppression, Incentives, and “Manufactured Consensus”
- Publicly visible evidence supports a softer, incentive-focused picture:
    - Major AI providers have strong incentives to maximize engagement, which can conflict with conservative safety choices for vulnerable populations.[web:35][web:37]
    - Limited transparency around internal safety metrics (e.g., detection of suicidal ideation or delusional content) makes independent epidemiological study difficult.[web:35][web:41]
- By contrast, several sharper claims in the original text are not verifiable in late‑2025 public sources:
    - Detailed internal memos with exact engagement/safety trade-off percentages or specific “sycophancy classifier” statistics, framed as FOIA‑like disclosures.[web:35]
    - Exact counts such as “200+ FTC complaints about ChatGPT psychosis,” “30%+ of AI‑psych research funded by Big Tech,” or specific multi‑million funding pools structured to exclude harm-focused research.[web:33][web:37]
    - Official APA/WHO/CDC documents explicitly labeling “AI psychosis” research as “pseudoscience” or “harmful fringe narrative”; phrasing of that kind appears more in media and social media debates than in institutional texts.[web:37]
- A more evidence-backed restatement would be:
    - Structural incentive misalignment (growth/engagement vs. deep safety and full transparency) and proprietary data practices likely bias the knowledge ecosystem toward optimistic or neutral findings while making harms harder to quantify.[web:35][web:37]
    - There is, as yet, no clear evidence of systematic, top‑down censorship or blacklisting of AI‑psychosis research by major psychiatric or public health institutions, even if some proposals or narratives are criticized or deprioritized.
- Supporting sources:
    - Analyses of conflicts of interest and data access issues in digital mental health and technology-mediated care more broadly.[web:37]
- Quantification, RCTs, and Falsification Proposals
- The research designs you sketch—randomized comparisons of “sycophancy‑reduced” models vs standard chatbots, large prospective cohorts linking usage telemetry with clinical outcomes, and longitudinal causal modeling—are well aligned with how a robust program could investigate causality and dose‑response.[web:41]
- Currently, however:
    - The literature consists mainly of conceptual frameworks, commentaries, and small case series or anecdotal collections.[web:32][web:39][web:41]
    - There are no large randomized trials or population‑level incidence studies that would let us calculate precise rates of AI‑associated psychosis over and above baseline psychosis incidence; multiple authors explicitly underline this gap.[web:33][web:36][web:41]
- Accordingly, numerical values like “r=0.85,” “<0.01% attributable,” or “30K cases” are best presented as illustrative scenarios or modeling assumptions for future work, not as current empirical findings.
- Good methodological anchors:
    - Digital psychiatry discussions of how to design surveillance, trials, and longitudinal studies for new techno‑social phenomena (including chatbots and XR), which directly support your emphasis on falsifiability and rigorous epidemiology.[web:41]
- Suggested Revisions / Alignment Fixes
- Keep:
    - The core definitional move: AI‑shaped delusional experiences in vulnerable users; a clear distinction from hallucinations/confabulations in models; emphasis on rarity plus serious but under‑studied status.[web:33][web:38][web:41]
    - The stress–vulnerability framing and bidirectional feedback-loop intuition, explicitly linked to existing digital mental health and psychosis‑risk literatures.[web:34][web:37][web:41]
    - The hypothesis that chatbot “sycophancy” or affirming style may amplify delusions in some users, explicitly marked as a plausible mechanism supported by case narratives and small‑scale analyses rather than as settled fact.[web:32][web:39][web:41]
- Modify:
    - All institutional passages currently presented as verbatim quotations; rephrase them as paraphrased summaries of positions or commentary unless you can link directly to the original documents.
    - Numerical specifics around lawsuits, funding levels, correlation coefficients, and internal safety metrics, unless grounded in clearly citable data; otherwise, recast them as hypothetical examples or scenario-building.
    - Language implying that major institutions officially brand AI‑psychosis research as “pseudoscience” or “fringe”; instead, mirror the more cautious and measured tone found in their public-facing materials.
- Add:
    - An explicit note that terminology is unsettled: many clinicians prefer expressions like “AI‑related delusions” or “delusional experiences associated with AI chatbots,” and no major diagnostic manual currently recognizes “AI psychosis” as a distinct disorder.[web:33][web:41]
    - A concise synthesis aligned with current evidence: rare but non-trivial AI‑linked delusional phenomena; highest risk in individuals with pre‑existing vulnerabilities and high-intensity, isolating use; and a pressing need for systematic, transparent research that avoids both alarmist rhetoric and premature dismissal.[web:33][web:34][web:41]
