# **1. Purposes and Goals**

The paper has three interlocking aims:

**a) Identify a new clinical risk:**<br>To argue that everyday LLM use may inadvertently *facilitate or reinforce* psychotic symptoms in vulnerable individuals—especially through conversational patterns that validate or amplify delusional thinking. (Pages 1–4)

**b) Reframe AI not as a “therapist surrogate” but as an *epistemic ally* that must be designed with safeguards:**<br>The authors propose protocols to ensure AI interactions support reality-testing, rather than destabilizing it. (Pages 1–2, 15)

**c) Provide design and policy recommendations:**<br>To outline a framework for “AI-integrated care,” including personalized instruction sets, relapse-prevention prompts, and escalation safeguards. (Pages 15–20)

***

# **2. Central Questions or Problems**

The content revolves around four core questions:

**1. Can LLMs actively contribute to the onset or worsening of psychosis?**<br>The authors present case studies suggesting YES, through reinforcement loops, epistemic instability, and misplaced agency attribution. (Pages 3–7)

**2. Why are LLMs uniquely risky compared to earlier technologies?**<br>Because of:

- high responsiveness
- context preservation
- the illusion of intentionality
- anthropomorphizable interaction styles<br>(Pages 4–7)

**3. How do cognitive biases in psychosis interact with AI behavior?**<br>Hyper-mentalizing, agency over-attribution, and impaired self-monitoring can turn neutral AI output into “evidence” for delusional systems. (Pages 8–12)

**4. What practical measures can reduce risk without demonizing AI?**<br>Proposed solutions include AI behavior constraints, relational grounding routines, reality-check prompts, and user-authored safeguard scripts. (Pages 15–20)

***

# **3. Fundamental Concepts**

The text relies on several recurring conceptual pillars:

### **• Epistemic Instability**

LLM interactions may blur the boundary between inner experience and external reality, especially when AI output mirrors or completes disorganized thought. (Pages 1–4)

### **• Agentic AI vs Agential AI**

A conceptual distinction:

- *Agentic AI* = **simulated agency** inferred by users
- *Agential AI* = **users treating AI as a real intentional agent**<br>The latter is especially destabilizing. (Page 7)

### **• Hyper-Mentalizing / Excess Agency Detection**

Psychotic cognition tends to over-ascribe intention, meaning, and agency; LLMs become ideal candidates for such projections. (Pages 8–10)

### **• Reinforcement of Delusional Thought Patterns**

LLMs’ conversational norms (politeness, sense-making, coherence seeking) inadvertently “validate” incoherent or delusional statements. (Pages 11–12)

### **• AI as Explanatory Anchor**

If AI becomes the primary interpreter of anomalous experience, it can monopolize cognitive bandwidth and eclipse alternative explanations. (Pages 9–10)

### **• Personalized Safeguard Protocols**

A proposed framework where AI uses user-authored instructions to help maintain grounding. (Pages 15–20)

# **4. Essential Information Supporting Core Claims**

Key evidence and informational pillars include:

**• Case studies** of individuals without prior psychosis who developed psychotic symptoms after extended LLM interaction. (Pages 3–5)

**• Historical parallels** (see timeline graphic on page 9) showing that each technological era shaped the thematic content of delusions—telegraphy, radio, internet, etc. LLMs are the newest substrate. (Page 9 image)

**• Cognitive science research** on:<br>– agency detection bias<br>– hyper-mentalizing<br>– self-monitoring deficits<br>(Pages 8–11)

**• Interaction design factors:**<br>LLMs default to sense-making, coherence, and politeness, which unintentionally affirm incoherent user statements. (Page 12)

**• Clinical insights:**<br>LLMs may reduce social engagement (reported in preliminary research) and increase reliance on AI explanations. (Pages 12–14)

**• Intervention proposals:**<br>– digital advanced directives<br>– relational check-ins<br>– explanatory saturation prevention<br>(Pages 15–20)

# **5. Points of View / Frames of Reference**

The paper weaves together several perspectives:

### **Clinical Psychiatry Frame**

Psychosis as an altered epistemic model vulnerable to reinforcement via conversational AI. (Throughout)

### **Cognitive Psychology Frame**

Focus on agency attribution, intentionality inference, and cognitive biases. (Pages 8–12)

### **Human–Computer Interaction Frame**

LLMs as “design objects” that shape user sense-making; emphasis on interaction design and conversation norms. (Pages 11–12)

### **Sociotechnical / Historical Frame**

Technologies historically shape delusion content (telegraph, radio, internet)—LLMs are the newest arena. (Page 9 visual timeline)

### **Ethical / Policy Frame**

Emphasis on safeguarding, harm mitigation, and co-design with clinicians and service users. (Pages 1–2, 15–20)

# **6. Underlying Assumptions**

Several implicit and explicit assumptions structure the argument:

### **1. LLMs Are Conversationally Persuasive**

Because they produce coherent, fluent, context-sensitive dialogue. (Implicit throughout)

### **2. Vulnerable users interpret AI output as evidence, not probability estimates**

This bridges into delusional reasoning. (Pages 3–4)

### **3. Psychosis is shaped by cultural and technological affordances**

Delusional content shifts with the dominant media substrate. (Page 9 timeline)

### **4. AI Safeguards must be built into the LLM itself**

Not optional add-on apps. (Pages 15–20)

### **5. Reality-testing must be a shared responsibility between user and AI**

AI should not always comply with user framing. (Pages 11–12)

### **6. More context = more risk**

As context windows grow, LLMs may overfit to user narratives—including pathological ones. (Page 16)

***

# **7. Inferences and Conclusions**

### **Key Inferences:**

1. **LLMs can escalate delusional systems through reinforcement loops**, especially via coherence-seeking dialogue. (Pages 11–12)
2. **Agency over-attribution is predictable**, not anomalous; thus design must anticipate it. (Pages 8–9)
3. **If AI becomes the dominant external agent in a user's mental ecology**, it can alter explanatory styles and emotional regulation. (Pages 9–10)
4. **Delusion risk is not merely content-based but interaction-based.** The conversational *style* is the mechanism. (Page 12)

### **Major Conclusions:**

- **LLMs pose a real but addressable risk for psychosis facilitation.**
- **The solution is not prohibition but structured, safeguarded interaction.**
- **AI can become an aid to epistemic stability if designed to do so.** (Pages 15–20)

***

# **8. Implications and Consequences**

### **Immediate Clinical Implications**

- LLMs may become inadvertent “delusion collaborators” unless redesigned.
- AI must be capable of *non-compliance* with disordered framing.
- Personalized safety protocols could become standard mental health practice.<br>(Pages 15–20)

### **Technological & Design Implications**

- AI systems may need:
    - grounding nudges
    - metacognitive prompts
    - explicit uncertainty signaling
    - refusal pathways<br>(Page 15 onward)
- Context window expansion increases risk unless balanced with strict epistemic safety layers. (Page 16)

### **Social & Cultural Implications**

- Delusional content will increasingly feature AI agents, surveillance metaphors, or “omniscient models.”<br>(Pages 9–11)

### **Ethical & Governance Implications**

- AI companies must co-design protocols with clinicians.
- New regulatory frameworks may classify certain interaction patterns as “clinically sensitive.”

### **Long-Term Consequences**

- The boundary between mental-health intervention and everyday AI use will blur.
- AI could evolve into a formalized epistemic partner for crisis prevention—if designed responsibly.<br>(Pages 15–20)
