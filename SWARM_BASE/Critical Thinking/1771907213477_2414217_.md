Deductive logic is the branch of logic that studies **arguments whose conclusions are guaranteed by their premises**.<br>If the premises are true and the argument is **valid**, the conclusion **must** be true; there is no possibility of the premises being true while the conclusion is false.

1. Canonical form<br>• **Premise 1**: All A are B.<br>• **Premise 2**: x is an A.<br>• **Conclusion**: Therefore, x is B.<br>Example:<br>• All planets orbit stars.<br>• Earth is a planet.<br>• Therefore, Earth orbits a star.
2. Key concepts<br>• **Validity**: An argument is valid when the conclusion follows **necessarily** from the premises.<br>• **Soundness**: An argument is sound when it is valid **and** all of its premises are actually true.<br>• **Formal systems**: Propositional logic, predicate (first-order) logic, and higher-order logics provide precise rules (e.g., modus ponens, universal instantiation) for deriving conclusions.
3. Contrast with other reasoning<br>• **Induction**: Extends observed patterns to new cases, yielding only **probable** conclusions.<br>• **Retroduction/Abduction**: Conjectures the **best explanation** for a given observation, yielding plausible but fallible hypotheses.

In short, deductive logic is the “logic of certainty”: when its rules are followed, the truth of the premises **transfers inexorably** to the truth of the conclusion.
