# Annaka Harris on the Nature of Consciousness

Created at 2024/11/29 12:02 PM

In her Big Think interview, Annaka Harris, author of Conscious: A Brief Guide to the Fundamental Mystery of the Mind, explores the enigmatic nature of consciousness. She questions the traditional view that consciousness arises solely from complex brain processes, suggesting instead that it might be a fundamental aspect of nature, akin to gravity. Harris discusses how modern neuroscience challenges our intuitions about consciousness and considers the possibility that all systems in nature could possess some form of felt experience. She emphasizes the importance of reevaluating our assumptions to advance the scientific study of consciousness.

**🧠 Fundamental Nature of Consciousness**
- **Consciousness as a Basic Feature**: Annaka Harris proposes that consciousness may not be exclusive to complex organisms but could be a fundamental aspect of nature, similar to gravity. This perspective challenges the traditional view that consciousness arises solely from intricate brain processes.
- **Felt Experience Beyond Complexity**: Harris emphasizes that consciousness can exist in simple organisms without complex thought, suggesting that even infants and non-human entities possess a form of awareness and felt experience.
- **Reevaluating Consciousness Definitions**: The definition of consciousness is broadened to include awareness and sentience, moving away from the notion that it is tied only to higher-order thinking or complex cognitive functions.

**🔍 Challenging Intuitions**
- **Intuitions About Consciousness Are Misleading**: Modern neuroscience has revealed that many intuitions regarding consciousness, such as its direct correlation with behavior, are incorrect. For instance, individuals with locked-in syndrome can have full conscious experiences without any observable behavior.
- **Behavior Does Not Equate to Consciousness**: The example of Jean-Dominique Bauby illustrates that conscious experience can exist independently of physical movement or communication, prompting a reevaluation of how we identify consciousness in other systems.
- **Consciousness and Fear Response**: Harris discusses how the experience of fear is a result of complex brain processing that occurs after the body has already reacted to a threat, indicating that consciousness may not drive behavior as intuitively believed.

**🔬 Scientific Inquiry**
- **Need for New Scientific Approaches**: To advance the understanding of consciousness, Harris argues for a reevaluation of existing assumptions and the exploration of consciousness in various systems beyond human-like complexity.
- **Exploring Shared Experiences**: Harris raises questions about the potential for shared experiences between different systems, suggesting that future scientific inquiry could uncover new dimensions of consciousness across various entities.
- **Ethical Considerations of Consciousness**: Understanding which systems may experience suffering is crucial for ethical considerations. Harris highlights the importance of recognizing consciousness in non-human entities, such as animals and potentially plants.

**🌌 Neuroscience's Role in Consciousness**

- **Overturning Assumptions**: Recent findings in neuroscience challenge the assumption that consciousness is exclusive to complex systems, suggesting that simpler systems may also possess forms of consciousness.
- **Complexity vs. Fundamental Consciousness**: Harris presents two possibilities: either consciousness arises from complex processing or it is a fundamental aspect of all systems, prompting a need for further investigation into the nature of consciousness.
