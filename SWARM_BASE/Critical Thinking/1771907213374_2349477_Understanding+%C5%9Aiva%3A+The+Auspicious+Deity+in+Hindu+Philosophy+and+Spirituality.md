The Sanskrit term **Śiva** (शिव) is a single word with profound significance in Indian philosophy, mythology, and spiritual traditions. Below is the definition and the term written in Sanskrit.

### Definition:

**Śiva** (शिव):

- **Śiva** means "auspicious," "benevolent," or "kind" in Sanskrit, derived from the root **śī** (to be gracious or auspicious). It is the name of one of the principal deities in Hinduism, often referred to as the "Destroyer" within the Trimurti (alongside Brahma the Creator and Vishnu the Preserver). However, Śiva’s role transcends mere destruction, encompassing transformation, regeneration, and dissolution of the universe to facilitate renewal.
- **Mythological Context**: Śiva is worshipped as the supreme deity in Shaivism, one of the major sects of Hinduism. He is depicted in various forms, such as the meditative ascetic, Nataraja (Lord of Dance), or the family deity with Parvati, Ganesha, and Kartikeya. Śiva is associated with attributes like asceticism, compassion, destruction of ignorance, and cosmic balance.
- **Philosophical Context**: In non-dualistic traditions like Advaita Vedanta and Kashmir Shaivism, Śiva represents pure consciousness, the ultimate reality (*Brahman*), or the unchanging essence of existence. In Kashmir Shaivism, Śiva is the dynamic consciousness that manifests the universe through his creative power (*Shakti*) and is inseparable from it.
- **Yogic and Tantric Context**: Śiva is often associated with the inner self (*Ātman*) and the awakening of spiritual energy (*kundalini*) in yogic practices. He is also linked to meditation, as the ultimate yogi, and to practices involving mantras, such as *Om Namah Shivaya*.
- **Symbolic Context**: Śiva embodies paradoxes—both ascetic and householder, destroyer and creator, fierce (*Rudra*) and benevolent. His symbols, like the *linga*, trident, and third eye, represent his transcendent and transformative nature.
