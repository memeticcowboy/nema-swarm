# Exploring Diverse Intelligences and Collective Decision-Making in Biological Systems

Created at 2025/06/20 6:33 AM

[Study Notes: “Against Mind-Blindness” by Michael Levin](https://youtu.be/rsODvjLDzww?si=_QzsprWD8MkYFPw2)

Overview

	•	Lecture Structure:

	1.	Conceptual background on diverse intelligences.

	2.	Detailed analysis of collective decision-making in cells.

	3.	Future implications and applications in regenerative medicine.

Key Concepts

	•	Diverse Intelligence:

	•	The need to unify various forms of intelligence across different mediums, scales, and contexts.

	•	Collective Intelligence:

	•	Decision-making processes of groups of cells.

	•	Cells operate in an “anatomical morphus space.”

Definitions

	•	Intelligence (William James’ Definition):

	•	The ability to reach the same goal by different means.

	•	Morphagenesis:

	•	The biological process that causes an organism to develop its shape.

	•	Anatomical Morphus Space:

	•	The high-dimensional space in which cells navigate to achieve physiological goals.

Main Ideas

	•	Unification of Intelligence:

	•	Early scientific theories divided phenomena (e.g., electromagnetism). Unifying theories allow for technological advancements.

	•	Apply a similar unifying approach to cognition by recognizing diverse intelligences.

	•	Philosophical Framework:

	•	Cognitive claims about systems are protocol claims; they indicate how we plan to interact with these systems.

	•	This leads to empirical questions rather than abstract categorizations.

	•	Biological Intelligence:

	•	Intelligence predates brains and neurons, demonstrated through the competency of cells in problem-solving.

	•	Collective intelligence in cells can be recognized through their ability to solve anatomical problems.

Examples

	•	Anatomical Homeostasis in Axolotls:

	•	Axolotls can regenerate limbs and know when to stop regenerating (error minimization).

	•	Metamorphosis in Tadpoles:

	•	Tadpoles can rearrange their body structures during transformation into frogs, demonstrating flexibility and problem-solving.

	•	Bioelectrical Communication:

	•	The use of bioelectrical signals to instruct cells on developmental patterns. For example, inducing eye formation in unexpected body areas by injecting RNA.

	•	Zenobots:

	•	Frog skin cells form a self-organizing construct capable of swimming and self-replicating using environmental materials.

	•	Anthrobots:

	•	Human cells that form self-motile constructs, capable of healing neural wounds and demonstrating emergent properties.

Important Terms

	•	Protocol Claims: Assertions about how to interact with cognitive systems.

	•	Hierarchical Non-local Control: The idea that intelligence involves global information affecting the entire system rather than local interactions.

	•	Hackability: The flexibility of biological systems to be influenced by external inputs or alterations.

Complex Concepts Simplified

	•	Collective Intelligence in Cells: Cells work together to make decisions without a central brain, similar to how a community operates.

	•	Learning Beyond the Nervous System: Learning capabilities exist in molecular networks, not just in brains, allowing cells to adapt and respond to new challenges.

Summary of Findings

	•	Biological systems exhibit intelligence and problem-solving capabilities that challenge traditional views of cognition.

	•	Regenerative medicine can benefit from understanding and communicating with various biological intelligences.

	•	The future of biology includes a spectrum of life forms, including artificial and biological hybrids.

Conclusion

	•	The lecture emphasizes the significance of recognizing and relating to diverse forms of intelligence, urging a philosophical and empirical approach to understanding cognition in both biological and synthetic systems
