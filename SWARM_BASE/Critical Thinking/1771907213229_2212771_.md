# 6. ⚡ Critical Algorithm Studies / Data Feminism / Decolonial Tech Ethics

Created at 2025/06/12 11:32 AM

### Key Points

- Research suggests technology reflects power imbalances, with Critical Algorithm Studies, Data Feminism, and Decolonial Tech Ethics highlighting biases in AI.
- It seems likely that AI systems embed values of dominant groups, like Western, white, male developers, potentially marginalizing others.
- The evidence leans toward historical biases being reproduced in AI, with controversy over how to address these issues.
- It’s debated who defines the "post-human," with concerns that marginalized groups, like people of color and those in the Global South, might be left behind.

#### Understanding the Fields

These fields—Critical Algorithm Studies, Data Feminism, and Decolonial Tech Ethics—look at how technology can create unfair advantages and disadvantages. They focus on uncovering biases in AI and ensuring technology serves everyone, not just the powerful.

- **Critical Algorithm Studies** shows how algorithms, like those in search engines, can reinforce racism, as seen in Safiya Noble’s work on biased search results.
- **Data Feminism**, led by Catherine D’Ignazio, pushes for data practices that challenge gender and racial inequalities, ensuring AI is fair and inclusive.
- **Decolonial Tech Ethics** aims to address colonial legacies in tech, ensuring it benefits marginalized communities, drawing from Sandra Harding’s ideas on inclusive science.

#### Whose Values Are in AI?

AI often reflects the values of its creators, typically from dominant groups. This can mean AI systems don’t work well for everyone, like facial recognition being less accurate for people of color, showing a need for more diverse input in design.

#### Historical Biases and AI

AI can carry forward past inequalities, like biased hiring data leading to unfair job recommendations. Ruha Benjamin’s "New Jim Code" concept highlights how technology can deepen racial divides, sparking debates on how to fix these biases.

#### Defining the "Post-Human"

The "post-human" might mean humans enhanced by tech, but there’s controversy over who gets to decide what that looks like. If only the wealthy can afford enhancements, it could leave marginalized groups, like those in the Global South, behind, raising ethical questions.
