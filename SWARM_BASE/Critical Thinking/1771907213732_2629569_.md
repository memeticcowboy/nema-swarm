Let me offer some frameworks that might illuminate what you’re sensing:<br><br>**The Interrogation Spectrum**<br>Paulo Freire distinguished between “banking” questions (depositing expected answers) and “problem-posing” questions (opening genuine inquiry). But there’s more texture here:<br>	∙	Extractive questioning: Treats the other as data source (journalism, interrogation, diagnosis)<br>	∙	Performative questioning: The questioner already knows; they’re testing or teaching<br>	∙	Catalytic questioning: Genuine not-knowing that invites co-discovery<br>	∙	Devotional questioning: Questions asked in service of the other’s unfolding<br>The aggression you’re sensing often comes when someone experiences extractive questioning but the asker claims catalytic intent. The mismatch creates a kind of epistemic violation.<br><br>**Martin Buber’s I-Thou vs I-It**<br>Questions can relate to someone as:<br>	∙	I-It: Object to be known, categorized, understood<br>	∙	I-Thou: Mystery to be encountered, where the question itself is an offering<br>Most “professional” questioning is necessarily I-It. But when done in contexts that demand I-Thou presence (intimacy, trauma, spiritual direction), it becomes violence.<br><br>**The Power Topology of Questions**<br>Questions always encode power relations:<br>	∙	Who has the right to ask?<br>	∙	Who must answer?<br>	∙	What counts as a legitimate question?<br>	∙	Whose curiosity is indulged vs whose is “inappropriate”?
