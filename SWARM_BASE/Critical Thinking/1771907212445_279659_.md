The article "The Five Stages Of AI Grief" by Benjamin Bratton explores the human reaction to the development of Artificial Intelligence (AI) through the lens of the five stages of grief, as identified by Elizabeth Kübler-Ross. Bratton argues that the reaction to AI is a form of "Copernican Trauma," a decentering of humanity's self-image, and that the various stages of grief are responses to this trauma.

The five stages of AI grief are:

1. **Denial**: AI is not real, not important, or just hype. This stage is characterized by a refusal to accept the reality of AI and its implications.

2. **Anger**: AI is a threat to humanity, freedom, and autonomy, and must be resisted. This stage is fueled by fear, both warranted and unwarranted.

3. **Bargaining**: The attempt to negotiate with AI, to make it more "human-centered" or to ensure that it aligns with human values.

4. **Depression**: The feeling of hopelessness and despair in the face of AI's potential consequences, such as job displacement or existential risk.

5. **Acceptance**: The recognition that AI is a reality that must be understood and worked with, rather than fought against.

Bratton argues that these stages are not mutually exclusive, and that individuals may oscillate between them. He also notes that these responses are often inadequate in addressing the complex challenges posed by AI, and that a more nuanced and multifaceted approach is needed.

The article concludes by suggesting that the conversations about AI are often "puerile" and that a more mature and informed discussion is necessary to address the implications of AI.
