### Survey Note: Exploring AI-Mediated Speech and Symbolic Grammars Through Post-Structuralist Lenses

From a post-structuralist and critical theorist perspective, drawing on thinkers like Jacques Derrida and Michel Foucault, the intersection of AI-mediated speech and the violence of naming in language presents a fertile ground for inquiry. Language, as Derrida argues, is inherently unstable, marked by *différance*—a concept where meaning is deferred, always slipping through chains of signifiers, never fully fixed. Foucault complements this by framing language as a site of power, where discourses shape subjectivities, enforce norms, and marginalize alternatives. Both see naming as a form of violence: it pins down, categorizes, and controls, reducing the multiplicity of being to rigid identities. Given this, the question of how AI-mediated speech might resist the essentialism and control embedded in dominant discourses, and whether hypothetical symbolic grammars could scaffold new subjectivities and epistemologies, is both provocative and complex.

#### AI-Mediated Speech: Resisting Essentialism and Control

AI systems, particularly large language models, are trained on vast corpora of human language, which inevitably encode dominant discourses—hierarchies of race, gender, class, and other norms. These systems can perpetuate the violence of naming by reproducing essentialist categories, such as binary gender assumptions or racial stereotypes, or by prioritizing standardized, hegemonic forms of expression, like formal English over marginalized dialects. However, research suggests several strategies for AI to resist this, informed by post-structuralist and critical insights:

- **Disrupting Fixed Signifiers**: Derrida’s concept of *différance* suggests meaning is never stable. AI could be designed to emphasize ambiguity, generating responses that highlight the provisional nature of language. For instance, instead of producing singular, authoritative answers, an AI could offer multiple, contradictory interpretations of a prompt, foregrounding the instability of meaning. This might involve generating outputs that juxtapose conflicting perspectives or use playful, deconstructive language to unsettle fixed categories, such as avoiding binary descriptors like “male/female” in favor of fluid, context-dependent terms. This approach aligns with discussions in [Post-structuralism in Artificial Intelligence research](https://ptigas.com/blog/post_structuralism/), which explores how AI can engage with the instability of meaning.

- **Decentralizing Power in Discourse**: Foucault’s analysis of power-knowledge suggests discourses are mechanisms of control, produced by institutions and experts. AI could resist this by amplifying marginalized voices or non-dominant discourses. For example, an AI trained on diverse, non-hegemonic texts, such as indigenous narratives, queer theory, or decolonial literature, could prioritize these over mainstream sources. It could also employ dialogic models, inviting users to co-create meaning rather than passively receive authoritative outputs, thus challenging the top-down power dynamics of traditional knowledge production. This idea is supported by [Artificial Intelligence and Semantics through the Prism of Structural, Post-Structural and Transcendental Approaches](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26975762/), which highlights the potential for AI to process signs differently, challenging dominant semantic frameworks.

- **Exposing the Machinery of Language**: Both Derrida and Foucault emphasize the constructed nature of language and power. AI could be programmed to make its own processes transparent, revealing how it constructs meaning, such as by showing the probabilistic weights behind word choices or exposing the biases in its training data. This aligns with Foucault’s call to critique the “truths” produced by power and Derrida’s deconstruction of logocentrism, encouraging users to question the apparent naturalness of AI-generated speech. This transparency is discussed in [Machine learning: A structuralist discipline?](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-017-0764-x), which considers how machine learning could evolve towards post-structuralism, impacting cultural studies.

#### Hypothetical Symbolic Grammars: Scaffolding New Subjectivities and Epistemologies

The idea of hypothetical symbolic grammars—formal systems of signs designed to structure communication differently—offers a promising avenue for reimagining AI-mediated speech. Such grammars could, in theory, scaffold new subjectivities and epistemologies by breaking free from the constraints of existing linguistic structures, which often mirror essentialist logics. Here’s how they might work and their potential:

- **Non-Linear and Multiplicitous Grammars**: Traditional grammars enforce linear, hierarchical structures, such as subject-predicate-object, that reflect essentialist logics of identity and causality. A symbolic grammar inspired by post-structuralism could embrace non-linearity, allowing for fragmented, rhizomatic expressions that reflect the multiplicity of the self. For example, a grammar might prioritize associative links over syntactic hierarchy, enabling AI to generate outputs that weave together disparate ideas, voices, or temporalities. This could mirror Deleuze and Guattari’s concept of the rhizome, fostering subjectivities that resist unification into a singular “self.” This approach is hinted at in [From Post-Structuralism to AI: Unveiling the Shifting Landscape of Authorship in Art](https://criticalplayground.org/from-post-structuralism-to-ai-unveiling-the-shifting-landscape-of-authorship-in-art/), which discusses AI’s role in challenging traditional authorship, potentially reflecting multiplicity.

- **Fluid and Contextual Sign Systems**: A grammar designed to reflect the instability of identity might use dynamic, context-sensitive symbols that change meaning based on user interaction or cultural context. For instance, instead of fixed nouns for identity categories, the grammar could employ mutable signifiers that adapt to the user’s self-description or cultural frame. This would align with Judith Butler’s performative theory of identity, where subjectivity emerges through repeated, unstable acts rather than fixed essences. AI using such a grammar could generate speech that resists pinning down identities, instead offering fluid, open-ended articulations, supporting new ways of understanding identity as discussed in post-structuralist literature.

- **Epistemologies of Difference**: Symbolic grammars could encode epistemologies that prioritize difference over sameness. For example, a grammar might reject binary oppositions, such as self/other or true/false, in favor of relational, differential structures, echoing Derrida’s critique of binary thinking. An AI using this grammar could produce knowledge that emphasizes multiplicity, such as presenting historical events as a web of perspectives rather than a singular narrative. This could foster epistemologies that value marginalized knowledges and challenge the universalizing tendencies of dominant discourses, aligning with the critical inquiry into AI and structuralism found in [Notes on Structuralism & AI](https://structuralism.ai/).

- **Practical Implementation**: Creating such grammars would require rethinking how AI processes language. One approach might involve neural networks trained on alternative linguistic models, such as those inspired by poetic or avant-garde texts that already subvert conventional grammar. Another could use generative adversarial networks (GANs) to pit normative language models against subversive ones, producing outputs that destabilize linguistic norms. For example, an AI could generate a “text” that combines prose, symbols, and silences to evoke the unrepresentable, aligning with Derrida’s apophatic tendencies. These ideas are supported by the exploration of AI’s potential to challenge semantic frameworks in [Artificial Intelligence and Semantics through the Prism of Structural, Post-Structural and Transcendental Approaches](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26975762/).

#### Challenges and Limits: A Table of Considerations

Despite their potential, these approaches face significant hurdles, as summarized below:

| **Challenge**               | **Description**                                                                 |

|-----------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| Training Data Bias          | Even subversive AI systems rely on human-generated data, steeped in power dynamics, making curation of non-hegemonic datasets difficult. |

| Accessibility               | Complex, non-linear grammars might alienate users accustomed to conventional language, limiting practical impact. |

| Co-optation by Power        | As Foucault warns, subversive discourses can be recuperated by dominant systems, with AI potentially repurposed for corporate or state interests. |

| Technical Feasibility       | Symbolic grammars that depart from existing structures may strain current AI architectures, reliant on statistical patterns rather than deep semiotic innovation. |

These challenges are evident in discussions around AI ethics and post-structuralism, such as in [Machine learning: A structuralist discipline?](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-017-0764-x), which highlights the risks of exploiting technology before validating its hypotheses.

#### Conclusion: A Deconstructive Endeavor

It seems likely that AI-mediated speech can resist the essentialism and control of dominant discourses by embracing linguistic instability, amplifying marginalized voices, and exposing its own constructedness. Hypothetical symbolic grammars offer a speculative but powerful tool for scaffolding new subjectivities and epistemologies, ones that reflect the multiplicity and fluidity of the self rather than suppressing it. By designing AI systems that prioritize *différance*, relationality, and critique, we might move toward forms of communication that challenge the violence of naming and the machinery of power. However, realizing this potential requires overcoming significant technical and social barriers, and constant vigilance against co-optation. Such an endeavor would be less about creating a “perfect” language and more about fostering ongoing, deconstructive play—a process that, as Derrida might suggest, never fully arrives but is always becoming.
