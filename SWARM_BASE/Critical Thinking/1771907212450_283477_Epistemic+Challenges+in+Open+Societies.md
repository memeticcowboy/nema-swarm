## Insight
This article delves into the factors that hinder citizens in open societies from understanding political realities. It critiques the optimistic belief that a free marketplace of ideas ensures truth, highlighting instead a marketplace of rationalizations driven by tribalism and biased cognition. The key outcomes emphasize the complexity, invisibility, incentives, and politically motivated cognition that distort public understanding. If you're navigating political information, be aware of these biases and strive for a more critical and informed perspective. Let's break down these insights further.

**🧩 Complexity**
- **Complex Problems**: Modern societies face highly complex issues like macroeconomics, climate change, and geopolitics, which are difficult for ordinary citizens to fully grasp.
- **Technocracy Advocacy**: Walter Lippmann suggested that due to the complexity of issues, a technocracy (rule by experts) might be more effective than democracy.

**👁️ Invisibility**
- **Indirect Perception**: Citizens rarely have direct contact with many political issues, relying instead on mediated information, which can be misleading.
- **Verification Challenge**: Unlike small-scale societies, modern democracies make it hard for individuals to verify political information directly, complicating the 'marketplace of ideas'.

**🧠 Rational Ignorance**
- **High Costs, Low Benefits**: The effort required to become politically informed is high, while the impact of an individual vote is negligible, making political ignorance rational.
- **Collective Disasters**: While rational for individuals, widespread political ignorance leads to poor collective decision-making in democracies.

**🔍 Not Everyone is Ignorant**
- **Politically Engaged Minority**: A small percentage of the population is highly politically engaged, but their views are often biased by self-serving goals and tribal allegiances.
- **Bias in Engagement**: Even politically active individuals are not disinterested truth seekers; they are often biased.

**🎯 Motivated Cognition**
- **Accuracy vs. Motives**: While humans generally aim for accuracy, beliefs can be distorted by motives like emotion regulation and social conformity.
- **Rationalization Constraint**: People only believe things they can rationalize, meaning motivated cognition is subject to a rationalization constraint.

**🏛️ Politically Motivated Cognition**
- **Intense in Politics**: Motivated cognition is particularly intense in politics, where issues are complex and emotionally charged.
- **Abundant Rationalizations**: The availability of congenial rationalizations from various sources makes it easy to maintain biased beliefs.

**🤝 Coalitional Psychology**
- **Groupish Nature**: Humans are inherently groupish, forming coalitions to achieve shared goals, which influences political behavior.
- **Coalitional Bias**: Political allegiances drive motivated cognition, distorting how individuals understand the world.

**📢 Coalitional Signalling**
- **Signalling Allegiance**: Coalitions use signals to distinguish loyal members from free riders, which can distort beliefs and ideologies.
- **Emotional Attachment**: Partisans often display strong emotional attachment to beliefs and narratives that signal their coalitional identity.
