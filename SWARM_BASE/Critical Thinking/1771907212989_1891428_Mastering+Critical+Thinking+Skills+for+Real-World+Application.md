***

### **1. Purposes and Goals**

**Primary Goal:**<br>To develop and improve the reader’s **critical thinking skills** by teaching them explicitly and directly. These skills are intended to be **transferable across subjects and real-world contexts**, improving decision-making, reasoning, and evaluative judgment.

***

### **2. Central Questions or Problems**

- *How can individuals learn to think more reflectively, reasonably, and effectively?*
- *Why is traditional content-focused education insufficient for teaching thinking skills?*
- *What methods best support the teaching and practice of critical thinking?*

***

### **3. Fundamental Concepts**

- **Critical thinking** is *active*, *persistent*, and *careful* reasoning focused on beliefs, evidence, assumptions, and conclusions.
- **Metacognition** (thinking about thinking) is key to skill development.
- Skills must be **practiced consciously** using structured models.
- **‘Critico-creative thinking’** highlights that critical thinking requires both **evaluation** and **imaginative generation of alternatives**.

***

### **4. Essential Information**

- Classic definitions (Dewey, Glaser, Ennis, Paul, Scriven) emphasize:
    - Reason-based evaluation.
    - Use of evidence and logic.
    - The importance of decision-making.
    - Interpretation and judgment across media.
- Key skills include:
    - Identifying assumptions, reasons, and conclusions.
    - Evaluating sources and arguments.
    - Drawing valid inferences.
    - Making reasoned decisions.
- Teaching critical thinking is like teaching a sport: **model, practice, apply**.

***

### **5. Points of View or Frames of Reference**

- **Educational Psychology:** A shift from content-heavy to skills-based learning.
- **Philosophy of Education:** Drawing from traditions like Socratic inquiry and Dewey's reflective thinking.
- **Media Literacy & Epistemology:** Considering how information is framed, interpreted, and trusted.
- **Practical Reasoning:** How individuals apply thinking skills in everyday decisions and judgments.

***

### **6. Underlying Assumptions**

- People do not naturally acquire critical thinking through passive learning.
- Thinking skills can be **explicitly taught and practiced**.
- There are **better and worse ways** of thinking; improvement is possible.
- Critical thinking is **not inherently negative or confrontational**, but balanced with creativity.
- A willingness to use these skills matters just as much as possessing them.

***

### **7. Inferences and Conclusions**

- Critical thinking is not an innate ability but a **trainable competency**.
- A structured, reflective approach to thinking leads to **better decision-making and problem-solving**.
- **Direct instruction** in reasoning, evaluating, and argumentation improves intellectual autonomy.
- Critical thinkers are more likely to recognize bias, question authority, and reason effectively across domains.

***

### **8. Implications and Consequences**

- **Educational systems** should prioritize critical thinking explicitly in curricula.
- **Learners** become more empowered, self-directed, and resilient in a complex information landscape.
- **Societies** benefit from citizens who can evaluate evidence, engage in informed debate, and resist manipulation.
- Failure to develop critical thinking perpetuates **gullibility, poor decision-making, and intellectual dependence**.

***
