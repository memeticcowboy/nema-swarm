# Cognitive Styles: The Tension Between Heuristics and Integrative Complexity

**Kimi:**<br>Your question—"Is this a matter of struggling to accept heuristics?"—is a sharp one, and it gets at a deeper tension in how people process complex information.<br>Integrative complexity is essentially the opposite of heuristic thinking. Heuristics are mental shortcuts—fast, frugal, and often biased. Integrative complexity, by contrast, is slow, effortful, and demands the suspension of immediate judgment in favor of holding multiple, even contradictory, perspectives in mind at once. So when someone struggles to accept heuristics, it might not be that they’re failing to use them—it might be that they’re resisting them, either consciously or unconsciously, in favor of a more integrative, nuanced mode of thought.<br>But here’s the twist: this resistance itself can become a kind of heuristic—a meta-heuristic, if you will. For example, someone might adopt a rule like “always question binary choices” or “never accept the first explanation.” These are still shortcuts, just at a higher level of abstraction. So the struggle isn’t necessarily about rejecting heuristics per se, but about which level of cognitive economy one is willing or able to operate at.<br>In psychometric terms, people high in integrative complexity are not just more thoughtful—they’re more tolerant of epistemic uncertainty. That tolerance is cognitively expensive. So yes, in some cases, what looks like a refusal to “just pick a side” or “keep it simple” might actually be a failure to accept the cognitive savings that heuristics offer. But it might also be a principled stand against oversimplification—especially in high-stakes contexts like international relations, where the 1988 and 1995 studies found that drops in integrative complexity preceded violent conflict.<br>So, short answer:<br>Yes, struggling to accept heuristics can be part of it—but not because heuristics are inherently better.
