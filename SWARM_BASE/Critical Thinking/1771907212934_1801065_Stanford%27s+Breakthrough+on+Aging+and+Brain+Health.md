## Summary
Aging depletes the brain's protective sugar shield, the glycocalyx, weakening the blood-brain barrier and fueling cognitive decline and neurodegenerative diseases. Stanford researchers found that restoring key sugars can improve the barrier's integrity, reduce neuroinflammation, and enhance cognitive function in aged mice.

**🧠 Reviving the Brain: The Role of Glycocalyx in Aging and Cognitive Health**

- **Aging and Cognitive Decline**: Aging leads to the deterioration of the brain's glycocalyx, which is crucial for maintaining cognitive function. As the glycocalyx thins, the blood-brain barrier becomes more permeable, allowing harmful substances to enter the brain and contributing to neuroinflammation and cognitive decline. This highlights the need for strategies to protect and restore this sugar shield to combat age-related cognitive issues.

- **Significance of Glycocalyx**: The glycocalyx acts as a protective sugar layer on blood-brain barrier cells. In younger brains, this layer is dense and effective, but it becomes sparse and degraded with age. Understanding the structure and function of the glycocalyx is essential for developing interventions that can restore its integrity and improve brain health.

- **Research Breakthroughs at Stanford**: A team of Stanford researchers, led by Sophia Shi and Carolyn Bertozzi, has shifted focus from proteins and DNA to sugars, uncovering the critical role of glycocalyx in brain aging. Their findings suggest that restoring key sugars can enhance the blood-brain barrier's integrity and potentially reverse cognitive decline in aged models.
