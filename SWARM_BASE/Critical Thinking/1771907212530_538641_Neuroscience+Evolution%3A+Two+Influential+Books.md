# Neuroscience Evolution: Two Influential Books

Created at 2024/08/31 8:08 PM

The article discusses how two books shaped the author’s understanding of neuroscience:

	1.	“The Astonishing Hypothesis” by Francis Crick (1994): This book influenced the author’s early career, presenting a reductionist view that consciousness and free will are the results of nerve cell activity. It sparked the author’s interest in neuroscience, although its ideas now feel outdated due to advances in the field.
	2.	“Free Agents: How Evolution Gave Us Free Will” by Kevin Mitchell (2023): Mitchell’s book challenges reductionism, proposing that free will emerges from the brain as a complex, dynamic system. This perspective has inspired the author to explore new ways to understand brain functions like free will, vision, and memory.

These books reflect the shift from reductionist to dynamical systems thinking in neuroscience over the past 30 years.

**📚 Crick's Reductionist View**
- **Reductionism in Neuroscience**: Crick's 'The Astonishing Hypothesis' posits that consciousness and free will are merely the results of nerve cell activity, reflecting a mechanistic approach to understanding the brain.
- **Influence on Early Career**: The book inspired the author to pursue a career in neuroscience, despite its ideas feeling outdated in light of recent advancements.
- **Legacy of Crick's Ideas**: While Crick's work was groundbreaking in the 1990s, it now serves as a historical reference point for the evolution of neuroscience thinking.

**🔄 Mitchell's Dynamic Systems Approach**
- **Emergence of Free Will**: Mitchell's 'Free Agents' argues that free will arises from the brain's complex dynamics, challenging the reductionist view by suggesting that higher-level mental states influence brain activity.
- **Top-Down Causality**: The concept of top-down influences shaping future brain states introduces a new understanding of causality, where mental states can both emerge from and affect brain activity.
- **Noise and Future States**: Mitchell posits that noise in brain activity allows for multiple potential future states, which are guided by top-down influences, creating a framework for understanding free will.
