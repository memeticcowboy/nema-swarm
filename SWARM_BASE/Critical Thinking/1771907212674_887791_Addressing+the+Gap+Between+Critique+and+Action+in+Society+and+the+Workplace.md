# Addressing the Gap Between Critique and Action in Society and the Workplace

Created at 2024/11/06 10:41 AM

The problem Peter Jones seems to address centers around a common pattern he observes: people’s preference for engaging in discussions at a "conspiracy theory level" or merely identifying problems without the follow-through to effect real change. This pattern reflects a larger issue with effort and accountability in the workplace and society—where individuals may critique and complain but often avoid the demanding work needed for impactful action.

Peter illustrates this through several points:

1. **Preference for Complaining over Solving**: He questions if people choose to engage in superficial critiques or conspiratorial thinking because it allows them to appear active and concerned without doing the "real work" to address the issues they critique.
2. **Ownership and Effort Divide**: He highlights the discrepancy between company owners or creators who put in substantial time and energy to build opportunities and employees who may expect results to come to them effortlessly, embodying a passive approach to work.
3. **Stages of Engagement (ACSEPO Sequence)**: Peter presents the stages people typically go through—Analyst, Creator, Strategist, Empath, Protector, and Optimizer. He argues that while many reach the Analyst stage (identifying problems), few advance to the later stages that involve actionable strategy, empathy, protection, or optimization, likely due to personal commitments like jobs and family.
4. **Potential in Web3 and Tokenization**: He sees Web3 and tokenization as a hopeful solution, where individuals could contribute to causes like climate change on a smaller, sustainable scale. Through tokenization, individuals could be rewarded for their impact (even if tokens hold nominal value), recognizing contributions to environmental and social impact.

The underlying problem Peter appears to be addressing is a societal tendency to fall short of translating awareness and criticism into concrete action, especially on large-scale issues like climate change. He suggests that while many are content to criticize or analyze, few progress to active roles (like strategist, empath, or optimizer), with Web3 presenting a structure to encourage and recognize genuine contributions.
