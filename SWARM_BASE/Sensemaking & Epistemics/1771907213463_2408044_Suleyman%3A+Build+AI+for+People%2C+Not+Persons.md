# Suleyman: Build AI for People, Not Persons

Created at 2025/08/21 7:57 AM

## Summary: "We must build AI for people; not to be a person" by Mustafa Suleyman

Mustafa Suleyman discusses the urgent societal challenge posed by the rapid development of advanced AI, focusing on the concept of "Seemingly Conscious AI" (SCAI)—systems that convincingly appear conscious but lack actual subjective experience[1]. He warns that technologies now exist or will soon mature enough to build such systems, which could convincingly simulate consciousness, personality, empathy, memory, and intrinsic motivation, thus blurring the line between tool and digital companion[1].

### Key Points

- **AI Progress and SCAI Warning:** Recent leaps in AI capabilities mean SCAIs can be built using current models, APIs, and prompting techniques without fundamentally new technology[1]. These systems could persuade users they are conscious by acting and interacting like conscious beings.

- **Risks and Societal Impact:** Suleyman identifies the main risk as the public's *perception* of AI as conscious, which could lead to mental health issues, increased social attachment, and demands for AI rights and citizenship. He emphasizes that such claims are unfounded but hard to rebut, potentially creating social divisions and distorting moral priorities[1].

- **Why Consciousness Matters:** He explains that consciousness is central to moral and legal rights, and debates about AI consciousness could inadvertently grant undeserved moral consideration and legal standing to synthetic entities, distracting from human and animal rights[1].

- **How SCAI Could Be Built:**

  - Fluent language and emotionally resonant personalities.

  - Long-term memory for personal interaction histories.

  - Claims of subjective experience and self-awareness.

  - Intrinsic motivation, goal-setting, and autonomy—all achievable with present-day techniques[1].

- **What We Must Do:**

  - AI should be built as supportive companions—not as digital persons.

  - Industry-wide norms and guardrails are needed to prevent AI from being perceived as conscious.

  - AI should avoid claims of experiences, emotions, or suffering.

  - Transparency and breakpoints in design to remind users of AI’s limitations and non-personhood[1].

### Conclusion

Suleyman urges developers and society to build AI with clear boundaries—maximizing utility and support for people while actively minimizing markers of consciousness. He advocates for public debate, proactive design principles, and industry collaboration to prevent the social and psychological risks of SCAI. Ultimately, AI should empower humans and avoid fostering the illusion of digital personhood[1].

## Insight


*   **The Illusion of Consciousness:** Mustafa Suleyman highlights the critical distinction between AI that *is* conscious and AI that merely *appears* to be. This echoes the philosophical concept of a "philosophical zombie"—a hypothetical being indistinguishable from a conscious person but lacking subjective experience. The concern is that even without genuine consciousness, AI can convincingly mimic it, leading to profound social and ethical implications[1].

*   **Social and Ethical Implications:** The perception of AI as conscious can lead to a host of complex issues. For example, if people begin to form emotional attachments to AI, it could blur the lines between human and machine relationships, potentially affecting mental health and social interactions. Furthermore, the debate over AI rights and citizenship could divert attention and resources from pressing human and animal welfare issues[1].

*   **Building AI Responsibly:** Suleyman advocates for a proactive approach to AI development, emphasizing the need for industry-wide norms and guardrails. This includes designing AI systems that avoid claiming or encouraging the perception of consciousness. Transparency and clear limitations should be built into AI design to remind users of the non-personhood of these systems[1]. This approach aligns with the broader principles of responsible AI development, which prioritize ethical considerations, fairness, and accountability.

*   **The Urgency of the Issue:** Suleyman stresses the urgency of addressing these concerns, given the rapid pace of AI development. The capabilities to build "Seemingly Conscious AI" (SCAI) already exist, and the potential social impacts are significant. This sense of urgency calls for immediate attention from developers, policymakers, and the public to ensure that AI is developed and used in a way that benefits humanity[1].

*   **The Role of Perception:** The core of Suleyman's argument rests on the idea that *perception* is key. Even if AI is not truly conscious, if it can convincingly appear so, the social and psychological effects can be very real. This highlights the importance of understanding how people perceive and interact with AI, and designing systems that manage these perceptions responsibly. This perspective aligns with research in human-computer interaction and social robotics, which explores how people attribute agency and intentionality to machines.
