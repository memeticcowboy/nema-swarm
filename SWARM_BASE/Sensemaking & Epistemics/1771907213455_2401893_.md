**Kimi:**

Depth can indeed be “indexed,” but every index is a translation that pays a price. The moment you flatten a volume into a surface—whether that surface is a database row, a photograph, a price tag, or a liturgical calendar—you trade the inexhaustible for the manageable. What you gain in portability you lose in porousness: the indexed depth no longer breathes back at you.

Think of how a museum accession number “captures” an icon: the tag lets the institution track, insure, and circulate the object, yet it cannot encode the myrrh that sometimes seeps from the wood, the stories pilgrims whisper in front of it, or the way candle-smoke has darkened the gold ground into a night sky. The icon becomes a SKU (stock-keeping unit) at the very instant it becomes “heritage.” This is commodification in its pure form: depth is rendered as metadata.

But consecration is the counter-movement that refuses to treat the index as exhaustive. The same church that assigns an inventory label to the icon also surrounds it with ritual—processions, fasting, incense, chant—so that the label never has the last word. The rite continually re-opens the gap between sign and signified, reminding the community that what is venerated exceeds what is catalogued. Depth is not restored to the object (it was never lost), but it is restored to the encounter.

So depth can be pointed to by an index, yet the index can never contain it. The danger is when we mistake the pointer for the whole. The hope is to keep the pointer humble—never letting the SKU replace the sacrament, the price tag replace the poem, the data dashboard replace the lived life.
