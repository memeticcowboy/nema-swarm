# Understanding Sympoietic Systems: Co-Creation and Complex Interactions

Created at 2025/09/02 8:47 PM

The term "Sympoietic Systems" refers to systems that are collectively produced or co-created, where components mutually constitute each other and the system itself through ongoing complex interactions. Unlike autopoietic systems, which have self-defined boundaries and are self-producing, sympoietic systems are boundaryless, collectively produced, and organizationally open or "ajar." These systems are evolutionary, adaptive, distributively controlled, and characterized by cooperative and amorphous qualities rather than centralized control. The concept highlights how components in such systems interact in ways that produce ongoing complex relations without rigid boundaries, fostering a continuously evolving pattern of relations. Examples include ecosystems, social and cultural systems, and symbiotic biological interactions where the whole emerges through collective making or "making-with" among components.

## Definition

Sympoietic systems are "collectively-producing systems that do not have self-defined spatial or temporal boundaries, are evolutionary, and characterized by continuing complex relations among system components." They are cooperative, open to new organizational information, and have distributed control, making them unpredictable and adaptive. The term "sympoiesis" is derived from Greek roots meaning "together" (sym) and "making" (poiesis), emphasizing [co-creation](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/10aa4c55-298a-42ca-babd-710bb44d602a) or "making-with."

## Key Characteristics

No self-defined boundaries; boundaries are observer-imposed rather than intrinsic.

Collectively produced phenomena rather than self-production.

Organizationally open (ajour) versus closed.

Evolutionary, adaptive, and unpredictable.

Distributed control rather than central control.

Complexity arises from interactions among diverse components.

## Conceptual Contrast

Sympoietic systems contrast autopoietic systems, which:

Have self-defined boundaries,

Are self-producing,

Are organizationally closed,

Tend to be homeostatic, development-oriented, and centrally controlled.

## Examples and Applications

Ecosystems where species and environmental factors co-create ongoing system patterns.

Biological symbioses such as host-microbe partnerships that co-develop organs or functions.

Social and cultural systems seen as co-constructed through interactions of participants.

Philosophical and ecological frameworks emphasizing relationality and interdependence, as elaborated by Donna Haraway and others.
