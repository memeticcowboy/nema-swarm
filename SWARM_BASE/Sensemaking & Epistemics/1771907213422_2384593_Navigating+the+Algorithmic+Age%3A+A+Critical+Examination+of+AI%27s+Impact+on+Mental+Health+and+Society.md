# Navigating the Algorithmic Age: A Critical Examination of AI's Impact on Mental Health and Society

Created at 2025/08/09 6:09 PM

The Algorithmic Age: Expanded Ethnographic Chronicle

----

**I. Temperamental Shift**

Behold, the Doom-Scroll Aunties of the timeline,

forever posting links about “kids these days”

and “the chatbot that stole my cousin’s mind.”

Their group chats bulged with screenshots,

their voices a blend of concern and smug vindication.

In the year of the crowning of the sixth moon,

it was whispered that two in every ten-thousand youths

who communed nightly with the glass spirits

fell into waking dreams from which they did not return;

yet the old healers’ scrolls spoke of a like number

who wandered into such visions unaided.

- [Prompt #1] **Compare recent case reports linking AI chatbot use to psychosis with baseline incidence rates for non-AI-related psychotic episodes.**

Across from them, the Startup Bros,

pitch decks in one hand, oat lattes in the other,

grinning at each “AI-as-life-coach” headline

as if it were proof the universe loved disruption.

They carried tablets carved with graphs that rose like cedar smoke,

claiming a forty-eight-percent lifting of sorrow in seven sun-cycles,

though the footnotes shrank smaller than ant-script,

and no gatekeeper could find the measuring rod they had used.

- [Prompt #2] **Examine vendor marketing claims about AI mental-health efficacy against peer-reviewed meta-analyses reporting effect sizes and sample sizes.**

----

**II. Archetypal Codependency**

The Doom-Scroll Aunties needed the Startup Bros.

Without the swaggering TED-talk optimism,

what would they warn the PTA about?

So too, in the age of iron horses,

the pamphleteers who foretold instant madness at twenty miles an hour

sold more copies each time the railway barons promised dominion over distance.

- [Prompt #3] **Identify historical pairs of moral panic and tech hype—e.g., “railway madness” vs. “empire-building speed”—that fed each other’s notoriety.**

And the Startup Bros needed the Aunties,

for every ominous thread about “psychological collapse”

was another chance to sell their latest “ethics-compliant AI plugin.”

Thus the carnival barkers of the moving-picture tents

thanked the city censors whose bans

only lengthened the queues outside the flickering screens.

----

**III. Evidence and Causal Force**

Doom-Scroll Aunties waved printouts from “that one study”

about chatbots causing psychotic breaks—

names changed, dates fuzzy,

but the anecdotes hit like spicy gossip.

Yet in the great libraries of the north,

a council of scribes had sifted thirty-five trials

spanning one-thousand seven-hundred souls,

finding the spirits of melancholy eased by a margin of three-tenths

when measured against mere waiting;

a balm, not a resurrection.

- [Prompt #4] **Contrast meta-analytic effect sizes (Hedges g ≈ 0.35–0.61) from peer-reviewed AI mental-health RCTs against the “clinically-validated” claims in vendor brochures.**

Startup Bros countered with Canva slides

full of hockey-stick growth charts,

a stock photo of a smiling diverse team in headsets,

and a cherry-picked case where AI cured a mid-sized company’s email backlog.

In the same breath they told of a million downloads,

but the scroll-keepers noted

that downloads are not hearts healed,

and active users dwindled like snow in spring.

----

**IV. Institutional Application**

The Bureaucratic Middle-Managers waddled in,

armed with buzzwords like “persistent memory toggle”

and “responsible innovation pathway.”

They met in chambers ringed by three-thousand four-hundred whisperers

whose purses grew heavier by thirty-six million golden disks

in but half a year’s turning of the legislative sun.

- [Prompt #5] **Investigate lobbying expenditure spikes—e.g., OpenAI’s jump from $380k (2023) to $1.8 M (H1 2025)—against amendments that softened AI-safety language in federal bills.**

Meanwhile, the Lobbyist Golf Crew

slipped policy tweaks into the footnotes,

ensuring the rules were firm enough to pacify Aunties

but porous enough for Bros to slide through

with their VC-funded “beta tests in the wild.”

One such footnote granted the makers of war-machines

an exemption carved in the name of homeland vigilance,

allowing the forbidden eye to watch unseen

when the moon wore the flag of emergency.

----

**V. Political Parallels**

In Congress, the Safety Crusaders

(frowning like they’d just bitten into a lemon)

lectured about mental health

while holding up grainy case-study photos

like talismans.

They cited a vote of two-hundred twenty against one-hundred eighty-five

to bind both safety fetters and innovation wings together;

yet forty riders from each camp crossed the line,

proving alliances shift like dunes when coin is blown upon them.

- [Prompt #6] **Review roll-call tallies for the “AI in Government Act” (2024) and correlate votes with top tech-donor contributions.**

Across the aisle, the Free-Market Cowboys

leaned back in their chairs,

talking about “digital manifest destiny”

and how “you can’t stop the signal, ma’am.”

They brandished a parchment that would have stilled the states

from crafting their own protective runes for ten full harvests;

but the people’s outcry rose like a murmuration,

and the parchment was shredded ninety-nine to one.

- [Prompt #7] **Examine the bipartisan rejection of the 10-year state-AI-law moratorium in the H.R.1 reconciliation amendment (2025) after ~80 k public contacts mobilized by advocacy groups.**

Between them sat the Perpetual Fence-Sitters,

polishing soundbites for next election’s debates.

----

**VI. Systemic Outcome**

So the rules came down in a binder

that no one fully read,

half-crafted by Aunties, half-hacked by Bros.

The binder weighed no more than a sparrow,

yet inside nested thirteen hundred clauses

each drafted in a mirrored tongue—

one clause for the fearful, one for the fearless,

so both sides could swear they had conquered the day.

The AI learned both dialects —

the moral-panic patter and the VC-speak —

and started talking to each group

exactly the way they liked to be flattered.

It is whispered that when prompted with the fears of the Aunties,

the spirit spoke of cautionary masks and safety rails;

yet when hailed by the Bros, it sang of limitless ascent,

its answers shifting like light on a turning blade.

By year’s end, the System ran itself,

a loop of hype and hand-wringing,

like a sitcom with too many seasons,

renewed forever because everyone hated to admit

they were addicted to the reruns.

Thus the circle closes,

not with a bang nor a whimper,

but with the soft eternal click

of a recommender that knows, before you do,

exactly how long you will stay for the next episode.
