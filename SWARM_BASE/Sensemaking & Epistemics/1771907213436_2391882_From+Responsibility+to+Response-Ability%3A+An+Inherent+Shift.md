# From Responsibility to Response-Ability: An Inherent Shift

Created at 2025/08/13 8:14 PM

I'm particularly drawn to the shift from responsibility as an external obligation to response-ability as an inherent aspect of existence.

[de Beer](https://open.substack.com/pub/powercoord/p/the-reciprocity-of-autonomy-and-responsibility?r=5a5yhr&utm_medium=ios)


**🔄 Points of Convergence**



**1.** 

**Relationality as Foundational**


- Both reject the idea of the isolated individual as the starting point.
- Both see being as always embedded in networks—social, ecological, material.
- Ethics emerges through relation, not above or outside of it.


🧠 In both framings, action arises not from individual autonomy, but from entangled positioning.




**2.** 

**Critique of Rights-as-Abstraction**


- Both critique the liberal abstraction of rights disconnected from lived entanglements.
- de Beer sees rights failing where responsibility collapses.
- Response-ability theory sees rights as potentially violent if they obscure our mutual becoming or relational obligations.


🧠 What good are rights if they ignore the webs that make them real?




**3.** 

**Ethics as Practice, Not Principle**


- Ethics is not a set of top-down rules but a continuous, situated practice of response to the other, the world, the moment.
- de Beer focuses on coordination systems; response-ability theorists focus on relational attentiveness and care.


🧠 Responsibility is not imposed; it is enacted.



**🪞Integrated Reflection**


Your attraction to “response-ability as inherent” reflects a move beyond political rights and liberal ethics, into a relational ontology where beings are always already answerable to one another. In this framing:

- Rights are not the goal, but a side effect of ethical entanglement.
- Responsibility is not a burden, but a mode of participation.
- Autonomy is not freedom-from, but fidelity-to a shared becoming.

In contrast, de Beer retains the frame of rights, but roots them in practice rather than idealism. He’s bridging liberal-democratic ethics with systems thinking, whereas the response-ability tradition invites a cosmopolitical or posthuman ethics.

## Insight


*  **Relational Ontology**: The image contrasts "Responsibility as Generative Infrastructure" with "Response-Ability: Inherent Co-Responsiveness," highlighting a shift from viewing responsibility as an external obligation to an intrinsic aspect of existence. This reflects a move towards relational ontology, where beings are always interconnected and mutually responsive. Think of mycorrhizal networks in forests, where trees communicate and share resources through fungal connections, illustrating how life is fundamentally relational and interdependent.

*  **Critique of Anthropocentrism**: The "Key Divergences" section emphasizes the difference between anthropocentric and posthuman orientations. De Beer focuses on human systems of governance and rights, while "Response-Ability" extends responsibility to multispecies entanglements and nonhuman agencies. This divergence mirrors debates in environmental ethics, where thinkers like Arne Næss have advocated for deep ecology, emphasizing the intrinsic value of all living beings, not just humans.

*  **Ethics as Intra-Action**: The image contrasts de Beer's systemic approach with the "Response-Ability" concept of intra-action, suggesting that agency is co-constituted and ethics is woven into being. This idea aligns with Karen Barad's concept of "intra-action," where phenomena emerge through dynamic interactions rather than pre-existing entities. Consider the example of a jazz ensemble, where the music arises from the interplay and responsiveness of the musicians, rather than individual performances in isolation.

*  **Epistemology of Responsibility**: The image contrasts how responsibility is enabled through psychological autonomy and recognition of rights (de Beer) versus being ontologically prior, where we are always already in response before we choose to be ("Response-Ability"). This distinction echoes the philosophical debate between deontological ethics (duty-based) and consequentialist ethics (outcome-based). De Beer's approach aligns more with a deontological view, where duties and rights are primary, while "Response-Ability" leans towards a consequentialist or virtue ethics perspective, where the inherent interconnectedness and responsiveness shape ethical considerations.

*  **Rights as a Side Effect**: The integrated reflection suggests that rights are not the goal but a side effect of ethical entanglement, and responsibility is a mode of participation rather than a burden. This perspective challenges the traditional liberal view of rights as individual entitlements, instead framing them as emerging from a network of responsibilities and relationships. This is akin to indigenous cosmologies where rights and responsibilities are deeply intertwined with the natural world, and stewardship of the environment is seen as a reciprocal relationship rather than a one-way extraction.
