Key Ideas

	•	Self as an Illusion:

	•	The self is not what we typically believe it to be. It’s an illusion shaped by our perception and cognitive processes.

	•	The term “illusion” indicates something that is not as it seems, not a denial of the self’s existence.

	•	Cartesian Theater:

	•	A metaphor for the belief in a central place in the brain where experiences come together. Blackmore argues this does not exist.

	•	Our experiences are processed in parallel across multiple areas of the brain without a unifying “experiencer.”

	•	Experience of Consciousness:

	•	We feel a unified self experiencing thoughts, but this is a delusion. The brain processes sensory input through various pathways without a central “self” managing these experiences.

	•	Visual Cortex: Involved in processing visual information through multiple pathways (ventral and dorsal streams).

	•	Mindfulness and Consciousness:

	•	Mindfulness practices reveal that awareness fluctuates; we are not continuously conscious.

	•	Engaging in mindfulness leads to an awareness of multiple streams of consciousness, not a singular self.

Important Terms

	•	Illusion: Something that misrepresents reality; in this context, the common perception of a singular self.

	•	Cartesian Dualism: The philosophical idea that the mind and body are separate; Blackmore argues this is outdated.

Critical Examples

	•	Decision-Making Evidence:

	•	Studies (e.g., Libet’s experiments) show that brain activity related to actions occurs before we are consciously aware of our decisions. This suggests that the “self” may not initiate actions.

	•	Mindfulness Practice:

	•	Asking oneself “Am I conscious now?” can lead to an awareness that previously unnoticed experiences were occurring.

	•	The phenomenon of becoming aware of consciousness indicates that our self-perception is not continuous.

Summary of Complex Concepts

	•	Parallel Processing in the Brain:

	•	The brain operates with numerous pathways processing different types of information simultaneously, leading to the illusion of a coherent self.

	•	Self-Construct:

	•	The self is a construct made from experiences and memories rather than a fixed entity.

	•	Temporal Parietal Junction:

	•	A brain region involved in constructing the body image and self-perception.

Philosophical Implications

	•	Moral Considerations:

	•	If the self is an illusion, questions arise about responsibility, suffering, and action.

	•	Blackmore suggests empathy remains important as future selves will share similarities with our current self.

	•	Experiencing Death:

	•	The act of “dying” can be viewed as a continuous process of self-disillusionment and rebirth of new selves.
