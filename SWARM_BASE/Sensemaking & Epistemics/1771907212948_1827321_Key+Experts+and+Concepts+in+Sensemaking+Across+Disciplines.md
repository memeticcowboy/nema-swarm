**Background on Sensemaking**
Sensemaking is the process by which people interpret and give meaning to ambiguous or complex situations, often used in organizational studies, information science, and human-computer interaction. It helps individuals and groups understand and act in uncertain environments.
**Notable Experts**
Here are some experts recognized for their contributions:
- **Karl E. Weick**: Known for introducing sensemaking to organizational studies, with significant works like “Sensemaking in Organizations” ([Sensemaking in Organizations book](https://www.amazon.ca/Sensemaking-Organizations-Karl-Weick/dp/080397177X)).
- **Brenda Dervin**: Pioneered sensemaking in information science, focusing on how people bridge knowledge gaps.
- **Christian Madsbjerg**: Focuses on sensemaking in business, with books like “Sensemaking: The Power of the Humanities in the Age of the Algorithm” ([Sensemaking book by Madsbjerg](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/30841991-sensemaking)).
- **Daniel M. Russell**: Contributed to sensemaking in human-computer interaction, particularly in designing interactive systems.
- **Dave Snowden**: Known for the Cynefin framework, which aids sensemaking in complex systems ([Centre for Public Impact on sensemaking](https://centreforpublicimpact.org/resource-hub/what-is-sensemaking/)).
- **Gary A. Klein**: Explored sensemaking in decision-making, especially in high-stakes environments.
This list reflects a mix of foundational and contemporary experts, acknowledging the interdisciplinary nature of the field.

**Definition and Scope**
Sensemaking, as outlined in resources like [Wikipedia on Sensemaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensemaking), is not a monolithic concept but varies by context. It has been defined as “the ongoing retrospective development of plausible images that rationalize what people are doing” (Weick, Sutcliffe, & Obstfeld, 2005, p. 409), introduced to organizational studies by Karl E. Weick in the late 1960s. It extends to information science, human-computer interaction (HCI), and decision-making, with applications in business, disaster response, and pandemic research, as seen in [Atlantic Council on sensemaking in pandemics](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/geotech-cues/making-sense-of-sensemaking-what-it-is-and-what-it-means-for-pandemic-research/).
The field’s interdisciplinary nature means experts come from diverse backgrounds, including psychology, sociology, and computer science, making the identification of key figures complex but enriching.
**Historical and Foundational Experts**
The foundational work in sensemaking is heavily attributed to Karl E. Weick, whose book “Sensemaking in Organizations” ([Sensemaking in Organizations book](https://www.amazon.ca/Sensemaking-Organizations-Karl-Weick/dp/080397177X)) is a landmark volume. Weick’s re-analysis of the Bhopal disaster in the 1980s highlighted situated sensemaking in crisis situations, emphasizing its role in organizational behavior and structure. His work, cited in [EPIC on sensemaking reflections](https://www.epicpeople.org/sensemaking-in-organizations/), has influenced organizational theory by shifting focus from decision-making to meaning-making processes.
Brenda Dervin, another pivotal figure, introduced sensemaking as a methodology in information science in the 1980s, focusing on how individuals bridge cognitive gaps, as noted in [Psychology Wiki on sensemaking](https://psychology.fandom.com/wiki/Sensemaking). Her work has been instrumental in understanding human information behavior, particularly in library and information science contexts.
In HCI, Daniel M. Russell, along with Mark Stefik, Peter Pirolli, and Stuart Card, contributed significantly through a 1993 seminal paper, as mentioned in [Wikipedia on sensemaking in information science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensemaking_%28information_science%29). Their work focused on designing interactive systems for sensemaking, influencing how technology supports human cognition in data-rich environments.
**Contemporary and Applied Experts**
More recent contributions include Christian Madsbjerg, whose books “Sensemaking: The Power of the Humanities in the Age of the Algorithm” ([Sensemaking book by Madsbjerg](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/30841991-sensemaking)) and related works advocate for humanities-based thinking in business. His consultancy, ReD Associates, applies sensemaking to strategy, as seen in [ReD Associates on sensemaking](https://www.redassociates.com/sensemaking), emphasizing human intuition over data-driven approaches.
Dave Snowden, known for the Cynefin framework, has advanced sensemaking in complex systems, particularly in organizational and public sector contexts, as discussed in [Centre for Public Impact on sensemaking](https://centreforpublicimpact.org/resource-hub/what-is-sensemaking/). His work with SenseMaker tools, detailed in [MDPI on SenseMaker as a research tool](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-8954/7/2/25), provides methods for capturing and analyzing human perceptions, influencing monitoring and evaluation practices.
Gary A. Klein, a cognitive psychologist, has explored sensemaking in decision-making, particularly in safety-critical situations, as seen in [ResearchGate on sensemaking and resilience](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/327546622_Sensemaking_and_resilience_in_safety-critical_situations_a_literature_review). His work on naturalistic decision-making complements sensemaking theories, offering insights into how professionals make sense in high-stakes environments.
**Emerging and Recent Contributors**
Recent research, such as [MDPI on multifaceted sensemaking theory](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-8954/11/3/145), highlights authors like Suliman Hawamdeh and G. Mastanamma, who conducted systematic reviews, indicating ongoing academic interest. R.J. Cordes, as noted in [Atlantic Council on sensemaking in pandemics](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/geotech-cues/making-sense-of-sensemaking-what-it-is-and-what-it-means-for-pandemic-research/), has contributed to sensemaking in narrative and memetic warfare, focusing on complex systems during crises like COVID-19.
Thomas Aston, in a 2024 Medium post ([Medium on sensemaking boom](https://thomasmtaston.medium.com/making-sense-of-all-the-sense-making-c6d6eff2994f)), reflects on the recent boom in sensemaking, integrating stakeholder experience with evidence, suggesting a growing practical application in organizational learning.

**Controversies and Debates**
There is some debate on who qualifies as an expert, given sensemaking’s interdisciplinary nature. For instance, while Weick is universally recognized, newer figures like Cordes and Aston may be seen as commentators rather than core researchers by some academics. Additionally, the lack of a single definition, as noted in [Wikipedia on Sensemaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensemaking), leads to disagreements on whether sensemaking is individual, social, or situational, affecting expert consensus.
