# Emerging Knowledge: Rethinking Rules and Patterns

Created at 2025/12/04 9:33 AM

Carlos wrote:
The power of emergent knowledge<br><br>For 50 years, linguists believed your brain runs a hidden grammar engine—unconsciously applying thousands of rules to generate sentences.<br><br>LLMs just proved this is completely wrong.<br><br>And the implications go way beyond language.<br><br>The old theory made sense. Language is structured. It has patterns. Where there are patterns, there must be rules generating them, right?<br><br>So we tried to formalize everything. The entire Semantic Web was built on this assumption: encode the rules, achieve understanding.<br><br>Then LLMs showed up.<br><br>They've never been taught a single grammar rule. They just... read a lot. Trillions of sentences.<br>And they write better than most humans.<br><br>This isn't an engineering trick. It's a discovery about what language fundamentally is.<br><br>Patterns don't require rules to generate them.<br>The rules were always just our description of patterns that already existed.<br><br>Where else have we made this mistake?<br><br>Where else have we confused "how we describe it" with "how it has to work"?<br><br>Consider Python dependency management.<br><br>For 20 years, millions of developers accepted it was inherently painful. Virtual environments were clunky, but that was "the state of the art."<br><br>Then uv appeared in 2024. One tool. Suddenly everything just worked.<br><br>The "rules" of how dependency management should work weren't requirements.<br><br>They were descriptions of one bad approach that everyone had collectively accepted as necessary.<br><br>NASA has twice SpaceX's budget.<br><br>SpaceX has launched more missions in the last decade than NASA did in fifty years.<br><br>Same physics. Same goal. Radically different relationship to "process."<br><br>NASA follows the rules:<br><br>Multi-year planning cycles<br><br>Formal review boards<br><br>Institutional accountability chains<br>SpaceX breaks things fast, learns fast.<br><br>The process was supposed to ensure quality. It became a substitute for it.<br><br>Something works through emergent patterns<br><br>We describe those patterns as "rules"<br><br>We forget they were descriptions<br><br>We enforce the rules as requirements<br>The rules become obstacles to the thing they were describing<br><br>Grammar rules don't generate language. Grammar rules describe where language already goes.<br><br>A river doesn't follow the map. The map describes the river.<br><br>The author I'm riffing on (Daniel Lemire @lemire) makes a provocative claim:<br><br>"The bureaucratisation of science was a death sentence for progress."<br><br>Strong. But the argument structure is hard to dismiss.<br><br>We created formal scientific processes to describe how good science happens.<br><br>Then we forgot these were descriptions and started treating them as requirements.<br><br>Now the process has become the point.<br><br>The tired excuse: "All the low-hanging fruit has been picked."<br>What if the fruit is right there, but we've convinced ourselves we need a 47-step approval process before we can reach for it?<br><br>Darwin needed a ship before he could develop evolution.<br>Einstein's relativity emerged when trains made relative motion obvious.<br><br>The neat story—professors think deep thoughts, engineers build stuff—is historically backwards.<br><br>Tools enable thoughts. Thoughts enable tools.<br>It's a loop, not a line.<br><br>LLMs aren't "just engineering." They revealed something true about the world.<br><br>So what do you do with this?<br><br>Next time you encounter a "rule" or "process," ask:<br>Is this a requirement? Or a description we've been enforcing?<br><br>Notice the difference between:<br><br>"This is how it has to work"<br><br>"This is how we've been describing it"<br>Most "has to" is actually "we assumed."<br><br>The deepest version of this insight:<br><br>What LLMs reveal about language might apply to institutions too.<br><br>Neither requires explicit rules. Both emerge from patterns of practice. Both calcify when we forget that.<br><br>I'm still working out the limits of this.<br><br>Does it apply to law? Medicine? Engineering safety standards?<br><br>Some rules probably are requirements. The question is which ones.<br><br>But I suspect we've overcounted badly.

* The image illustrates a paradigm shift from rule-based approaches to pattern recognition in various fields, notably language and science, reflecting Carlos's insight about how traditional beliefs may obstruct progress. This evolution challenges the assumption that explicit rules are essential for generating complex systems like language.

This aligns with the principles of emergent knowledge, where behavior and patterns can emerge from practice rather than enforced rules. For instance, LLMs (Large Language Models) perform remarkably by assimilating vast amounts of data without having predefined grammar rules, showcasing the power of underlying patterns. 

* The text parallels NASA's bureaucratic processes with SpaceX's iterative and experimental approach in space exploration, suggesting that rigid structures can hinder innovation. This reflects a broader critique of how institutional frameworks can sometimes become obstacles to efficiency and discovery, as discussed by Daniel Lemire.

* The notion of "emergent knowledge" extends beyond linguistics and engineering to various fields such as law and medicine, prompting us to reassess the necessity of established processes. It questions whether these frameworks genuinely serve progress or merely entrench outdated methods.

* Finally, challenging entrenched rules and processes by asking whether they are requirements or simply descriptive can empower more effective practices, leading to greater innovation across disciplines. This insight encourages a critical reflection on how we approach complex problems and the potential pathways for future advancements.
