# Primal World Belief Survey

Created at 2024/09/24 12:20 PM

Midjourney's [user survey](https://www.midjourney.com/surveys?survey=personality-values-beliefs) is based on this work.

The paper titled *Primal World Beliefs* by Clifton et al. (2019) introduces and validates the concept of "primal world beliefs" (or "primals"), which are fundamental beliefs about the overall character of the world. Here are the key findings from this study:

- **Introduction of Primals**: Primals are beliefs that reflect the world's fundamental characteristics, such as whether it is safe, interesting, or dangerous. These beliefs influence how people perceive and interact with their environment, impacting behavior and psychological well-being.
- **Development of Primals Inventory (PI-99)**: The researchers developed a 99-item inventory, the Primals Inventory (PI-99), which identifies 26 primal beliefs. These were categorized into three higher-order beliefs: *Safe*, *Enticing*, and *Alive*. The inventory showed strong psychometric properties with high internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = 0.93) and stability across time.
- **Stability Over Time**: Test-retest data demonstrated that primal world beliefs are stable over time, with correlations of 0.88, 0.75, and 0.77 over 2 weeks, 9 months, and 19 months, respectively.
- **Correlation with Well-being and Personality**: The primal beliefs were strongly correlated with personality traits and measures of well-being. For example, the belief in a *Safe* world correlated with optimism (r = 0.61), while the belief in an *Enticing* world negatively correlated with depression (r = -0.52).
- **Explanation of Variance**: These primal beliefs explained more variance in life satisfaction, transcendent experiences, trust, and gratitude than the Big Five personality traits, with improvements of 3%, 3%, 6%, and 12%, respectively.

The study provides strong evidence that primal world beliefs are critical in shaping an individual’s personality, well-being, and worldview. The introduction of the PI-99 opens new avenues for exploring how fundamental beliefs about the world impact mental health and life outcomes.
