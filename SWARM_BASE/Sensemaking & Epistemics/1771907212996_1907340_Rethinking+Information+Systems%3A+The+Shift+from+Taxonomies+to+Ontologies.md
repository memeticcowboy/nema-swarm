# Rethinking Information Systems: The Shift from Taxonomies to Ontologies

Created at 2025/03/24 12:09 PM

❓ Why Classification Systems Fail Humans ❓ <br><br>Rigid, hierarchical classification systems that impose a taxonomy-first view—parent/child hierarchies, labels, and silos, rather than a contextual, relational, semantic, and purpose-driven model, fail humans.<br><br>While the Linnean model worked well for classifying the natural world, it has limited applicability in managing business information. Humans don’t naturally think in rigid hierarchies; they operate in relational networks, where meaning arises from context and interactions. Yet modern systems, through reliance on rigid taxonomies and naming conventions, have entrenched this unnatural structure into information design. This misalignment leads to problems like:<br><br>👉 Information silos that obstruct data flow and integration.<br>👉 Misinterpretation of function and purpose due to isolated classification.<br>👉 Overreliance on naming conventions, which are not sufficient in multilingual, cross-disciplinary or complex environments.<br><br>💡 The Case for Ontological Thinking in Modern Information Systems 💡 <br><br>To truly unlock the potential of data, information systems must adopt ontological, not taxonomical, structures. Ontologies enable:<br><br>👏 Relational, multi-dimensional modelling of reality (e.g. Digital Twins).<br>👏 Cross-functional, lateral data connections, enabling better decision-making.<br>👏 Semantic interoperability, especially across cultural, disciplinary, or linguistic boundaries.<br>👏 Data reuse and integration, where an object can serve many purposes depending on context (semantic contextuality and disambiguation).<br><br>An ontology allows data to mirror human experience, enabling richer, more intuitive, and more accurate representations of the real world. It also allows concepts to be mapped across cultures. This is why AI systems are successful—they reflect the ontological, not the 17th Century taxonomical, approach to connecting and retrieving information.<br><br>The ontological approach brings together history, philosophy, and data science to make a compelling argument: information should be managed the way humans naturally think and work, not the way legacy systems have forced us to. That means:<br><br>🔦 Prioritising a single version of the truth to ensure accuracy and trust.<br>🔦Structuring information based on context and function, not rigid labels.<br>🔦Embracing ontologies over taxonomies, to enable cross-system, cross-disciplinary, and cross-cultural understanding.<br>🔦Reorienting our information systems away from silos and toward integrated, relational data ecosystems that support real-world human tasks and decision-making.<br><br>[Stephen Clarke MRIM](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/steffclarke_ontology-knowledgegraphs-ai-activity-7306893100281405442-PCuQ) On LinkedIn
