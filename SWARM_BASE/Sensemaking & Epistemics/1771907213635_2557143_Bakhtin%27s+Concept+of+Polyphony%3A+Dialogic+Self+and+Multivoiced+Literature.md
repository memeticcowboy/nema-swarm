# Bakhtin's Concept of Polyphony: Dialogic Self and Multivoiced Literature

Created at 2025/11/09 5:29 PM

Mikhail Bakhtin's concept of "polyphony," as developed in his work *Problems of Dostoevsky's Poetics*, articulates a radical reimagining of the self and consciousness as inherently dialogic. Bakhtin argues that the self is never a singular, isolated consciousness but is always constituted in dialogue with others. This means the psyche functions like a novel with multiple unmerged voices or archetypes, each fully valid and autonomous, engaged in a live, ongoing, and unresolved conversation. <br><br>In Bakhtin's analysis of Dostoevsky's novels, multiple independent voices coexist without being subordinated or merged into a single author's perspective. These voices—characters like Ivan, Alyosha, and the Grand Inquisitor in Dostoevsky’s works—represent different ideological truths (religious, nihilistic, ethical) that clash and coexist in tension rather than resolve into harmony. This "polyphony" is not about harmony but about the coexistence of diverse, often conflicting, truths. The self is thus a "plural constellation" or chorus, where each internal voice retains its legitimacy and independence, creating a dynamic and open-ended inner dialogue.<br><br>Bakhtin contrasts this polyphonic approach with monologism—the imposition of a single voice or truth—whether in novels or criticism. He critiques traditional monologic interpretation that seeks one authoritative meaning, favoring instead dialogic criticism that opens texts to multiple, interacting interpretations resembling a conversation rather than a verdict. Polyphony reveals consciousness and the social world as fundamentally dialogic, made up of many voices that shape identity and meaning through their interaction rather than through final synthesis or unity.<br><br>In essence, Bakhtin’s "polyphony" maps the psyche as a vibrant space where multiple voices continuously engage, conflict, and coexist, rejecting the notion of a unified, self-sufficient self in favor of a dynamic plurality of consciousnesses in dialogue.[1][5][6][8][10]<br><br>Sources<br>[1] Dialogism in Literature: Bakhtin, Polyphony, Power of Many Voices https://theliteraryscholar.com/dialogism-in-literature/<br>[2] Key Theories of Mikhail Bakhtin https://literariness.org/2018/01/24/key-theories-of-mikhail-bakhtin/<br>[3] Voices: Bakhtin's Heteroglossia and Polyphony, and the ... -
