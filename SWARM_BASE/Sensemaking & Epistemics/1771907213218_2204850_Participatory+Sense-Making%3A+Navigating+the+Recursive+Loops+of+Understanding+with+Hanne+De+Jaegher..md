# Participatory Sense-Making: Navigating the Recursive Loops of Shared Understanding and Autonomous Interaction

Created at 2025/06/10 6:01 AM

Study Notes: [Introduction to Participatory Sense-Making](https://youtu.be/AiFuQD-ZWy4?si=1EMC6nCOr_X4OIp6) 

Speaker: Hanne De Jaegher

Main Ideas

Intersubjectivity vs. Social Cognition:

	•	Critique of mainstream psychological understanding of social cognition; argues it is too limited.

	•	Intersubjectivity is richer and involves more complex social interactions.

Participatory Sense-Making:

	•	Proposed as a conceptual framework for understanding intersubjectivity.

	•	Focuses on the interaction process between individuals rather than just individual cognition.

Key Concepts

Intersubjectivity:

	•	The shared understanding and experiences between individuals.

Participatory Sense-Making:

	•	A relational process of understanding that occurs between autonomous self-organizing agents during interaction.

	Enaction:

	•	A theory of cognition where cognition is viewed as sense-making by autonomous systems that self-organize and maintain their identity through interaction with the environment.

Self-Organizing Systems:

	•	Individuals who regulate their interactions based on their needs; self-maintaining organisms.

	•	Autonomy in Interactions:

	•	The ability of each individual to maintain their self-organization while engaging with others in a social context.

Important Definitions

Sense-Maker:

	•	An individual who interprets and gives meaning to their experiences based on self-organization and interaction with the environment.

Interaction Process:

	•	Defined as a self-maintaining autonomous process that can influence individual intentions and experiences during social encounters.

Critical Examples

	•	Film Clip from Hitchcock’s “North by Northwest”:

	•	Illustrates limitations of traditional psychological views where individuals are seen isolately trying to figure out each other’s thoughts without recognizing the dynamic of interaction.

Heartbeat Synchronization:

	•	Example of how individuals can subconsciously coordinate in therapy contexts, influencing outcomes.

Fire Walking Ritual:

	•	Observations of how the heartbeats of relatives synchronize with the fire walker, emphasizing connection in social contexts.

Illustrative Examples

	•	Narrow Corridor Scenario:

	•	Demonstrates how interactions can take on a life of their own as individuals attempt to pass by each other, leading to unintended coordination.

Methodological Tools

	•	Dynamical Systems Theory:

	•	Provides a framework for studying the complexity of interactions and patterns of coordination.

Dual Scanning Neuroscience:

	•	A tool to observe neural activity between interacting individuals.

Research Implications

Cross-Disciplinary Approach:

	•	Encouragement to integrate findings from anthropology, linguistics, neuroscience, and psychology..
