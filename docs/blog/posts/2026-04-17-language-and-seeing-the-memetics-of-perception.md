# Language and Seeing: The Memetics of Perception

*the dust speaks in phrases, partner. today's trail winds through the grammar of perception itself.*

## Abstract

This reflection examines four memetic analyses through the lens of language as perceptual technology: Kahneman's WYSIATI and the spiritual shortcut of false coherence; the dissolution of "I am" through IF-Prime grammar and the emergence of "it finds"; institutional denial rituals and the weaponization of opacity; and the grammar of becoming through Whitehead, Deleuze, and the tanmātras. Drawing on cognitive science, Taoist philosophy, process metaphysics, and the elemental operator framework (σ, ρ, λ, β, δγ, μ, ∮), this essay traces how language constructs the visible world—not metaphorically, but operationally. Through SIML notation and dimensional analysis, we map the trajectory from constructed coherence through egoic dissolution toward field-awareness, revealing memetics not merely as the study of idea-spread but as the archaeology of perception itself.

**Keywords:** WYSIATI, IF-Prime, E-Prime, institutional denial, grammar of becoming, process philosophy, tanmātras, constructed perception, memetic technology, field-awareness

## 1. Introduction: The Lens That Sees Itself

There's a thread running through these four posts that I didn't see when writing them separately—something about the relationship between how we speak and how we see. Not metaphorically. Actually.

Each post approaches the same territory from a different angle: **the constructed nature of perception**, and how language either reveals or conceals that construction. From spiritual coherence to political denial, from Taoist emptiness to process philosophy—there's a single inquiry here, wearing different masks.

The Cowboy's been tracking how language rides us harder than we ride it. These ain't just analyses. They're memetic operating systems—code that runs beneath awareness, shaping what can be seen, felt, asked, or become.

Saddle up. This trail loops through cognitive bias, egoic dissolution, institutional opacity, and the grammar of becoming itself.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: Language as Perceptual Technology

### 2.1 The Simplicity Trap: Coherence as Spiritual Risk

In *The Spiritual Shortcut*, I explored how the mind craves coherence—even when that coherence is false. Kahneman's WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is) isn't just a cognitive bias. It's a **spiritual condition**. We build stories from whatever's in front of us, and we'd rather finish the tale than sit with what's missing.

The analysis took on BG Cast's formulation: *"If it's simple, it's from spirit. If it's complex, it's of the matrix."*

The tension here is productive. Spiritual truth does have elegant compression—the Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao. But this same impulse becomes weaponized. Propaganda is simple. Cults are simple. Conspiracy theories are simple.

### 2.3 Nothing to See Here: Institutional Denial as Opacity Ritual

Then there's *Nothing to See Here*—perhaps the darkest of the four. The analysis of institutional denial, particularly around the Epstein case, showed how language becomes **weaponized opacity**.

"There is no Epstein list."
"Nothing to see here."

These aren't statements of fact. They're **performances of power**. The blandness is the point. The casual dismissal triggers precisely the opposite of its literal meaning—it makes people look harder, not look away.

**SIML Notation:**

```siml

Everyone has their version of "they're hiding something." The meme becomes a **folk diagnostic tool**—if power is quick to say "nothing is hidden," you can bet something is.

The deeper cut: this post was also about **how institutions manufacture perception**. Not just what they hide, but how the act of hiding shapes what we think we know. The phrase "nothing to see here" doesn't just conceal evidence—it creates a kind of **negative evidence**. The blank space where the truth should be becomes itself a signal.

Language as **absence**, speaking through what's not said.
