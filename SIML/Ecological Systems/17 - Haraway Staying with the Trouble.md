# Donna Haraway's 'Staying with the Trouble': A Multispecies Approach to Ecological Crisis

Created at 2025/08/18 11:12 AM

Donna Haraway's concept of **"staying with the trouble,"** articulated in her 2016 book *Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene*, represents a revolutionary call to embrace the complexities of living on a damaged planet while rejecting simplistic solutions or escapist fantasies. This comprehensive framework offers an ethical and political response to the ecological, social, and political crises of our time, emphasizing collective creativity and multispecies collaboration over despair or technological fixes.[read.dukeupress+2](https://read.dukeupress.edu/books/book/27/Staying-with-the-TroubleMaking-Kin-in-the)

## What is Staying with the Trouble?

"Staying with the trouble" constitutes Haraway's fundamental ethical framework for engaging with contemporary crises—including climate change, mass extinctions, and systemic inequality—without resorting to despair, technofixes, or denial. At its core, the concept emphasizes **response-ability**, a term Haraway uses to denote the capacity to respond collectively and creatively to challenges while fostering multispecies connections and mutual flourishing.[wikipedia+3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staying_with_the_Trouble)

The framework operates through what Haraway calls **"tentacular thinking,"** a mode of cognition that embraces interconnectedness and rejects bounded individualism. As one scholar notes, **"everything's tentacles are in everything else, everything intertwines with everything else"**, capturing the essential understanding that humans cannot be thought of as disconnected from or dominant over the rest of life on earth.[westdenhaag+1](http://westdenhaag.nl/Harawiki/lib/exe/fetch.php?media=wiki%3Aarticle_9008783.pdf)

## Key Features of Haraway's Framework

## Rejecting the Anthropocene for the Chthulucene

Haraway critiques the term "Anthropocene" for its anthropocentric focus, proposing instead the **"Chthulucene"**—an epoch that highlights the **"tentacular"** interdependence of human and nonhuman beings. Drawing from Greek roots *khthon* (earth) and *kainos* (time), the Chthulucene encompasses **"diverse earth-wide tentacular powers and forces"** including figures like **"Naga, Gaia, Tangaroa, Terra, Spider Woman, Pachamama"** and many others.[vtechworks.lib.vt+2](https://vtechworks.lib.vt.edu/server/api/core/bitstreams/c5049841-0d0f-468c-adb7-00de7b1657a9/content)

Unlike the Anthropocene and Capitalocene narratives that **"end in double death"** and **"are not about ongoingness,"** the Chthulucene offers stories of **"ongoing multispecies stories and practices of becoming-with in times that remain at stake"**. As Haraway emphasizes, this temporal framework is **"made up of ongoing multispecies stories and practices of becoming-with in times that remain at stake, in precarious times, in which the world is not finished and the sky has not fallen—yet"**.[sciencetechnologystudies.journal+1](https://sciencetechnologystudies.journal.fi/article/download/63108/25011/)

## Sympoiesis: Making-With Rather Than Self-Making

Central to Haraway's framework is the concept of **sympoiesis**—literally meaning **"making-with"**. This contrasts with autopoiesis (self-making), emphasizing that **"nothing makes itself; nothing is really autopoietic or self-organizing"**. Sympoiesis represents **"collectively-producing systems that do not have self-defined spatial or temporal boundaries"** with **"information and control distributed among components"**.[libarynth+2](https://libarynth.org/staying_with_the_trouble)

The biological foundation for this concept draws from contemporary research on **holobionts**—symbiotic assemblages that reveal how what we consider individual organisms are actually complex ecosystems. As Haraway explains, **"holobionts require models tuned to an expandable number of quasi-collective/quasi-individual partners in constitutive relatings; these relationalities are the objects of study"**.[sydneyreviewofbooks](https://sydneyreviewofbooks.com/reviews/what-matters-staying-with-the-trouble-by-donna-haraway)

## Making Kin, Not Babies

Perhaps Haraway's most provocative proposal is her call to **"make kin, not babies"**. This involves **"making oddkin"**—forming non-biological, cross-species relationships that transcend traditional family structures to build solidarity across differences. As Haraway states, **"staying with the trouble requires making oddkin; that is, we require each other in unexpected collaborations and combinations, in hot compost piles"**.[read.dukeupress+3](https://read.dukeupress.edu/environmental-humanities/article/6/1/159/8110/Anthropocene-Capitalocene-Plantationocene)

The demographic dimension of this proposal envisions a **"sliding-scale approach to global reduction in human numbers"** where reproductive tokens would be distributed based on regional class status. While controversial, this reflects Haraway's broader argument that **"making many fewer new babies"** is an **"inescapable thread in the weave"** of multispecies environmental and reproductive justice.[read.dukeupress](https://read.dukeupress.edu/environmental-humanities/article/12/2/475/167000/Situated-Kinmaking-and-the-Population-Problem)

## Speculative Fabulation (SF)

Haraway employs **"SF"** as a multivalent practice encompassing **"science fiction, speculative fabulation, string figures, speculative feminism, science fact, so far"**. This approach uses storytelling, art, and interdisciplinary collaboration to imagine alternative futures. As she articulates, **"SF is storytelling and fact telling; it is the patterning of possible worlds and possible times, material-semiotic worlds, gone, here, and yet to come"**.[sobrief+4](https://sobrief.com/books/staying-with-the-trouble)

String figures serve as a particularly important metaphor within this framework, representing **"one kind of patterning for restoring hózhó"** (harmony, beauty, and right relations) and demonstrating how **"worlds of SF are not containers; they are patternings, risky co-makings, speculative fabulations"**.[markfoster+1](http://www.markfoster.net/dcf/speculative_fabulation.pdf)

## Contemporary Applications and Examples

## PigeonBlog: Interspecies Environmental Justice

One of the most compelling examples Haraway cites is [Beatriz da Costa's](https://expandedenvironment.org/beatriz-da-costa/) **PigeonBlog** project (2006-2008). This pioneering work equipped homing pigeons with miniature air pollution sensors to collect and disseminate real-time air quality data, demonstrating genuine interspecies collaboration for environmental justice. Recently reactivated in 2024 as part of the PST ART exhibition, the project continues to showcase how **"art offers a way of generating attention, a way of disturbing things"** in environmental activism.[nearfuturelaboratory+3](https://nearfuturelaboratory.com/blog/2024/12/beatriz-da-costa-pigeon-blog/)

## Crochet Coral Reef: Collective Art-Science Practice

The **[Crochet Coral Reef](https://crochetcoralreef.org/)** project by Margaret and Christine Wertheim exemplifies Haraway's vision of collaborative multispecies response-ability. This ongoing initiative creates large-scale coralline landscapes using crochet techniques that mathematically replicate hyperbolic geometry found in actual coral organisms. With 52 community-based **"Satellite Reefs"** created worldwide, the project demonstrates how **"a livable world must be composed collectively bit by bit, or not at all"**.[madmuseum+5](https://madmuseum.org/exhibition/crochet-coral-reef-toxic-seas)

As Haraway herself notes about the project, the **"Crochet Coral Reef risks making real and fabulated things together to open up still-possible times for flourishing ... Palpable, polymorphous, terrifying and inspiring stitchery"**.[crochetcoralreef](https://crochetcoralreef.org/)

## The Camille Stories: Multispecies Futures

Perhaps Haraway's most speculative contribution is **"The Camille Stories"**—a collaborative narrative exploring five generations of human-butterfly symbionts from 2025 to 2425. These stories imagine **"symbiogenetic communities"** where children are genetically bonded with endangered species as a practice of **"repairing damaged places and making flourishing multispecies futures"**.[projects.harvard+4](https://projects.iq.harvard.edu/files/retreat/files/haraway_camillestories.pdf)

In this speculative fabulation, the first Camille is born in 2025 when Earth's population reaches eight billion, while the fifth Camille dies in 2425 when the population has stabilized at three billion. The stories demonstrate how **"taking symbiosis with another animal as a practice of repairing damaged places and making flourishing multispecies futures"** might work in practice.[necrocenenecrolandscaping.wordpress+2](https://necrocenenecrolandscaping.files.wordpress.com/2018/01/haraway_camillestories-min.pdf)

## Critical Perspectives and Challenges

## Accessibility and Language

Critics note that Haraway's **"dense, playful language"** including neologisms like **"sympoiesis"** and **"Chthulucene"** can be challenging, though some argue it's more accessible than her earlier works. As one reader observes, much of the difficulty stems from Haraway's attempt to **"break out from some of the most deadly habits of Euro-American thinking"** through linguistic innovation.[goodreads+1](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/28369185-staying-with-the-trouble)

## Reproductive Politics and Justice

The **"make kin, not babies"** proposition has generated significant scholarly debate. Critics argue it potentially places **"unnecessary and disproportionately distributed constraints on reproductive freedoms and bodily sovereignty"**. Scholars like Kathryn Dow note that **"both antinatalism and pronatalism lead to different lives being valued differentially"** and are **"anathema to reproductive justice"**.[read.dukeupress](https://read.dukeupress.edu/environmental-humanities/article/12/2/475/167000/Situated-Kinmaking-and-the-Population-Problem)

## Practical Implementation

While Haraway's speculative approach inspires creative thinking, critics question whether it provides **"concrete strategies for systemic change"**. The emphasis on individual decision-making regarding reproduction may inadequately address the **"inequalities and infrastructures that make reproduction (im)possible"**.[goodreads+1](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/28369185-staying-with-the-trouble)

## Contemporary Relevance and Applications

Haraway's framework remains influential across environmental humanities, feminist theory, and activism. Recent applications include climate justice movements, Indigenous knowledge systems, and digital communities where collaborative storytelling and multispecies ethics challenge capitalist and anthropocentric norms.[europenowjournal+1](https://www.europenowjournal.org/2018/03/22/staying-with-the-trouble-making-kin-in-the-chthulucene/)

The COVID-19 pandemic has particularly highlighted the relevance of **"staying with the trouble"** as communities worldwide have had to develop new forms of solidarity and care while navigating unprecedented global disruption. Digital gatherings and virtual communities have created new forms of **"making kin"** that echo Haraway's vision, though they complicate the embodied, place-based quality she originally emphasized.[reddit+2](https://www.reddit.com/r/CriticalTheory/comments/jkuh37/can_someone_help_me_understand_haraways/)

## Key Theoretical Contributions

## Response-ability and Ethics

Haraway's concept of **response-ability** offers a sophisticated framework for environmental ethics that moves beyond individual responsibility toward collective, multispecies accountability. As she explains, this involves **"taking moral responsibility for the world's interconnected troubles, working collaboratively with humans and nonhumans"**.[brooklynrail+2](https://brooklynrail.org/2017/12/art/DONNA-HARAWAY-with-Thyrza-Nichols-Goodeve/)

## Multispecies Feminism

The framework significantly advances feminist theory by extending kinship beyond human relationships to include multispecies assemblages. This challenges traditional notions of reproduction, family, and care while creating space for more inclusive definitions of flourishing.[read.dukeupress+2](https://read.dukeupress.edu/environmental-humanities/article/6/1/159/8110/Anthropocene-Capitalocene-Plantationocene)

## Temporal Complexity

The Chthulucene concept offers an alternative temporality that resists both apocalyptic despair and techno-optimistic salvation narratives. Instead, it emphasizes **"polytemporal, polyspatial knottings"** that acknowledge both damage and possibility.[libarynth+2](https://libarynth.org/staying_with_the_trouble)

Haraway's **"staying with the trouble"** ultimately offers a radical reimagining of human-nonhuman relationships for the twenty-first century. By rejecting both despair and false hope, her framework provides tools for collective action that acknowledge complicity while working toward multispecies flourishing. The concept continues to influence contemporary scholarship and activism, offering essential insights for navigating our current planetary crisis through collaborative creativity rather than technological domination or apocalyptic resignation.
