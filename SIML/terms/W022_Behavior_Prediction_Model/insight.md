# Latent Inference Budget Model

## Source
MIT researchers developed the 'latent inference budget model' to predict human behavior by accounting for computational constraints and sub-optimal decision-making.

## Core Insight
Traditional models of human behavior assume rationality— that humans make optimal decisions given available information. The latent inference budget model inverts this assumption: it treats sub-optimal decision-making not as noise to be filtered out, but as signal to be decoded. By inferring the computational constraints (time pressure, cognitive load, information limits) that lead to imperfect choices, the model achieves superior predictive accuracy.

## Key Mechanism
The model learns to predict not just what humans *should* do, but what they *will* do given their inference budget—the finite cognitive resources available for decision-making. This budget varies by individual, context, and moment, creating predictable patterns of "mistakes" that are actually rational responses to constraint.

## Applications
- **AI Assistants**: Predicting when users are about to make errors and intervening proactively
- **Robotic Helpers**: Adapting to individual decision-making styles and cognitive limitations
- **Behavioral Prediction**: Forecasting human actions in complex, time-constrained environments

## Ethical Dimensions
The model raises fundamental questions about autonomy and manipulation:
- **Enhancement vs. Control**: Does predicting errors to prevent them enhance human agency or constrain it?
- **Transparency**: Should humans know when AI is modeling their cognitive limitations?
- **Power Asymmetry**: Who controls the predictive models, and to what ends?

## Connection to Critical Thinking
This model embodies a metacognitive stance toward human cognition—it treats thinking itself as a bounded process subject to analysis and prediction. For critical thinking, this suggests:
1. Awareness of one's own inference budget is essential for self-correction
2. Sub-optimal decisions often reflect legitimate constraints, not mere failure
3. Predictive models of cognition can be tools for enhancement or instruments of control

## Research Significance
Developed collaboratively by MIT and University of Washington, the model outperforms older approaches in predicting both human and machine behavior. It represents a shift from normative models (how agents should act) to descriptive models (how agents do act given constraints)—a shift with profound implications for AI-human collaboration.
