# Lacan's Mirror Stage and Desire
## SIML Term Entry S017
### Source: 1771907212412_264798_Lacan%27s+Mirror+Stage+and+Desire+Theory.md

## Overview

Lacan's mirror stage describes the foundational moment in psychic development when the infant, between six and eighteen months, encounters its own reflection and identifies with it as a complete, unified self. This identification is simultaneously a misidentification: the infant's actual bodily experience is one of fragmentation, motor incoordination, and dependence, yet the mirror image presents a coherent, bounded, autonomous figure. The ego is thus born from a fundamental misrecognition — the subject takes itself to be something it is not, and this imaginary wholeness becomes the template for all subsequent identity formation.

This originary misrecognition creates the structural foundation for Lacan's theory of desire and lack. Because the ego is constituted through an image that promises a completeness the subject never actually possesses, desire becomes the permanent condition of subjectivity. The subject is always seeking to recover or achieve a wholeness that was never real to begin with. Unconscious desires are shaped by society, culture, and the Symbolic order, but their underlying structure is the same: desire is always desire for what one lacks, directed toward external objects, others, and ideals that promise but never deliver fulfillment. This persistent incompleteness is not a failure of the subject but its constitutive condition — the subject is its lack.

## Key Tensions

- **Self ↔ Reflection**: Experiencing subject vs. its imaginary double in the mirror
- **Desire ↔ Lack**: Perpetual seeking vs. constitutive absence that drives it
- **Recognition ↔ Misrecognition**: Identification with the image as simultaneously a fundamental error
- **Completeness ↔ Incompleteness**: Promised wholeness vs. structural impossibility of fulfillment
- **Image ↔ Identity**: Mirror reflection as both foundation and prison of ego formation
