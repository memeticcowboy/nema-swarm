# Insight — Distributed Cognition Citadel Model (S0C8)

## Overview

The Western world largely operates under what Tanya Luhrmann calls the "citadel model" of mind — the assumption that the mind is a private, isolated fortress located inside the skull, fundamentally separate from other minds and the world. This model, with roots in ancient Greek philosophy, Reformation theology, and Enlightenment rationalism, treats thinking as something that happens inside individuals. Distributed cognition research reveals a radically different picture: intelligence is spread across systems of people, tools, and environments. Edwin Hutchins' landmark study of naval navigation showed that no single person "navigated" — the cognitive system included the entire team and their instruments operating together.

Luhrmann's cross-cultural research in the United States, Ghana, and India reveals that the citadel model is culturally specific, not universal. In Ghana, a cosmocentric model treats the mind as open to spiritual forces and community influence. In India, a sociocentric model defines the self primarily through relationships rather than internal states. Crucially, these different models have measurable consequences for mental health: the citadel model's emphasis on isolation and self-sufficiency correlates with higher rates of loneliness, anxiety, and depression in Western populations.

The historical dimension is equally significant. The citadel model was not merely developed in the West but actively exported through colonialism, religious conversion, and contemporary cultural hegemony. As Western materialist worldviews spread globally, they overwrite indigenous cognitive ecologies that may have been better adapted to human flourishing in relational contexts. Understanding this history transforms the question from "which model is correct?" to "which model serves human well-being in which contexts?"

## Key Tensions

1. **Distributed vs. isolated mind** — Intelligence spread across systems vs. mind as private fortress
2. **Western citadel vs. cosmocentric/sociocentric models** — Isolated self vs. relationally embedded self
3. **Individual cognition vs. systemic intelligence** — Thinking as private act vs. thinking as distributed process
4. **Privacy vs. interconnection** — Protected interiority vs. permeable boundaries with community and environment
5. **Cultural model vs. mental health** — How models of mind actively shape psychological outcomes across cultures
