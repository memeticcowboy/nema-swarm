# Insight — Neurodivergence Memetic Framework (S11D)

## Overview

Sher Griffin's Neurodivergence Memetic Framework applies poststructural and ecological lenses to the cultural understanding of cognitive difference. At its core sits a single potent meme: "Cognition is an Ecosystem." This reframes neurodivergent minds not as broken versions of a neurotypical standard but as ecological variations — different species in a cognitive forest, each contributing unique adaptive capacities to the collective.

The framework performs memetic analysis on the neurodivergence discourse itself, identifying how the ecological meme propagates through emotional hooks (felt recognition, relief from deficit framing), adjacent memeplexes (disability justice, biodiversity ethics, systems ecology), and cultural vectors (online advocacy communities, academic poststructuralism, therapeutic paradigm shifts). Griffin demonstrates that the shift from medical model to ecological model is not merely an argument but a memetic event — a self-propagating pattern that rewires perception once internalized.

## Key Tensions

- **Deficit model vs. ecological model**: The central axis of the framework. The medical model locates the "problem" in the individual brain; the ecological model distributes cognitive function across a system where variation is feature, not bug.
- **Individual pathology vs. systemic variation**: Diagnosis implies something wrong with the person. Ecological framing implies the environment may be poorly matched to the organism — the tension between "fix the person" and "redesign the niche."
- **Normalcy vs. cognitive biodiversity**: The statistical concept of neurotypicality as norm versus the ecological concept of diversity as strength. Monocultures are efficient but fragile; diverse ecosystems are resilient.
- **Medical intervention vs. environmental design**: If cognition is ecological, the intervention point shifts from pharmacology and behavioral correction to environmental design, accommodation, and systemic flexibility.
