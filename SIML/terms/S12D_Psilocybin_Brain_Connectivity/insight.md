# Insight — Psilocybin Brain Connectivity (S12D)

## Overview

This entry encodes research that applies algebraic topology — specifically homological scaffolds and persistent homology — to the study of brain functional connectivity under psilocybin. Traditional neuroimaging analyses focus on pairwise correlations between brain regions, but topological methods capture higher-order structural features of network organization: loops, cavities, and persistent features across scales. The key finding is that psilocybin induces brain states that are topologically more diverse but less stable than placebo states. The brain under psilocybin explores a wider space of connectivity configurations, forming transient bridges between normally segregated networks. This mathematical approach opens new dimensions for understanding psychedelic-altered consciousness.

## Key Tensions

- **Stable brain states vs. diverse topology**: Psilocybin trades stability for diversity. The brain accesses more topological configurations but holds none of them stably. Whether this instability is therapeutically productive or merely disorganizing remains debated.
- **Placebo baseline vs. psychedelic expansion**: The placebo state provides the reference topology against which psilocybin effects are measured. The question is whether the baseline is a "default" or simply one stable attractor among many.
- **Mathematical modeling vs. subjective experience**: Persistent homology captures structural features of network organization but says nothing directly about what it feels like to be in an altered state. Bridging the topological and the phenomenological remains a challenge.
- **Controlled structure vs. emergent connectivity**: The default mode network provides organized, hierarchical brain function. Psilocybin disrupts this control, and the resulting emergent connectivity may be creative, therapeutic, or simply chaotic.
