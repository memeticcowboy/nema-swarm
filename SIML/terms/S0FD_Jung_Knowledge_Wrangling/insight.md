# Insight — Jung on Knowledge Wrangling (S0FD)

## Overview

This entry draws on Carl Jung's perspective to articulate a fundamental distinction between knowledge management and knowledge wrangling. Knowledge management operates through capturing, organizing, and distributing knowledge as a controlled resource — centralized, systematized, treated as an asset within institutional frameworks. Knowledge wrangling, by contrast, engages with ideas as autonomous entities that capture human attention and imagination, recognizing that the relationship between humans and ideas is not one of mastery but of mutual engagement.

Jung's insight inverts the conventional assumption: ideas are not passive objects that humans manage, but active agents that capture people's imagination and drive innovation through collaboration and interaction. This is not metaphorical embellishment but a substantive claim about the dynamics of knowledge. Ideas combine, mutate, propagate, and attract attention according to dynamics that exceed any individual's or institution's control. The management paradigm, built on assumptions of capture and control, systematically misunderstands this autonomy.

The practical implication is that knowledge systems should be designed for wrangling rather than management — creating conditions where ideas can interact, evolve, and find their hosts rather than attempting to contain and distribute them through centralized channels. This positions knowledge as a dynamic organic process rather than a static resource, and innovation as an emergent property of collaborative engagement with autonomous ideas rather than a product of systematic management.

## Key Tensions

1. **Management vs. wrangling** — Capturing and controlling knowledge as resource versus engaging with ideas as autonomous entities that capture human imagination
2. **Controlled resource vs. autonomous entity** — Ideas as passive assets within management systems versus ideas as active agents with their own trajectories and attractions
3. **Centralized authority vs. emergent collaboration** — Top-down knowledge distribution versus organic innovation through collaborative interaction
4. **Static organization vs. dynamic evolution** — Systematic, fixed knowledge structures versus living, evolving processes of idea mutation and propagation
5. **Human as knowledge carrier vs. idea as active agent** — Anthropocentric mastery over ideas versus Jungian recognition that ideas possess and direct human attention
