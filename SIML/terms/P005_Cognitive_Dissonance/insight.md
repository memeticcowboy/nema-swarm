## 6. Insight Generation

### 6.1 Core Question

How does the discomfort of inconsistency drive belief and behavior change?

### 6.2 Hypothesis

Cognitive Dissonance Theory reveals that the mind is not a neutral information processor but a **coherence-seeking system**. The discomfort of inconsistency is not merely unpleasant—it is a motivational force that drives learning, change, or creative rationalization. The key insight is that **dissonance is energy**—it can be channeled toward growth (belief/behavior change) or dissipated through defense (rationalization, denial).

### 6.3 One-Sentence Insight

> Festinger's Cognitive Dissonance Theory reveals that the mind is a coherence-seeking system where the discomfort of inconsistency serves as motivational energy—driving either genuine transformation through belief/behavior change or defensive self-deception through rationalization, depending on the context and resources available.

### 6.4 Three-Bullet Expansion

- **What:** A theory of psychological tension arising from inconsistent cognitions (beliefs, behaviors, values), creating a motivational drive to restore consistency through multiple resolution strategies
- **How it works:** Inconsistency detected → psychological discomfort aroused → drive to reduce dissonance activated → resolution strategy selected (change belief, change behavior, add consonant cognitions, reduce importance) → consonance restored
- **Where it aims:** Toward cognitive consonance and psychological harmony—though the path taken determines whether the result is growth (belief/behavior alignment with reality) or defensive maintenance (rationalization protecting existing beliefs)
