## Etymology
From Latin "Magna Carta" (Great Charter), the 1215 charter of English liberties; "rebellion" from Latin "rebellio" meaning "renewal of war." Article 61 specifically established the constitutional right of barons to lawfully rebel against the crown when governance violated agreed-upon rights.

## Commentary
The Magna Carta's Article 61 represents a radical memetic innovation: the institutionalization of resistance. Unlike modern conceptions of revolution as extra-legal, this framework embeds rebellion within the legal structure itself. The concept of "lawful rebellion" creates a strange loop where the system contains the seeds of its own legitimate dissolution.

This maps onto contemporary discussions of AI governance, corporate accountability, and digital rights. As Huxley's "Brave New World" illustrates, control through pleasure and distraction may prove more durable than overt oppression—making the question of when and how to resist increasingly complex. The Magna Carta tradition suggests that resistance must itself be structured, principled, and grounded in prior agreement rather than mere outrage.

The modern interpretation of Article 61 as a living right (rather than historical curiosity) demonstrates how memes persist and mutate across centuries, finding new hosts in contemporary political movements.
