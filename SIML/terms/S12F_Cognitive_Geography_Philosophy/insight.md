# Insight — Cognitive Geography Philosophy (S12F)

## Overview

Cognitive geography examines how minds represent space — the mental maps, spatial distortions, and place-based cognition through which humans navigate and understand their environments. The philosophical analysis of cognitive geography elevates this inquiry from empirical psychology to metaphysics, epistemology, and political theory. Constructivist metaphysics proposes that cognitive space is not merely an approximation of objective geography but is ontologically significant in its own right — mental maps do not just represent reality but participate in constituting the spatial reality experienced by cognitive agents.

The study reveals that spatial distortions in mental maps are not simple errors to be corrected but windows onto deeper assumptions about reality. How individuals and cultures represent distance, direction, and place reflects metaphysical commitments about the nature of space, epistemological assumptions about how space can be known, and political implications about whose spatial knowledge counts. The tension between subjective spatial cognition and objective geography is irreducible — neither can be fully absorbed into the other.

This philosophical analysis also exposes a tension between different epistemological approaches: empirical psychology with its controlled studies of spatial cognition, and phenomenological and indigenous traditions that ground spatial knowledge in lived, embodied, culturally situated experience. The political implications are substantial — cognitive geography shapes territorial claims, resource distribution, border perception, and the lived experience of belonging and displacement.

## Key Tensions

1. **Subjective spatial cognition vs. objective geography** — Mental maps as ontologically significant constructions versus geographic space as objectively given and independently existing
2. **Empirical psychology vs. phenomenological/indigenous knowledge** — Laboratory studies of spatial cognition versus lived, embodied, culturally situated ways of knowing space
3. **Individual cognitive maps vs. collective spatial understanding** — Personal mental geographies versus shared cultural and political mappings that shape and constrain individual spatial cognition
4. **Constructivist metaphysics vs. realist cartography** — Cognitive space as ontologically constitutive versus physical geography as the ultimate ground of spatial truth
5. **Spatial distortion as error vs. spatial distortion as revelation** — Mental map inaccuracies as failures of cognition versus windows onto deeper metaphysical and political commitments
