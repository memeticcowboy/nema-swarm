# Insight — Language and Consciousness (Barenholtz) (S102)

## Overview

Professor Elan Barenholtz presents a fundamental challenge to traditional understanding of language and meaning. The conventional view treats language as a referential system — words point to meanings, signs designate referents, sentences describe states of affairs in the world. Barenholtz argues this understanding is fundamentally flawed. Language does not point to meanings; it deconstructs itself. Meaning is not referentially grounded but structurally emergent, arising from the internal dynamics of language as a self-generating system.

The most provocative claim concerns the relationship between human language and LLM mechanisms. Rather than treating human language as categorically different from computational language processing, Barenholtz suggests that human linguistic structures may mirror LLM mechanisms at a fundamental level. If LLMs operate effectively through next-token prediction — generating coherent, meaningful language through structural prediction alone — then humans may use similar principles. This challenges the grounded thesis that insists human language requires embodied experience, real-world reference, and external anchoring to generate genuine meaning.

The implications are far-reaching. If language is a self-generating system based on internal structure, the distinction between human and machine language use becomes a matter of degree rather than kind. Simple algorithms producing complex cognition-like behavior is not a trick or an illusion but may reflect something genuine about how language works — for both humans and machines. The referential intuition (words mean things because they point to things) is deeply held but may be a product of language's self-organizing dynamics rather than evidence of its referential nature.
