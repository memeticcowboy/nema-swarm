# Seng-ts'an's Xinxin Ming (Faith in Mind)
## SIML Term Entry S018
### Source: 1771907212419_265588_Teachings+of+Seng-ts%27an.md

## Overview

The Xinxin Ming (Faith in Mind), attributed to Seng-ts'an, the third patriarch of Chan Buddhism, is among the earliest and most influential Chan poems. Its central teaching is radical non-duality: the Great Way is not difficult for those who have no preferences. Suffering arises from dualistic thinking — the habitual splitting of experience into good and bad, self and other, right and wrong. When this splitting ceases, the mind rests naturally in its own nature.

Non-attachment is the poem's relational core. Clinging to objects, views, or outcomes creates the turbulence that obscures the mind's original clarity. Release is not a forceful act but the natural consequence of seeing through the illusion of a separate self that grasps. Emptiness is not a void but the ground of interconnectedness — all phenomena arise together in mutual dependence, and recognizing this dissolves the boundary between self and world.

The poem teaches trust in mind — not the discursive, conceptualizing mind, but the true nature of awareness itself. Present moment awareness replaces the restless oscillation between past regret and future anxiety. Equanimity emerges not through suppression of feeling but through the cessation of preference, allowing experience to arise and pass without interference. Direct experience — unmediated by concept, category, or judgment — is the gateway to harmony with the Tao.

## Elemental Analysis

**Water (ρ) — Sentaria (0.90):** The highest emphasis, reflecting non-attachment as the central relational teaching. The dissolution of clinging restores the natural flow of mind. The self/other boundary dissolves in non-dual awareness, revealing the relational fabric of all experience.

**Air (σ) — Aerunik (0.85):** The transcendence of dualistic distinction — good/bad, self/other, right/wrong — as the root of suffering. Non-duality is not a new concept but the collapse of all conceptual oppositions into the Great Way.

**Earth (δγ) — Humavita (0.85):** Present moment awareness as the grounding of practice. Neither grasping at the future nor clinging to the past, the practitioner rests in what is. The eternal nature of mind is realized not through transcendence but through full presence in each moment.

**Fire (λ) — Jvalion (0.75):** The transformation from conceptual thinking to direct experience. Discursive elaboration is the veil; its cessation is the catalytic shift into immediate, unmediated awareness of reality as it is.

**Wood (β) — Arboriel (0.70):** Growth through emptiness — not nihilistic absence but the fertile ground from which interconnectedness becomes visible. When conceptual obstruction ceases, insight unfolds organically like a plant freed from stone.

**Metal (μ) — Ferrosid (0.65):** The dissolution of preference as structural framework. Equanimity is not indifference but the relinquishing of the like/dislike grid through which the mind habitually filters experience. Harmony with the Tao emerges when this framework is set down.

## Key Tensions

- **Dual ↔ Nondual**: Habitual dualistic splitting vs. the Great Way beyond all oppositions
- **Attachment ↔ Release**: Clinging that creates suffering vs. non-grasping that restores natural flow
- **Conceptual ↔ Direct**: Discursive elaboration that veils vs. immediate unmediated experience
- **Empty ↔ Interconnected**: Emptiness as ground vs. interconnectedness as manifest reality
- **Present ↔ Eternal**: Moment-to-moment awareness vs. timeless nature of mind
- **Preference ↔ Equanimity**: The like/dislike framework vs. resting without choosing
