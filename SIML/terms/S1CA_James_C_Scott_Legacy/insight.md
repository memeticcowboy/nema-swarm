# Insight — James C. Scott: Agrarian Studies Legacy (S1CA)

## Overview
James C. Scott (1936-2024) was a political scientist whose landmark works challenged the assumption that state legibility equals progress. "Seeing Like a State" revealed how high-modernist schemes fail when they override local knowledge (metis) with administrative simplification. "Weapons of the Weak" demonstrated how peasant resistance operates through everyday acts rather than organized revolt. "The Moral Economy of the Peasant" showed how tacit reciprocal norms bound exploitation.

His intellectual legacy centers on the legibility-illegibility distinction: states simplify populations through standardization and cadastral mapping to make them governable, but this simplification destroys the local complexity that makes systems actually work. The political power of illegibility lies in resistance to administrative capture.

Beyond scholarship, Scott's legacy includes his founding of the Program in Agrarian Studies at Yale, creating inclusive interdisciplinary communities. He is remembered for exceptional mentorship, generosity, and the ability to connect across diverse backgrounds, embodying the values his scholarship advocated.

## Key Tensions
1. **Legibility vs Illegibility** — State simplification versus local complexity.
2. **Centralized Planning vs Local Knowledge** — High-modernist schemes versus metis.
3. **Organized Revolt vs Everyday Resistance** — Visible politics versus weapons of the weak.
4. **Administrative Categories vs Lived Complexity** — Governance simplification versus actual functioning.
