## 6. Insight Generation

### 6.1 Core Question

How does learning change when knowledge is distributed across networks rather than contained in individual minds?

### 6.2 Hypothesis

Connectivism reveals that **knowledge is ecological** — it lives in the relationships between nodes, not in the nodes themselves. In a world of information abundance, the critical skills become: finding relevant nodes, filtering signal from noise, maintaining connections, and recognizing patterns across distributed sources. Learning is no longer acquisition but navigation; knowing is no longer possession but connection.

### 6.3 One-Sentence Insight

> Connectivism reveals that in a world of information abundance, learning is not about what you know but about how well you're connected — knowledge lives in the network, and the capacity to navigate, filter, and synthesize across distributed nodes matters more than memorized facts.

### 6.4 Three-Bullet Expansion

- **What:** A learning theory for the digital age that views knowledge as distributed across networks of connections (people, devices, organizations) rather than residing solely in individual minds
- **How it works:** Learners aggregate information from diverse nodes, remix and repurpose it for their context, share back to the network, and receive feedback — with pattern recognition and connection maintenance as core skills
- **Where it aims:** Toward networked literacy and distributed cognition — the capacity to navigate information abundance, maintain currency in rapidly changing fields, and leverage technology as an extension of cognitive capability
