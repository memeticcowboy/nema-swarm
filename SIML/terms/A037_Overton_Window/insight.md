## CONTEXT & APPLICATION

**Origin:** Named after Joseph P. Overton of the Mackinac Center for Public Policy, who observed that politicians typically stay within the range of acceptable ideas rather than advocating for positions outside it. The concept describes not what politicians *prefer* but what they can *advocate* while maintaining electoral viability.

**Window Zones (from center to edge):**
- **Policy:** Currently implemented
- **Popular:** Widely supported, politically viable
- **Sensible:** Acceptable for mainstream debate
- **Acceptable:** Tolerated but not actively promoted
- **Radical:** Outside acceptable discourse
- **Unthinkable:** Beyond the pale, not to be discussed

**Mechanisms of Window Shift:**
- **Gradual Advocacy:** Sustained pressure moving ideas from radical → acceptable → sensible
- **Crisis Events:** Disruptions that rapidly redefine what is thinkable
- **Generational Change:** New cohorts with different baseline assumptions
- **Elite Signaling:** Authority figures legitimizing previously fringe positions

**In AI Contexts:**
- Models may present current window boundaries as "neutral" or "objective"
- Training data encodes historical window positions, potentially lagging shifts
- "Safety" frameworks may enforce current window as permanent boundary
- Risk: AI systems becoming active enforcers of discourse limits

**Related Terms:**
- Spiral of Silence (self-censorship of minority views)
- Framing Effects (how presentation shapes acceptability)
- Norm Persistence (resistance to discourse change)
- Cultural Hegemony (dominant ideology presenting itself as common sense)
