# Insight — Butler Mourning Political Resistance (S149)

## Overview

Judith Butler's reframing of mourning as political resistance begins with a deceptively simple question: whose deaths count as losses? The answer reveals the deep structure of political power. Selective grievability is not accidental but architectonic — it encodes hierarchies of recognition that determine whose lives are apprehended as livable and whose deaths register as grievable. When certain populations are rendered ungrievable — their deaths unacknowledged, their losses unmourned — this is not merely an oversight but an active operation of power that sustains the conditions for violence against them.

Butler's intervention transforms mourning from private emotion into political act. When grief enters the public sphere — when the ungrieved are mourned publicly, when the foreclosed are recognized — it challenges the normative frames that determine whose lives matter. This is not sentimental inclusivity but structural transformation. Public mourning becomes a resource for political resistance precisely because it demands recognition for those the political order has rendered invisible. The link to nonviolence is essential: shared vulnerability and interdependence, revealed through grief, become the ground for an ethics that refuses to authorize violence through selective grievability.

The critique of state manipulation of grief is equally central. States instrumentalize mourning to justify militaristic and nationalist agendas — grieving "our" dead to authorize killing "theirs." This capture of grief reinforces the very hierarchies of recognition that determine who is grievable. Resistant mourning practices disrupt this cycle by insisting on grieving across the boundaries the state enforces, by expanding the circle of recognizable lives, and by grounding political community in shared precarity rather than shared identity.
