# Insight — Self-Cognition in Sense-Making (S0FA)

## Overview

This entry examines whether the self — understood as identity construct — serves as an ideal vector for cognition in sense-making processes. The inquiry challenges the assumption that individual selfhood is the natural or optimal locus of cognitive activity, particularly within participatory sense-making frameworks. The self's static nature, its overemphasis on individuality, its illusion of autonomy, and its susceptibility to cognitive bias all suggest that it may limit the fluidity required for genuine intersubjective engagement.

Counter-arguments provide important ballast: the self as necessary anchor for cognitive coherence, the rational actor model's empirical traction, documented support for individual cognition as a real and measurable phenomenon, and the evolutionary survival value of self-differentiation. These are not trivially dismissed — the self clearly serves functional purposes in navigation, decision-making, and social coordination.

The synthesis proposes self as lens or filter rather than engine of cognition. Self shapes perception and channels attention without being the primary source of understanding. In participatory sense-making, the self is functional but not foundational — a useful instrument within a larger relational process. This reframing avoids both the trap of dissolving selfhood entirely and the trap of enshrining it as the sovereign seat of cognition.

## Key Tensions

1. **Static self vs. dynamic interaction** — Fixed identity construct versus the fluid, responsive engagement required by participatory sense-making
2. **Individual perspective vs. intersubjective space** — Self-contained cognitive viewpoint versus the shared relational field where meaning emerges between participants
3. **Autonomy illusion vs. interdependence** — The felt sense of autonomous selfhood versus the recognition that cognition is fundamentally relational and co-constituted
4. **Self as anchor vs. self as filter** — Self as necessary foundation for cognitive coherence versus self as permeable lens shaping but not determining understanding
5. **Individual cognition vs. relational emergence** — Empirically supported individual cognitive processes versus participatory meaning-making that exceeds any single self
