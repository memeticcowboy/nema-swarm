## Overview

The Nested Model of Well-Being proposes that human flourishing cannot be adequately understood through any single dimension. Instead, well-being comprises multiple interconnected domains — subjective experiences (happiness, life satisfaction, sense of purpose), health and functioning (physical and mental health, functional capacity), environmental context (material conditions, social environment, access to resources), and evaluative values (the criteria by which individuals and cultures judge a good life). These domains do not operate independently but are nested within one another, each influencing and being influenced by the others.

The nesting metaphor is significant. It conveys that subjective experience occurs within a body (health), which exists within an environment (context), which is interpreted through values (evaluation). Interventions targeting one layer without addressing the others tend to produce limited or temporary effects. A person whose subjective happiness is high but whose health is deteriorating exists in an unstable configuration; similarly, excellent health in a hostile environment does not constitute flourishing. The model demands integrated assessment.

Applications extend to both research and practice. In research, the nested model challenges single-variable studies of well-being and advocates for multi-domain assessment instruments that capture the relationships between levels. In practice, it informs intervention design — effective programs must address multiple nested domains rather than optimizing a single variable. The framework provides a comprehensive map of human flourishing that respects the complexity of lived experience.

Many well-being frameworks privilege one side — either emphasizing subjective reports (how people feel) or objective indicators (income, health metrics). ## Key Tensions

- **Subjective Experience vs Objective Conditions**: The persistent tension between privileging how people feel about their lives versus measuring the material and health conditions they actually face, and the nested model's insistence that both are necessary.
- **Domain Independence vs Domain Interconnection**: The methodological tension between studying well-being domains in isolation (for analytical clarity) and studying them as interconnected nested systems (for ecological validity).
- **Individual Flourishing vs Environmental Constraint**: The tension between individual-level well-being interventions and the recognition that environmental context — material conditions, social structures, cultural values — constrains and shapes individual flourishing in ways that individual-level interventions cannot fully address.

## Source Context

The source presents a framework for understanding human flourishing that integrates subjective experiences, health and functioning, environmental context, and evaluative values. It emphasizes the interconnectedness of well-being domains, their nested mutual influence, and applications in both research methodology and practical intervention design.
