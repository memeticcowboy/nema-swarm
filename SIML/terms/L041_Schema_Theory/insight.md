## 6. Insight Generation

### 6.1 Core Question

How does the organization of prior knowledge shape what we can learn and remember?

### 6.2 Hypothesis

Schemas are the **fundamental units of cognition** — structured mental frameworks that organize knowledge into coherent patterns. They function as both lenses (enabling rapid interpretation) and filters (constraining what can be perceived). Learning occurs not through information accumulation but through schema construction, elaboration, and transformation. The paradox of schemas is that they are simultaneously essential for understanding and limiting in their rigidity — requiring the dynamic balance of assimilation (expanding existing structures) and accommodation (transforming structures themselves).

### 6.3 One-Sentence Insight

> Schema Theory demonstrates that knowledge exists as organized mental structures which simultaneously enable understanding by providing frameworks for interpretation and constrain perception by filtering experience through existing categories — with learning occurring through the continuous evolution of these structures via assimilation and accommodation.

### 6.4 Three-Bullet Expansion

- **What:** Mental structures (schemas) that organize knowledge into coherent frameworks with slots, defaults, and constraints — serving as recognition systems, prediction generators, and memory organizers that shape perception, comprehension, and recall
- **How it works:** New information is processed through existing schemas (assimilation/β-branching when it fits, accommodation/δγ-cycling when it doesn't) — with learning occurring through schema activation, elaboration, and transformation toward increasingly integrated mental models
- **Where it aims:** Toward cognitive equilibrium and expertise — where automated, elaborate, and flexible schema networks enable expert-level understanding, efficient processing, and adaptive application of knowledge across contexts
