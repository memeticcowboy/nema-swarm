# Insight — E-Prime Language (S0DB)

## Overview

E-Prime — English-Prime — represents a modified form of English that eliminates all forms of the verb "to be." Developed by David Bourland in the 1940s, building on Alfred Korzybski's general semantics, E-Prime operates on the insight that "to be" verbs embed hidden absolutism into ordinary speech. When we say "He is lazy" or "The economy is bad," we make identity and class claims that masquerade as objective descriptions. E-Prime forces speakers to replace these deity-mode declarations with process-oriented descriptions: "He appears lazy to me" or "The economy seems to function poorly this quarter."

The transformation goes deeper than mere grammar. By eliminating "is," E-Prime dismantles what Bourland called the "deity mode of speech" — the linguistic habit of pronouncing on the essential nature of things as if from an omniscient vantage point. This restriction catalyzes a shift from dogmatic declaration to dialogic exchange, from certainty to acknowledged uncertainty, from identity claims to process descriptions. The speaker must confront their own perspective as a perspective, rather than encoding it as absolute truth through the invisible machinery of "to be."

The practical implications ripple outward from individual communication to broader epistemic culture. E-Prime promotes dialogue over declaration, encourages more precise and descriptive language, and cultivates awareness of the gap between map and territory that Korzybski placed at the center of general semantics. The discipline of restriction — the ongoing effort to express oneself without "to be" — serves as a continuous practice of linguistic hygiene that reshapes thought patterns, not just speech patterns.

## Key Tensions

1. **Absolute language vs. provisional language** — Fixed identity claims through "is" vs. process-oriented descriptions acknowledging perspective
2. **Dogmatic vs. dialogic** — Deity-mode declarations vs. genuine dialogue where uncertainty and perspective become explicit
3. **Identity statements vs. process descriptions** — "X is Y" vs. "X appears/functions/seems Y" — the observer re-enters the description
4. **Simplicity vs. precision** — Convenient "is" shortcuts vs. the demanding precision of fully descriptive language
5. **Natural speech vs. disciplined restriction** — Habitual "to be" saturation vs. ongoing E-Prime practice as linguistic hygiene
