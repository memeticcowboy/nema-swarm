# Insight — Taxonomies to Ontologies (S0AA)

## Overview

The source argues that rigid, hierarchical classification systems — derived from the Linnean model of parent/child hierarchies, labels, and silos — fail humans because they do not match how humans naturally think. While the Linnean model worked for classifying the natural world, it has limited applicability in managing business and complex information. Humans operate in relational networks where meaning arises from context and interactions, yet modern information systems have entrenched this unnatural taxonomical structure into information design, leading to information silos that obstruct data flow, misinterpretation of function due to isolated classification, and overreliance on naming conventions that fail in multilingual and cross-disciplinary environments.

The source advocates for ontological thinking in modern information systems, enabling relational multi-dimensional modeling (including Digital Twins), cross-functional lateral data connections for better decision-making, semantic interoperability across cultural, disciplinary, and linguistic boundaries, and data reuse where objects serve many purposes depending on context. The source explicitly connects this to AI success, arguing that AI systems reflect the ontological rather than the taxonomical approach to connecting and retrieving information. The prescription is to prioritize a single version of truth, structure information based on context and function rather than rigid labels, embrace ontologies over taxonomies, and reorient information systems toward integrated relational data ecosystems.

## Key Tensions

1. **Taxonomy vs. ontology** — The fundamental tension between classifying information through rigid hierarchies and organizing it through relational, contextual networks
2. **Silos vs. integration** — The tension between isolated classification that obstructs data flow and cross-functional lateral connections that enable holistic understanding
3. **Naming conventions vs. semantic meaning** — Whether information identity should rest on fixed labels (which fail across languages and disciplines) or on contextual semantic relationships
4. **Legacy systems vs. cognitive fit** — The tension between entrenched 17th-century classification paradigms and information architectures that match how humans actually think and work
5. **Single-purpose vs. multi-purpose data** — Whether data objects should be fixed in one classification or serve multiple purposes depending on semantic context
