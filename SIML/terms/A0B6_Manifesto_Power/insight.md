## Summary
Manifestos represent a distinct genre of transformative discourse that combines philosophical critique with revolutionary praxis. Through analysis of four canonical manifestos—Marx and Engels' Communist Manifesto, Marinetti's Futurist Manifesto, Haraway's Cyborg Manifesto, and Solanas' SCUM Manifesto—this entry explores how manifestos function as memetic engines for social and intellectual change.

## Key Concepts

### The Core Question
Manifestos derive their power from posing questions that destabilize existing assumptions:
- "What if the history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles?" (Marx/Engels)
- "Why should we look back, when what we want is to break down the mysterious doors of the Impossible?" (Marinetti)
- "Why should our bodies end at the skin, or include at best other beings encapsulated by skin?" (Haraway)
- "What if men, not women, were the biological accident?" (Solanas)

### The Alternate Principle
Each manifesto proposes a radical reorientation of perception:
- Materialist conception of history grounding social life in economic structures
- The beauty of speed and technology surpassing traditional artistic values
- The cyborg as hybrid creature dissolving boundaries between human/machine/organism
- Male-dominated society as fundamentally flawed and destructive

### Actionable Possibilities
Manifestos bridge critique and praxis:
- Workers uniting to overthrow bourgeoisie and establish classless society
- Embracing noise, aggression, and machine age in art and life
- Technological and biological hybridity challenging identity politics
- Women overthrowing government to establish female-led society

### Charged Symbolic Power
Manifestos deploy resonant imagery:
- The specter of communism haunting Europe; workers breaking chains
- Racing car more beautiful than Victory of Samothrace
- Cyborg as creature of social reality and fiction, post-gender world
- SCUM as metaphor for radical feminist action

## Dialectical Pairs
- **Question vs. Assumption**: Disrupting received wisdom through radical inquiry
- **Principle vs. Paradigm**: New frameworks replacing entrenched worldviews
- **Action vs. Inertia**: Mobilizing movement from philosophical critique
- **Symbol vs. Abstraction**: Embodied imagery vs. detached theory
- **Possibility vs. Constraint**: Opening new horizons vs. accepting limits
