# Insight — Epistemological Research Framework (S09C)

## Overview

The source presents a structured epistemological framework illustrating the relationships between different epistemological stances, methodologies, methods, and ontological dimensions in research. Three primary epistemological stances are distinguished: objectivist (assuming objective reality, aligned with empirical methods), constructionist (meaning through world-interaction, aligned with qualitative methods like ethnography), and subjectivist (individuals imposing meaning, aligned with interpretive approaches like phenomenology and narrative research).

Ontological dimensions underpin these stances: realist ontology (single independent reality) supports objectivism, relativist ontology (multiple socially shaped realities) aligns with constructivism and subjectivism, and dynamic process ontology (emergent contextual realities) is embedded in interpretivist methodologies. The framework highlights systemic interconnections and feedback loops between epistemology, methodology, and methods, where each level recursively influences the others. Strategic applications span education, organizational decision-making, and healthcare. Limitations include binary classifications that oversimplify epistemological overlaps, insufficient accounting for recursive and emergent knowledge formation, and the need for hybrid approaches across disciplines. The conclusion advocates for recognizing fluidity between epistemologies and embracing non-linearity, emergent complexity, and adaptive methodologies.

## Key Tensions

1. **Objectivism vs. constructionism/subjectivism** — Whether reality is independent of the observer or constituted through interaction and interpretation, and how this shapes every methodological choice
2. **Methodological rigidity vs. fluidity** — The tension between adhering to a single paradigm for coherence and embracing multiple paradigms for comprehensiveness
3. **Binary classification vs. epistemological overlap** — How the framework's clean categorical distinctions oversimplify the messy reality of actual research practice where paradigms intermingle
4. **Linear vs. recursive knowledge formation** — Whether knowledge production follows a straightforward path from data to conclusions or involves recursive loops where insights reshape foundational assumptions
5. **Universal framework vs. contextual adaptability** — Whether a single epistemological framework can accommodate the diverse needs of different disciplines, or whether hybrid approaches are always necessary
