## CONTEXT & APPLICATION

### Core Definition
Dark patterns are deceptive UX designs that manipulate users into actions benefiting companies, exploiting cognitive biases like FOMO and loss aversion. These designs prioritize corporate gain over user autonomy, eroding trust and making users feel deceived.

### Common Dark Pattern Tactics
- **Forced Continuity**: Hard-to-cancel subscriptions, hidden cancellation processes
- **Hidden Fees**: Costs revealed only at final checkout
- **Misleading Privacy Settings**: Confusing toggles that share more data than intended
- **Guilt-Tripping Language**: "No, I don't want to save money" opt-out buttons
- **Urgency Triggers**: Fake countdown timers, "limited stock" warnings
- **Confirmshaming**: Making users feel bad for declining offers

### Target Identity Roles
- Digital consumers navigating e-commerce and subscription services
- UX designers committed to ethical practice
- Regulators developing consumer protection frameworks
- Researchers studying algorithmic persuasion
- Advocates for digital rights and user autonomy

### Regulatory Responses
- **GDPR**: General data protection requirements affecting consent mechanisms
- **Digital Services Act**: Explicitly bans dark patterns in online interfaces (effective May 2024)
- **EU Unfair Commercial Practices Directive**: Prohibits misleading practices
- **FTC Guidelines**: US regulatory attention to deceptive design

### AI-Driven Evolution
AI personalization threatens to evolve dark patterns into hyper-targeted manipulation:
- Individual cognitive vulnerability profiling
- Real-time adaptation of persuasive tactics
- Exploitation of psychological states detected through behavior
- Precision manipulation that evades detection by regulators and users
