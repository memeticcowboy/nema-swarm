# Insight — Knowledge Management through Snowden's Framework (S0FC)

## Overview

This entry examines knowledge management and decision support systems through the lens of Snowden's Cynefin framework, operating across individual, team, organizational, and societal levels. The central claim is that KM/DSS design must be fundamentally adapted to the complexity domain in which it operates — what works in ordered and complicated domains (structured databases, expert systems, best-practice repositories) fails when applied to complex domains where knowledge is emergent, contextual, and resistant to formalization.

The inquiry probes several critical dimensions: the role of abductive reasoning alongside deduction and induction, the interplay between tacit and explicit knowledge, and how enabling versus governing constraints shape knowledge system architecture. Abductive reasoning — the creative generation of hypotheses from incomplete data — fills a gap that deduction and induction leave open when facing genuinely novel situations. Tacit knowledge, which resists codification, demands different infrastructure than explicit knowledge that can be captured and distributed through formal systems.

A particularly significant finding concerns the role of informal networks during crisis sensemaking. When formal KM systems encounter situations beyond their designed parameters, informal networks of relationships, trust, and shared experience consistently augment and sometimes supplant institutional structures. This reveals that robust knowledge management requires cultivating both formal architecture and the informal relational substrate that activates under stress.

## Key Tensions

1. **Individual vs. collective knowledge** — Knowledge held by persons versus knowledge distributed across teams, organizations, and societies requiring different management approaches
2. **Formal KM vs. informal networks** — Designed knowledge infrastructure versus organic relational networks that augment formal systems during crisis sensemaking
3. **Deductive vs. abductive reasoning** — Established logical modes versus the creative hypothesis-generation needed for genuinely novel complex situations
4. **Explicit vs. tacit knowledge** — Codifiable, transferable knowledge versus embodied, contextual knowledge that resists formalization
5. **Complicated vs. complex domains** — KM architectures suited for analyzable systems versus those required for emergent, unpredictable domains
