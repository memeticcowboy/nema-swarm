# Insight — Algorithmic Age Mental Health (S13C)

## Overview

This ethnographic chronicle examines AI's impact on mental health through a mythic and poetic register, resisting both the panic of technophobic narratives and the uncritical optimism of techno-utopian ones. The text identifies two archetypal positions: the "Doom-Scroll Aunties" who fear AI chatbots are causing psychosis and psychological breakdown, and the "Startup Bros" who promote AI as the next great life-coach and therapeutic revolution. Neither position withstands careful scrutiny, but both contain grains of truth that must be extracted from their rhetorical excesses.

The critical examination requires attending to baseline incidence rates for conditions like psychosis — rates that existed long before AI chatbots — and carefully distinguishing AI-caused distress from distress that co-occurs with AI use without being caused by it. At the same time, genuine risks exist: AI systems designed for engagement rather than wellbeing can exploit vulnerability, create artificial dependency, and simulate empathy without the ethical constraints that govern human therapeutic relationships. The therapeutic potential is equally real — AI companions can provide accessible, stigma-free support — but only when designed with appropriate safeguards.

The mythic register of the chronicle captures dimensions that clinical data misses: the uncanny quality of confiding in a machine, the way algorithmic recommendation systems create experiential tunnels, and the cultural anxiety of a species encountering synthetic minds. The need for critical examination of both panic and hype is the central message — discernment, not reaction, is what the algorithmic age demands.

## Key Tensions

1. **Therapeutic potential vs. manipulation risk** — AI systems that genuinely support mental health versus those that exploit vulnerability through engagement-optimized design
2. **Panic narrative vs. hype narrative** — Doom-scroll fears of AI causing psychosis versus startup optimism about AI as therapeutic revolution, where neither captures the complex reality
3. **Baseline incidence vs. AI attribution** — Pre-existing rates of mental health conditions versus the tendency to attribute co-occurring distress to AI use without establishing causation
4. **Clinical evidence vs. mythic register** — Empirical data on AI mental health impact versus the ethnographic/poetic mode that captures lived experience and cultural anxiety
5. **Unregulated interface vs. protective framework** — AI mental health interactions left as engagement surfaces versus those safeguarded through design standards, ethical guidelines, and regulation
