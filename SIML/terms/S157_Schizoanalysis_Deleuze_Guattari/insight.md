# Insight — Schizoanalysis Deleuze Guattari (S157)

## Overview

Schizoanalysis, developed by Gilles Deleuze and Felix Guattari, offers theories and techniques for understanding complex systems that exceed reductionist analysis. Rather than breaking phenomena down into simple components or tracing them back to a single origin, schizoanalysis embraces ontological heterogeneity — the recognition that reality operates through multiple coexisting registers that cannot be collapsed into one explanatory framework.

The key concepts form an interconnected apparatus. Desiring-machines are productive assemblages where desire flows through connections between heterogeneous elements — bodies, institutions, signs, materials — generating reality rather than representing it. The body without organs serves as a plane of consistency on which these flows circulate without predetermined organization. The four functors — fluxes of matter-energy, territories and codes, universes of incorporeal reference, and machinic phylum — provide analytical dimensions for engaging with systems of any complexity.

The central question driving schizoanalysis is how desire can be made to desire its own repression. Rather than treating desire as constituted by lack (the psychoanalytic model), Deleuze and Guattari understand desire as fundamentally productive. The analytical task becomes understanding how productive desire gets captured, overcoded, and territorialized into structures that turn desire against itself. Schizoanalysis is simultaneously transcendental (analyzing conditions of possibility) and materialist (attending to actual flows, bodies, and productions), combining philosophical rigor with clinical, political, and aesthetic practice.

## Key Tensions

1. **Reductionist analysis vs. ontological heterogeneity** — Approaches that collapse complexity into simple components versus schizoanalysis's embrace of irreducible multiplicity across coexisting registers of reality
2. **Isolated subject vs. desiring-machine assemblage** — The bounded individual as origin of desire versus machinic assemblages where desire flows through heterogeneous connections and the subject emerges as effect
3. **Repressive desire vs. productive desire** — Desire as constituted by lack and turned against itself through self-repression versus desire as fundamentally productive, seeking lines of flight from capture and overcoding
4. **Oedipal psychoanalysis vs. four-functor complexity** — The reduction of desire to familial triangulation versus the analytical expansion through fluxes, territories, universes of reference, and machinic phylum
5. **Territorialization vs. deterritorialization** — The establishment and maintenance of codes and territories versus the perpetual movement of decoding and deterritorialization, with reterritorialization as the dynamic counterpoint
