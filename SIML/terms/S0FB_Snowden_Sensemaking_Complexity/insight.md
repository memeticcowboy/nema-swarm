# Insight — Snowden Sensemaking and Complexity (S0FB)

## Overview

Dave Snowden's sensemaking and complexity framework, anchored by the Cynefin framework, provides a foundational toolkit for navigating different types of systems. The core insight is deceptively simple but profoundly consequential: ordered, complex, and chaotic systems each demand fundamentally different approaches to decision-making. Applying ordered-domain practices (best practice, linear cause-and-effect reasoning) to complex systems is not merely suboptimal but actively harmful. The framework demands that practitioners first discern which domain they inhabit before selecting their approach.

Central to Snowden's framework is the distinction between enabling and governing constraints. Enabling constraints create flexible boundaries that allow growth, adaptation, and innovation — exemplified by Wikipedia's editorial rules that guide without rigidly controlling content creation. Governing constraints impose restrictive boundaries that limit action to predetermined paths. The design choice between these constraint types shapes whether systems foster emergence or enforce compliance, making it a critical leverage point in organizational and systemic design.

The framework insists on the primacy of context in all decisions. No absolute statements hold in complexity — what works in one context may fail catastrophically in another. This anti-universalist stance extends to incorporating natural sciences into sensemaking methodology, using empirical findings from biology, physics, and complexity science to reduce uncertainty while acknowledging that irreducible unpredictability is a feature of complex systems, not a problem to be eliminated.

## Key Tensions

1. **Ordered vs. complex vs. chaotic** — Different system types demanding fundamentally different decision-making approaches and sensemaking practices
2. **Enabling vs. governing constraints** — Flexible boundaries fostering growth and emergence versus restrictive boundaries limiting action to predetermined paths
3. **Absolute statements vs. contextual flexibility** — The desire for universal rules versus the recognition that context determines the validity of any approach
4. **Certainty vs. uncertainty** — Deterministic expectations versus irreducible unpredictability as a permanent feature of complex systems
5. **Top-down vs. emergent approaches** — Command-and-control decision frameworks versus adaptive, emergent strategies appropriate for complex domains
