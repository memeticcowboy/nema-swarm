# Insight — Forest Carbon Restoration (G005)

## Overview

Forest restoration for carbon sequestration targets the recovery of 350 million hectares of forest by 2030, as articulated by the Bonn Challenge. The approach evaluates three primary restoration pathways: natural regeneration, which allows forests to recover with minimal human intervention and promotes biodiversity; mixed restoration combining techniques like agroforestry; and plantations that rapidly increase tree cover and carbon capture but require careful management to avoid biodiversity and soil health impacts.

The carbon sequestration potential is concentrated in tropical and sub-tropical regions where deforestation has been most extensive. Climate models and biomass data provide the analytical foundation for estimating how different restoration strategies perform under various climate scenarios, including the effects of climate change on restored forest ecosystems themselves. The analysis reveals that restoration at scale can meaningfully enhance planetary carbon capture, though it cannot substitute for direct emissions reduction.

Beyond carbon, forest restoration generates cascading benefits: biodiversity recovery as native species return, watershed restoration, soil health improvement, and community livelihood support through sustainable forestry and agroforestry. The Bonn Challenge creates an institutional framework for coordinating these efforts internationally, while evidence-based monitoring and adaptive management ensure that strategies can be refined based on measured outcomes. The urgency of the 2030 timeline underscores the need for immediate action and global cooperation.

## Key Tensions

1. **Natural Regeneration vs Plantation Speed** — Natural regeneration promotes biodiversity and resilience but proceeds slowly, while plantations capture carbon rapidly but risk monoculture simplification and soil degradation.

2. **Carbon Sequestration vs Biodiversity** — Maximizing carbon capture may favor fast-growing plantation species that suppress biodiversity, while biodiversity-rich restoration may sequester carbon more slowly.

3. **Global Targets vs Local Conditions** — The Bonn Challenge's 350-million-hectare target creates a global imperative that must be translated into locally appropriate strategies across diverse ecological, social, and economic contexts.

4. **Ecological Restoration vs Economic Pressure** — Reforesting land removes it from agricultural and extractive use, creating tension with communities dependent on those economic activities despite the long-term benefits of restoration.

5. **Climate Mitigation vs Climate Adaptation** — Restoration strategies must account for how climate change itself will affect the restored forests, requiring models that project both the mitigation contribution and the adaptation needs of restored ecosystems.
