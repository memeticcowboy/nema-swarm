## Overview

Research conducted in the semi-natural "Souris-City" environment demonstrates that individual reward-seeking behaviors in mice reliably predict their responses to nicotine. Rather than treating substance response as uniform across a population, this work reveals that pre-existing personality differences — particularly along the reward-seeking versus avoidance axis — create distinct vulnerability profiles for nicotine effects and potential addiction.

The Souris-City paradigm is significant because it allows observation of behavior in a complex, socially rich environment that more closely approximates natural conditions than standard laboratory cages. Within this setting, mice develop distinct behavioral strategies, and these strategies shift notably when individuals are isolated from peers. The social dimension of personality expression proves inseparable from individual trait profiles.

The mechanistic link runs through the dopamine system. Personality differences in reward-seeking behavior are associated with variations in dopamine system activity, and these same dopaminergic variations predict differential nicotine responses. Environmental factors — social context, habitat complexity, peer availability — shape the expression of these behavioral traits, creating a multi-layered model of addiction susceptibility that integrates personality, neurobiology, and ecology.

## Key Tensions

- **Individual Trait vs Environmental Shaping**: Pre-existing personality as predictor versus social and ecological context as active modifier of behavioral expression and substance vulnerability.
- **Social vs Isolated Behavior**: The divergence between behavioral strategies expressed in social Souris-City settings and those that emerge under isolation, revealing the social constitution of personality.
- **Prediction vs Determinism**: Behavioral profiles predicting nicotine response without fully determining it, leaving space for environmental intervention and individual variation.
