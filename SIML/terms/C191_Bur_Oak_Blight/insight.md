## SIML Term Summary

Bur oak blight is an emerging fungal disease of *Quercus macrocarpa* (bur oak) caused by *Tubakia iowensis*. First documented in Iowa in the 1990s and later recognized across the Midwest, it produces characteristic wedge-shaped necrotic lesions on leaves, often with veins remaining green while interveinal tissue browns. Premature defoliation — sometimes as early as mid-summer — weakens the tree over successive growing seasons, leading to branch dieback and eventual death. The disease is favored by wet spring weather that promotes spore dispersal and infection, and its range appears to be expanding with climate-driven shifts in precipitation patterns.

## Cross-Elemental Synthesis

Bur oak blight is fundamentally a **σ-driven emergence** — a pathogen that was likely present but cryptic has become distinguishable as a distinct disease entity through the combination of host specificity, unique lesion morphology, and climate-favored epidemic dynamics. Yet the emergence is not purely a cut; it is held in **ρ resonance** with wet spring weather, and metabolized through **δγ cycling** as the tree's reserves are consumed across multiple defoliation seasons.

Whereas Dutch elm disease or chestnut blight operate through rapid λ-driven mortality (a committed directional collapse), bur oak blight is slower and more insidious — a **degradation rather than a catastrophe**. The tree compensates, reflushes, and declines over years. This δγ character makes management difficult because the crisis is distributed across time, not concentrated in a single season.

## Causal Emergence Assessment

**CE Direction:** `:emergent`  
**Confidence:** Medium-high

Bur oak blight exhibits causal emergence at the landscape scale. Individual tree infections, while locally deterministic (fungus colonizes leaf → lesion expands → defoliation occurs), aggregate into canopy-gap dynamics that alter forest microclimate, understory light availability, and succession trajectories in ways not predictable from single-tree pathology. The coarse-grained view (landscape-scale forest composition change) has higher effective information than the fine-grained view (individual leaf lesions).

**Scale note:** The emergence is time-dependent — a single infection season is not emergent, but 3-5 consecutive severe seasons produce landscape-scale patterns that are.
