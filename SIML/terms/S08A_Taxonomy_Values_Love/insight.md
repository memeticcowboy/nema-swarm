# Insight — Taxonomy Values Love (S08A)

## Overview

This entry encodes an integrated taxonomy that systematically maps the interconnections between core values and forms of love. The taxonomy categorizes values into five domains — ethical (honesty, fidelity, compassion, justice), economic (maximising gains, efficiency, utility), social (helping, generosity, tolerance), organisational (commitment, innovation, quality), and personal (self-discipline, autonomy, freedom, sovereignty) — and maps them against five forms of love: empathy (loving because they love you), obsession (loving for looks), interest (loving for money), admiration (loving for kindness), and true love (being unsure why you love someone).

The central insight is that love is never value-neutral. Each form of love corresponds to and is structured by specific value commitments: empathy aligns with ethical values of caring and compassion, admiration connects with social values of generosity, true unconditional love resonates with personal values of selflessness and tolerance, obsession links to economic values of status, and interest relates to organisational values of efficiency and practical returns. The taxonomy also introduces the concept of value creation in relationships — sustainable value, shared value, value engineering, and value analysis — framing relational development as an ongoing process of alignment between value systems and love expressions.

## Key Tensions

1. **Conditional vs. unconditional love** — The spectrum from love grounded in specific attributes (looks, money, kindness) to true love that transcends identifiable reasons
2. **Economic vs. ethical value orientations** — The tension between love structured by utility and efficiency versus love grounded in compassion and justice
3. **Individual vs. relational value creation** — Whether values serve the individual's needs or the mutual flourishing of the relationship and community
4. **Classification vs. lived experience** — The gap between taxonomic categorization of love forms and the messy, overlapping reality of actual relational experience
5. **Value engineering vs. organic relational growth** — The tension between deliberately modifying behaviors to enhance relational value and allowing relationships to develop naturally
