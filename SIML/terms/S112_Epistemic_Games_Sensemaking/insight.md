# Insight — Epistemic Games & Sensemaking (S112)

## Overview

Epistemic games represent a paradigm shift in how we understand learning and knowledge construction. Originally developed by Collins and Ferguson for physics education, the framework identifies structured patterns of inquiry — "games" — that learners play when constructing knowledge. These are not games in the entertainment sense but formalized cognitive strategies: mapping games, modeling games, physical mechanism games, and others. The critical insight is that education should teach students not merely what to know but how to know — the meta-epistemological layer that governs which knowledge-construction strategy is appropriate for a given problem.

The sensemaking epistemic game, elaborated by Odden and Russ, reveals a specific iterative cycle: a learner perceives a gap between their current understanding and the phenomenon they face, then engages in repeated cycles of building, checking, and refining explanations until that gap closes. This process is driven by a felt sense of incoherence — an emotional-cognitive signal that current models are insufficient. The gap is not a deficit but a generative engine, pulling the learner into deeper engagement.

The broader significance extends well beyond classrooms. Epistemic fluency — the capacity to fluidly navigate multiple epistemic games — constitutes a form of cognitive resistance against algorithmic manipulation. When a person can recognize which epistemic game they are being invited to play (and can choose to play a different one), they become significantly harder to manipulate through framing effects, filter bubbles, and narrative capture. The framework thus connects pedagogy to political epistemology and information resilience.

## Key Tensions

1. **Knowledge Absorption vs. Knowledge Construction** — The fundamental tension between transmission models of education (teacher deposits knowledge into student) and constructivist models (student builds knowledge through active inquiry). Epistemic games decisively favor construction, but institutional pressures persistently pull toward absorption.

2. **Single Perspective vs. Epistemic Fluency** — Mastering one epistemic game deeply versus developing fluency across many. Depth within a single framework provides power but risks tunnel vision; fluency across many provides adaptability but risks superficiality.

3. **Passive Learning vs. Active Sensemaking** — The sensemaking cycle requires cognitive effort, emotional engagement, and tolerance for confusion. Passive learning is cognitively cheaper and institutionally easier. The tension is both individual (effort vs. comfort) and systemic (efficiency vs. depth).

4. **Algorithmic Manipulation vs. Epistemic Resistance** — The scaling question: can epistemic fluency, developed through educational games, provide genuine resistance against sophisticated algorithmic manipulation? The gap between classroom epistemology and information warfare remains largely uncharted.
