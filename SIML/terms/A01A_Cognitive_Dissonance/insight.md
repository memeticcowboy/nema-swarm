# SIML Insight: Cognitive Dissonance

## Core Definition

**Cognitive Dissonance** is the psychological tension that occurs when an individual holds two or more contradictory beliefs, values, or attitudes simultaneously, or when their behavior conflicts with their beliefs. Developed by Leon Festinger in 1957, this theory describes the uncomfortable state of mental inconsistency and the powerful drive to reduce this discomfort through belief change, rationalization, or behavior modification.

## Source Context

From Leon Festinger's 1957 theory and the Atlantic article "This Article Won't Change Your Mind":

> "The theory of cognitive dissonance, developed by Leon Festinger in the 1950s, describes the discomfort of holding conflicting thoughts."

The classic case study of Dorothy Martin's doomsday cult illustrates the phenomenon dramatically: when the predicted apocalypse failed to materialize, believers didn't abandon their worldview—they doubled down. The social and psychological investment had become too great to permit belief revision. Instead, they rationalized: the faith of the group had saved the world.

Daniel Shaw's experience with a guru accused of misconduct demonstrates identity-fusion: he "chose to believe" despite evidence because the belief was inseparable from his sense of self, community, and meaning. The dissonance between "my guru is enlightened" and "my guru abused students" was resolved not by abandoning the guru but by reinterpreting the evidence.
