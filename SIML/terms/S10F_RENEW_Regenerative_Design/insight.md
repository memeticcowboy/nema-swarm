# Insight — RENEW Regenerative Design (S10F)

## Overview

The RENEW Manifesto, developed through the University of Bath's Regenerative Design Guidebook, articulates six principles for design practice that moves beyond sustainability. While sustainability seeks to reduce harm — minimizing resource extraction, lowering emissions, decreasing waste — regenerative design seeks to actively restore and enhance the systems within which human activity operates. The distinction is qualitative, not quantitative: regeneration is not "better sustainability" but a fundamentally different design paradigm with different goals, metrics, and methodologies.

The six principles center on holistic integration (treating environmental, social, and economic dimensions as interdependent), nature alignment (mimicking ecological patterns rather than imposing human engineering logic), resilience (building adaptive capacity rather than static robustness), regeneration (targeting net-positive outcomes rather than net-zero), reflective governance (continuous evaluation rather than design-and-deploy), and interconnectivity (understanding all systems as nested within and dependent upon larger systems). Together, these principles describe a design philosophy that treats the built environment, agriculture, manufacturing, and governance as potential contributors to ecological and social flourishing rather than inevitable sources of degradation.

Circular economy principles figure prominently: waste as nutrient, outputs feeding inputs, material flows mimicking ecological nutrient cycling. But the framework extends beyond material circularity to include knowledge transmission across generations, community cohesion, and the restoration of place-based belonging. Regenerative design is not merely a technical methodology but a cultural practice that requires shifts in value orientation, success metrics, and the relationship between human activity and natural systems. The practical challenge is translating these principles into measurable outcomes and accountable governance structures without reducing regeneration to a checklist of technical specifications.

## Key Tensions

- **Sustainability vs. regeneration:** The central paradigm tension. Sustainability aims for net-zero impact; regeneration aims for net-positive impact. The shift requires fundamentally different design logics, not merely more ambitious targets.

- **Extraction vs. restoration:** Industrial design defaults to extractive logic (take, make, dispose); regenerative design inverts this toward restorative logic (source, cycle, regenerate). The tension plays out in every material decision.

- **Human design vs. natural patterns:** Engineering logic imposes human order on natural systems; biomimetic logic learns from natural systems and translates their principles into human practice. The tension is methodological: who is the teacher?

- **Local practice vs. global dissemination:** Regenerative design is inherently place-based (responding to specific ecosystems, communities, climates) yet aspires to global influence. Scaling without losing site-specificity is the core dissemination challenge.

- **Aspiration vs. accountability:** Regenerative language can become greenwashing without rigorous evaluation frameworks. The tension between visionary aspiration and measurable accountability defines the framework's credibility.

## Source Context

The source document presents the RENEW Manifesto developed through the University of Bath, positioning regenerative design as the necessary evolution beyond sustainability. It draws on biomimicry, circular economy theory, systems ecology, and participatory design to articulate principles applicable across built environment, agriculture, manufacturing, and governance. The practical emphasis on measurable outcomes and reflective governance distinguishes this from purely philosophical treatments of ecological design.
