## Summary
Cognitive Work Analysis (CWA) is a comprehensive framework developed by Jens Rasmussen for analyzing and designing complex sociotechnical systems. It focuses on understanding cognitive demands placed on workers—how they perceive information, make decisions, and adapt to dynamic environments. Unlike traditional task analysis, CWA examines the entire work system including constraints, strategies, and worker competencies.

## Key Concepts

### Work Domain Analysis
Describes system purposes, values, and constraints using abstraction hierarchy to map functional relationships. Examines what the system is designed to achieve across multiple levels from physical components to functional purposes.

### Control Task Analysis
Focuses on strategies and control tasks needed to manage the work domain. Identifies what needs to be done to achieve system goals under various conditions and constraints.

### Strategies Analysis
Examines different ways workers approach tasks, considering both optimal and suboptimal strategies. Recognizes that workers adapt their approaches based on context, expertise, and situational demands.

### Social Organization and Cooperation Analysis
Analyzes how work is distributed among team members, examining communication patterns, coordination mechanisms, and role definitions. Essential for understanding collective cognition in complex systems.

### Worker Competencies Analysis
Assesses the skills, knowledge, and abilities required by workers to perform effectively. Identifies expertise levels and training needs for safe and efficient system operation.

### Systems Thinking Approach
CWA understands work systems as wholes, including parts and their interactions. It analyzes systems at multiple abstraction levels from physical components to high-level purposes.

### Functional Modeling
Focuses on what needs to be done rather than how it's done, helping identify system functions independent of specific implementations or current practices.

### Worker-Centered Design
Considers cognitive capabilities and limitations of workers in system design, ensuring technology supports rather than hinders human performance.

### Adaptability Support
Recognizes that workers adapt to environmental changes and designs systems that support this adaptive capacity rather than constraining it.

## Dialectical Pairs
- **Abstraction vs. Concretion**: Multiple levels of system analysis from physical to purpose
- **Constraint vs. Freedom**: System boundaries that both limit and enable worker action
- **Individual vs. Collective**: Worker cognition distributed across teams and tools
- **Optimal vs. Suboptimal**: Both expert and novice strategies have value
- **Structure vs. Adaptation**: Stable systems that support dynamic worker responses
