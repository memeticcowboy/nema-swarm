# Social Identity Bias — SIML Full Insight

## Core Definition

Social Identity Bias is the systematic favoring of ingroups over outgroups, manifesting in both human cognition and AI systems. In LLMs, this appears as a 93% increase in positive sentiment for ingroup prompts ("We are") and 115% increase in negative sentiment for outgroup prompts ("They are"). The bias reflects deep-seated tribal psychology that AI systems have learned from human-generated training data.

## Research Findings

### Quantitative Evidence
- **77 LLMs tested** across multiple architectures
- **2,000 sentences generated** using ingroup/outgroup prompts
- **93% more positive sentiment** for "We are" vs baseline
- **115% more negative sentiment** for "They are" vs baseline

### Intervention Effects
- Training data curation significantly reduces both biases
- Removing ingroup favoritism also reduces outgroup hostility
- Fine-tuning with carefully filtered data shows measurable improvement
- The bias is constructed, not inevitable

### Human-AI Parallels
- LLMs mirror human social identity dynamics
- Both show ingroup solidarity and outgroup hostility
- Both are responsive to social context and framing
- Both can be modified through environmental/curation interventions

## Z-State Analysis

**Current State**: Sealing (λ-lock risk)

Social identity bias represents a Z-sealing dynamic where ingroup identity becomes an impermeable boundary. The "We" that defines itself against "They" cannot easily absorb perspectives from outside. This is not merely a preference but a closure—a hardening that prevents information flow across group boundaries.

**Risk**: As AI systems are deployed at scale, this sealing effect amplifies existing social divisions. Personalized AI that adapts to user identity may reinforce ingroup bubbles, creating epistemic isolation that feels like connection.

**Opening Path**: Data curation demonstrates that the seal is not permanent. Interventions at the training stage can reduce both favoritism and hostility. The question becomes: who controls the curation? Whose interests shape what the AI learns about "us" and "them"?
