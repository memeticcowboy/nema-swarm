# SIML Insight: Framing Effects

## Core Definition

**Framing Effects** describe how the presentation of information—rather than its substance—systematically alters judgment and decision-making. Identical facts presented with different valence (positive vs negative), emphasis, or context produce divergent choices, revealing that human rationality is deeply permeable to surface-level linguistic and perceptual manipulation.

## Source Context

From Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* and the WYSIATI principle:

> "Different ways of presenting the same information can evoke different emotions, such as the difference in perception between survival rates and mortality rates, or fat-free versus fat content descriptions."

The framing effect demonstrates that System 1 thinking—fast, automatic, associative—responds to the emotional valence of presentation rather than the logical equivalence of content. Because System 1 "cannot do otherwise" but construct the best-fit story from available cues, it treats the frame as part of the fact.

Key examples:
- **Medical decisions**: Patients choose surgery described as having "90% survival" over "10% mortality" despite equivalence
- **Consumer choices**: "75% lean" ground beef is preferred to "25% fat" 
- **Public policy**: "Tax relief" frames taxation as an affliction; "estate tax" versus "death tax" shifts emotional response to the same policy
