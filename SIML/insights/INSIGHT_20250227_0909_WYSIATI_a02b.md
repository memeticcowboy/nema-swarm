## CONTEXT NOTE

Kahneman's "What You See Is All There Is" describes the operating rule of System 1: it exploits only the information that is currently visible, automatically crafts a coherent story from it, and treats that story as valid. Because System 1 has no mechanism for representing missing evidence or alternative causes, the result is subjective certainty, over-confidence, and an absence of felt ambiguity.

**Associated biases:** Overconfidence effect, framing effects, base-rate neglect, halo effect, confirmation bias

**AI Risk:** AI-generated content exploits WYSIATI by presenting coherent but incomplete narratives that feel true because they fit together, bypassing critical evaluation of missing evidence. The fluency of AI-generated text—always coherent, never uncertain—triggers the same confidence mechanisms that WYSIATI describes, making users more susceptible to accepting incomplete or fabricated information.

## SYNTHESIS

WYSIATI reveals a fundamental architecture of human cognition: we are coherence-seeking, not truth-seeking. The feeling of knowing—subjective certainty—emerges from story coherence, not evidence completeness. This is not a bug but a feature of a cognitive system optimized for speed under uncertainty. But in an environment of AI-generated, always-coherent content, this feature becomes a vulnerability.

The six elements each illuminate a different facet of this bias:
- Air: the distinction that cannot be made
- Water: the emotional relief of coherence
- Fire: the automatic direction toward narrative
- Wood: the possibilities closed by premature closure
- Earth: the metabolic cost of certainty
- Metal: the structures that might protect against it

To work with WYSIATI is to recognize that we do not see all there is—and to build practices, relationships, and institutions that compensate for this blindness.
