## CONTEXT NOTE

Cognitive Dissonance, developed by Leon Festinger in the 1950s, describes the psychological discomfort experienced when holding contradictory beliefs, values, or attitudes—especially when confronted with evidence challenging deeply held convictions. The theory predicts that individuals will seek to reduce this discomfort through:

1. **Selective Exposure**: Avoiding contradictory information (media bubbles, echo chambers)
2. **Motivated Reasoning**: Interpreting evidence to support existing beliefs
3. **Post-hoc Rationalization**: Inventing justifications for behavior inconsistent with beliefs

Key manifestations include:
- **The doomsday cult effect**: Doubling down on beliefs after disconfirmation (Festinger's study of Dorothy Martin's UFO cult)
- **Motivated reasoning as evolutionary shield**: Protecting against manipulation by sticking to trusted beliefs
- **Social identity protection**: Beliefs as litmus tests for group belonging

In the post-truth era, cognitive dissonance mechanisms contribute to:
- Political polarization and partisan perception
- Fake news persistence and misinformation resistance
- Decline of shared epistemic standards
- Social media filter bubbles and selective exposure

The concept reveals that humans are not primarily truth-seeking creatures but meaning-making, identity-preserving, relationship-maintaining beings. Truth is often sacrificed to preserve coherence—personal, social, existential.
