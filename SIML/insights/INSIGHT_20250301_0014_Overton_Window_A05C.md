Ideas are excluded not because they are wrong but because they threaten the stability of existing power arrangements.

## Context & Connections

### Source Domain
The Overton Window emerges from political science analysis of how ideas become policy. Named after Joseph Overton, the concept describes the range of acceptable discourse and how it shifts over time. The window is not fixed—it moves through strategic framing, crisis catalysts, and grassroots mobilization.

### Key Mechanisms
1. **Normalization Through Repetition**: Repeated exposure shifts window boundaries
2. **Cognitive Dissonance**: Resistance to ideas challenging established beliefs
3. **Institutional Inertia**: Organizations favor stability over radical change
4. **Social Learning Theory**: Observational learning perpetuates conventional discourse
5. **Spiral of Silence**: Self-censorship reinforces dominant narratives
6. **Anchoring Heuristics**: New ideas evaluated against entrenched reference points

## Design Implications

### For Democratic Discourse
1. **Notice the window**: What ideas are literally unsayable in current discourse?
2. **Feel the constraint**: Who benefits from current boundaries? Who is excluded?
3. **Track the direction**: Is discourse moving toward safety or toward truth?
4. **Grow alternatives**: What radical ideas might address unsolved problems?
5. **Check the cost**: What is paid for maintaining narrow boundaries?

### For AI Systems
- Avoid reinforcing current window boundaries through training data bias
- Flag when responses assume current "common sense" as natural
- Encourage exploration of perspectives outside mainstream discourse
- Design for deliberative expansion, not just coherence with current norms

### For Social Change

- Crisis catalysts can rapidly shift window boundaries
- Grassroots mobilization builds pressure for frame expansion
- Historical examples (suffrage, marriage equality) show windows can move
