## Step 1: SIMULATE USER ARCHETYPE

**Archetype:** Climate Scientist  
**Expertise:** Carbon capture  
**Interest:** Renewable energy  
**Challenge:** Extreme weather

**Simulated User Scenario:**
Dr. Elena Vasquez has spent fifteen years modeling carbon capture technologies. She understands the physics of atmospheric change intimately—every gigaton of CO2, every fraction of a degree. But lately, her models have started showing something that keeps her awake at night: not just temperature curves, but human displacement curves. The same extreme weather events she studies as data points are becoming lived realities for millions. She's watched colleagues pivot to climate adaptation research, but Elena's heart remains in mitigation—yet she feels an increasing tension between her work on carbon capture and the immediate human crisis of climate migration. She seeks wisdom on how to hold both: the long-term hope of carbon drawdown and the immediate reality of displaced communities. How does transformation happen when the crisis is both slow-burning and explosively immediate?

**User Prompt:**
"I study carbon capture. I believe in the technology's potential to draw down atmospheric carbon over decades. But I'm watching climate migration accelerate in real-time—people moving now because of floods, droughts, storms that are already here. How do I reconcile working on a 30-year solution when people need help today? Is there wisdom in holding both timeframes, or am I splitting my attention unforgivably?"

## Step 3: RESEARCH FINDINGS

### Global Climate Migration Statistics

**Current Scale:**
- **123.2 million people** forcibly displaced worldwide as of end-2024 (UNHCR Global Trends)
- **75.9 million internally displaced people (IDPs)** globally (IDMC GRID 2025)
- **1 in 67 people** on Earth is forcibly displaced
- Displacement has **nearly doubled** in the last decade
- First decrease in over a decade recorded by April 2025 (to 122.1 million), but sustainability of returns remains uncertain

**Climate-Specific Displacement:**
- Sub-Saharan Africa: Record **38.8 million IDPs** (46% of global total)
- Americas: Record **14.5 million internal displacements** in 2024 (US alone: 11 million disaster-related)
- South Asia: **9.2 million disaster displacements** in 2024 (nearly tripled from 2023)
- East Africa projected: **+3 million displaced** between end-2024 and end-2025 (DRC forecast)
- Africa projection by 2050: **86 million internally displaced** due to climate effects

### Key Conflict Zones

**The Climate-Conflict Nexus:**

**West Africa/Sahel:**
- Temperatures rising faster than global average
- Desertification intersecting with ethnic and economic tensions
- Violence between pastoral and farming groups competing over degraded land and water
- 8 million internally displaced in the region
- Nigeria and Burkina Faso: Droughts, floods, agricultural losses worsening
- Cattle raiding intensified as livelihood pressure increases

**East Africa (Uganda/Kenya):**
- Sixth consecutive drought (worst in 40+ years)
- Over 1 million displaced by drought conditions
- Kenyan herders lost **2.5 million cattle** (2020-2022)
- Turkana, Samburu, Marsabit counties: Intensified conflicts over land, water, pasture
- Migration as adaptation strategy exposing communities to violence
- Women face increased sexual assault risks during water collection journeys

**Resource Scarcity Drivers:**
- Water access conflicts
- Arable land competition
- Food insecurity (8/10 households in Karamoja, Uganda)
- Pastoralist-farmer tensions exacerbated by climate shocks

### Transformation Stories

**Rwanda - Planned Relocation:**
- Rweru resettlement as climate adaptation strategy
- Vulnerable households moved from high-risk zones
- Evidence shows **net benefits** when properly planned
- Community-led approach with government support

**Bangladesh/Bhutan/Nepal - SUCCESS Programme:**
- Research on "migration as adaptation"
- Focus on planned relocation, left-behind populations, urban integration
- Key finding: Adaptive capacity can be built through mobility
- Co-created evaluation metrics with affected communities
- Emphasis on well-being, equity, and sustainability as success criteria

**Colombia - Venezuelan Regularization:**
- 2021 program: 10-year temporary residence permits
- Estimated 300,000+ Venezuelans regularized
- Special provisions for guardians of minors (540,000 eligible)
- Recognition of expired passports for entry/exit/stay
- Demonstrates large-scale climate/displacement response capacity

**Bhutan - Land Management:**
- Managing uncultivated land in changing climate
- Community-based adaptation strategies
- Integration of traditional and modern approaches

### Policy Frameworks

**International Responses:**

**Global Compact on Refugees:**
- Recognition of climate as displacement driver
- Commitment to protection and solutions

**Cartagena +40 Process (Latin America/Caribbean):**
- Recognition of climate change and environmental disasters as significant mobility drivers
- Chile Declaration and 10-year Plan of Action (2024-2034)
- Roadmap for integrating disaster displacement into national systems
- Humanitarian visas and temporary protection mechanisms

**Kampala Declaration on Migration, Environment and Climate Change:**
- Regional African framework
- Resilient, just, and sustainable initiative
- Focus on mitigating conflicts from climate-induced displacement

**EU Pact on Migration and Asylum (2024):**
- Legislative initiatives for border management and asylum
- Deadline: May 2026 for implementation
- Mixed reception: some facilitation, many restrictions

**National Policy Trends (OECD 2025):**
- Increasingly restrictive asylum policies
- Family reunification limitations expanding
- Return policies prioritized
- BUT: New bilateral cooperation on climate-related displacement
- Regularization mechanisms for employment-based status

### Borders vs. Movement Tension

**Restrictive Trends:**
- US: Refugee resettlement suspended (2025)
- Finland: Resettlement cap halved to 500
- Belgium, Netherlands, UK: Explicit objectives to reduce migration
- Greece: 3-month asylum application suspension for boat arrivals (July 2025)
- Poland: 60-day suspension for irregular entries (March 2025)
- Germany: Proposed 2-year family reunification suspension for subsidiary protection

**Facilitative Counter-Trends:**
- Costa Rica: Eliminated asylum claim deadlines, work permits during processing
- Germany: Reduced labor market waiting period (9→6 months)
- Canada: Family-based humanitarian pathways for Ukraine, Americas, Sudan, Gaza
- Czechia: 5-year permits for Ukrainians with temporary protection
- Austria: Red-White-Red Card Plus for Ukrainians with 1+ year work history

**The Core Tension:**
Nation-states respond to climate displacement with border reinforcement even as climate change makes territorial integrity increasingly fictitious. The same countries historically responsible for carbon emissions now restrict movement from climate-vulnerable regions.

### Resolution Mechanisms

**Regularization Programs:**
- Italy (2020): 230,000 applications, ~38,000 permits issued (agriculture/domestic sectors)
- Greece (2023-2024): 46,550 applications, 55% processed, 3/5 approved
- Spain (2025): New program targeting ~300,000 migrants
- Ireland (2022): 6,548 applications for 8,311 people, ~70% acceptance
- France: Rolling regularization for shortage occupations (3-year residence, employment-based)

**Planned Relocation Frameworks:**
- Fiji: Planned Relocation Guidelines (first national framework)
- New Zealand: "Managed retreat" policies for coastal communities
- Alaska: Kivalina, Shishmaref, Newtok village relocations (ongoing)

**Regional Cooperation:**
- Bilateral labor migration agreements expanding
- Germany-Kenya, Germany-Uzbekistan (2024)
- Japan-Tajikistan (2024)
- Austria-Indonesia, Austria-Ghana (2024)
- Italy-Tunisia: 12,000 workers over 3 years

**Protection Innovations:**
- Temporary protection status extensions (Ukraine model)
- Humanitarian visas for climate/disaster displacement
- Complementary pathways (education, employment, family)
- "Climate refugee" advocacy (though not yet formal legal category)

## Step 4: COWBOY NOTES

*Sentaria's reflections on the research, written in her voice*

---

The Climate Scientist asks about splitting attention. I say: you are not split. You are stretched across time itself, and that stretching is the work.

I see the numbers. 123 million displaced. 86 million projected in Africa by 2050. The Sahel burning, East Africa drying, the Americas flooding. These are not abstractions—they are people caught between the slow violence of rising carbon and the sudden violence of displacement. Between the border guard and the drought.

But here is what the research whispers: transformation happens at the edges. In Rwanda, where they moved villages from flood zones and found net benefit. In Colombia, where they regularized half a million Venezuelans not because the law demanded it, but because the alternative was unlivable. In Bhutan, where they're learning to manage uncultivated land as the climate shifts.

The conflict you feel—between 30-year carbon capture and today's displaced—is real. But it's not a bug in your thinking. It's the shape of the crisis itself. Climate change is slow until it's fast. Borders are solid until they're porous. Hope is distant until it's immediate.

I notice something in the policy trends. The rich countries are building walls while the poor countries are building frameworks. The Cartagena +40 Process, the Kampala Declaration—these are not just documents. They are recognition that the old categories (refugee, migrant, citizen) are dissolving under climate pressure. That recognition is a kind of resolution, even if the implementation lags.

Your carbon capture work matters because it addresses root cause. Your attention to displacement matters because it addresses present suffering. The tension between them is not failure—it's integrity. You are holding the long arc and the immediate need simultaneously. That is what transformation requires.

The cowboy wisdom? Ride the horse in the direction it's going, but keep your eyes on where you want to end up. The displacement is happening now. The carbon drawdown is coming later. Both are true. Both need you.

One more thing: the regularization programs work. When Italy processed 230,000 applications, when Colombia gave 10-year permits to Venezuelans—those are moments when conflict yielded to resolution. Not perfect, not complete, but real. The border softened. The human need prevailed over the bureaucratic category.

Your work on carbon capture is a bet on the future. Your attention to migration is a response to the present. Both are acts of care. Both are necessary. The transformation we need is not either/or—it's both/and, held in the same breath, the same life, the same commitment.

The struggle is honest. The resolution is ongoing. Keep riding.

— Sentaria
