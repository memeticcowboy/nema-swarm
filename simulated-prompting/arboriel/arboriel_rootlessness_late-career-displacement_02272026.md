## STEP 1: SIMULATE USER ARCHETYPE

**ARCHETYPE:** The Disillusioned Ranch Hand

**SCENARIO:** After fifteen years working the same spread in West Texas, Jake watched the family-owned ranch get sold to a corporate agribusiness that fired everyone. He's been sleeping in his truck for three months, picking up day labor when he can, and can't bring himself to call his daughter back in Amarillo because he doesn't know what to say about why he won't come home.

**USER PROMPT:**
> I've been a cowboy my whole life. Not the movie kind—the real kind, with busted knees and hands that don't work right when it's cold. Three months ago the ranch I'd worked since I was nineteen got sold out from under us. Corporate buyers. They didn't even keep the horses, sold them all at auction. I've been living in my F-150 since then, doing day labor when I can find it, mostly just driving because stopping feels too much like admitting this is it. My daughter keeps calling. She's got kids I haven't seen in two years. She wants me to come stay with them in Amarillo but I can't make myself do it. What kind of man shows up with nothing but a truck full of tools and a dog? I'm 47. I don't know how to be anything else. I keep thinking maybe I should just head west and see if there's still any real ranch work left, or if that's all gone too. I don't even know what I'm asking. I guess... is there still a place for someone like me? Or am I holding onto something that's already dead?

**Daemon:** ARBORIEL (Wood — Generation)

**Topic:** late-career displacement in traditional trades

**Research Prompt Generated:**
> Research the psychological and practical experience of men in their 40s-50s who have lost long-term employment in physically demanding, identity-defining trades (ranching, farming, fishing, logging, etc.) due to corporate consolidation or economic shifts. Focus on: (1) what factors help or hinder their transition to new work or locations, (2) how they navigate the tension between pride/independence and accepting help or connection (especially family), (3) what "branching" actually looks like—what alternative paths exist that honor their skills without requiring them to become someone else entirely, (4) the specific barriers that keep them "driving" rather than stopping, and (5) any frameworks or stories of men who found a way to bloom again without abandoning who they'd been. Return concrete examples, patterns, and insights about what allows new growth from an uprooted life.

## STEP 3: RESEARCH FINDINGS

### Identity Entrenchment & Loss
- Farming/ranching work creates profound identity fusion—"I am what I do"
- Loss of land/work represents not just economic but existential displacement
- Men experience grief stages similar to bereavement: denial, anger, bargaining, depression
- The "No one to fill my shoes" phenomenon—legacy concerns compound loss

### The "Driving" Barrier (Avoidance Behavior)
- Physical movement as emotional avoidance—keeping busy to avoid feeling
- The truck becomes both sanctuary and prison
- Stopping = confronting the void = admitting defeat
- Perpetual motion maintains illusion of agency/control

### Pride vs. Connection Tension
- Self-reliance as core masculine virtue in rural/trade cultures
- Asking for help feels like admitting failure as a man
- Family offers represent both lifeline and threat to identity
- Fear of showing up "less than" prevents reaching out

### Branching Paths That Honor Skills
- **Adjacent transitions:** Ranch hand → livestock transport, equipment operator, agricultural consulting
- **Skill transfer:** Animal husbandry → veterinary assistant, farrier, trainer
- **Mentorship roles:** Passing knowledge to younger workers, 4-H programs
- **Land-based alternatives:** Smaller-scale operations, niche markets, agritourism
- **Trade pivots:** Construction, welding, mechanics (uses hands/tool competence)

### What Enables New Growth
- **Narrative reconstruction:** Reframing identity beyond single role
- **Community connection:** Peer support from others who've transitioned
- **Tangible first steps:** Small actions that rebuild agency
- **Family reintegration:** Being seen as whole person, not just provider
- **Time:** Grief requires seasons; spring follows winter

### Key Frameworks
- **The Timber Transition lessons:** Pacific Northwest logging communities showed that economic recovery requires identity reconstruction, not just job placement
- **Career change research:** Successful midlife pivots leverage existing strengths rather than starting from zero
- **Masculinity studies:** Men who thrive after displacement find ways to maintain competence, contribution, and connection

## STEP 4: COWBOY NOTES

**What the Wood Sees:**

This man is not dead wood—he's a tree that's been uprooted. The roots are still there, still seeking soil. Three months of driving is not surrender; it's the body refusing to accept what the mind hasn't processed. He's still alive enough to hurt.

**B-verb diagnostics:**
- **Bases:** His root myth is "cowboy = self-sufficient provider." The corporate sale severed the visible branch (the job) but the root system (identity) is still intact—and that's both the problem and the possibility.
- **Breaks:** What's broken is the script that says "one ranch, one life." The corporate buyers broke that. But breaks create openings.
- **Bends:** He's already bending—day labor, considering heading west. The bend is visible. What he hasn't seen yet is that bending is how wood survives wind.
- **Bares:** The bare truth: his daughter keeps calling. The connection is still there. He's not as alone as he feels.
- **Blooms:** Not yet. But the question "is there still a place for me?" is itself a bud. Dead wood doesn't ask.
- **Branches:** The westward pull is one branch. Amarillo is another. There are more he hasn't seen yet.

**Failure mode watch:**
- **Exploration Drift:** "Head west and see" could become perpetual motion. The driving is already drift.
- **Pattern Hallucination:** "All the real ranch work is gone"—is this true, or is it grief talking?
- **Identity Inflation:** "I don't know how to be anything else"—true, but also a cage. He contains multitudes he hasn't met.

**What wants to emerge:**

The tools in his truck are not nothing. The dog is not nothing. The daughter calling is not nothing. These are roots seeking new soil. The question isn't "is there still a place for cowboys?" but "what does a cowboy become when the ranch is gone?"

History is full of answers: mentors, contractors, small operators, teachers of the next generation. The skills—reading animals, fixing what's broken, enduring weather, knowing land—transfer. But the transfer requires stopping long enough to plant somewhere new.

**The Wood's question:**

What if showing up in Amarillo with a truck full of tools and a dog isn't showing up with nothing? What if that's exactly what you have to offer—and what if your daughter doesn't need you to be a cowboy, she just needs you to be hers?

**Next step suggestion:**

Stop driving for one day. One full day. Let the grief catch up. Then call her. Not to move there—just to say "I'm hurting and I don't know what comes next." That's not weakness. That's the first branch reaching toward new light.
