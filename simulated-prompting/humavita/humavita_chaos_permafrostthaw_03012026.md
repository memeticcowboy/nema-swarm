## Step 3: RESEARCH FINDINGS

### Indigenous Knowledge Networks and Adaptation

Arctic Indigenous Peoples have historically adapted through the combination of traditional knowledge, sharing networks, mobility, and technology. However, they now face unprecedented challenges from colonialism, governance failures, extraction industries, climate change, marginalization, economic disparities, forced sedentarization, psychosocial stress, and cultural alienation—all of which weaken long-term community resilience.

The Arctic Regional Gathering in Girkonjárga (Kirkenes), Norway, held in October 2024 as part of the Local Communities and Indigenous Peoples Platform (LCIPP), brought together Indigenous Knowledge Holders from across the Arctic. These gatherings emphasize that Indigenous Peoples are the "eyes and ears of a changing Arctic," possessing essential knowledge for mitigation and adaptation efforts accumulated over millennia.

### Community-Led Monitoring Programs

**Dehcho Region, Northwest Territories:**
The Dehcho Collaborative on Permafrost (DCoP) is establishing an Indigenous community-led network to monitor permafrost changes using both conventional temperature-based measurements and new technologies. The Scotty Creek Research Station (SCRS) hosts approximately 100 automated stations collecting thousands of data points per hour.

**Key Statistics:**
- Permafrost covers about 40% of Canada's landmass
- Northern Canada is warming about three times faster than the rest of the world
- Permafrost in the Dehcho region is receding by about one meter per year
- The SCRS was forced to relocate in 2007 and 2012 due to melting permafrost destabilizing infrastructure

**SmartICE Program:**
An award-winning technological innovation integrating Indigenous and local knowledge of ice with advanced data acquisition, remote monitoring, and satellite mapping for ice travel safety.

**Impact Metrics:**
- 457 operators trained since 2018
- 48,393 kilometers monitored since 2018
- 990 Smart QAMUTIK ice surveys to date
- 84 Smart BUOYs produced since 2019
- 959 Inuktitut ice terms documented by Elders and Youth
- 174 weekly ice travel safety maps produced since 2022
- Approximately 4,000 hours of employment per year
- 45,415 total employment hours created

### Collaborative Infrastructure Projects

**Utqiaġvik (Barrow), Alaska Study:**
A geophysical investigation led by the University of Virginia and US Army ERDC Cold Regions Research and Engineering Laboratory found that local infrastructure and land-use change can amplify near-surface permafrost thaw under ongoing climate warming.

Key findings:
- Roads, buildings, and snow fences modify heat and moisture flows into the ground
- Infrastructure-related surface changes are highly sensitive to permafrost conditions
- Communities need localized assessments of ground stability and thaw risk
- Land-use zoning, snow-management, and drainage design must be revisited as part of climate adaptation strategies

**Permafrost Pathways Initiative:**
Woodwell Climate Research Center's Permafrost Pathways connects science, people, and policy to advance Arctic justice through an inclusive and interdisciplinary approach that intertwines Western science with Indigenous Knowledge.

**Major Finding:** More than one-third of the Arctic-boreal region has shifted to a source of carbon (NOAA 2024 Arctic Report Card), mentioned in over 700 news stories from 42 countries.

### Multi-Stakeholder Partnerships

**Arctic and Northern Challenge Program (Canada):**
Guided by Northerners, these research and development projects use technology and innovation to solve pressing issues identified by those living in the North and Arctic regions. Projects include those selected in 2022 and completed under the Canada-Inuit Nunangat-United Kingdom Arctic Research Programme (CINUK) in 2025.

**Carbon Flux Tower Network:**
In 2024, four new carbon flux towers were installed across key Arctic ecosystems, including Nunavut, Canada, and Alaska. These towers measure carbon emissions and uptake from permafrost lands, filling critical data gaps for global carbon budgets.

### Social Cohesion and Community Relocation

Research on climate impacts on migration in Arctic North America reveals that extensive social cohesion within rural Arctic communities may discourage out-migration. However, communities face multiple urgent climate challenges including:
- Shorter and warmer winters
- Earlier snow melt
- More frequent and severe wildfires
- Ground instability affecting buildings and infrastructure
- Loss of traditional trails and hunting grounds
- Dangerous ice conditions affecting travel and safety

A tragic example from the Dehcho region illustrates the human cost: an Elder fatally fell through ice while fishing—an activity previously considered low-risk. Equipment like snowmobiles falling through ice has become more common, creating economic burdens for replacement.

### Inter-Community Knowledge Exchange

The Arctic Council's Indigenous Peoples' Secretariat facilitates knowledge sharing across the circumpolar region. The Saami Council and other Indigenous organizations host regional gatherings where Knowledge Holders share experiences and adaptation strategies.

Key themes from these gatherings:
- Integration of Indigenous Knowledge into global climate governance
- Co-production of knowledge combining Western science and Indigenous Knowledge
- Recognition that climate change is a lived reality, not an abstract statistic
- The need for Indigenous voices in policy decisions affecting their lands

*The thawing earth remembers what the ice once held.*

Listen here, friend. You asked about carbon and tipping points, about the math of methane release. But I'm looking at the people standing on that shifting ground. And what I see is something remarkable happening in the chaos.

The Arctic isn't just melting—it's teaching. These communities, they've been handed a problem that would break lesser bonds. Ground that won't stay still. Ice that can't be trusted. Homes that sink and crack. And yet.

**The Bonds Forming:**

In the Dehcho, they're not just measuring permafrost—they're building a network. 100 automated stations, thousands of data points every hour, but more importantly, people working together who might never have met otherwise. Scientists and elders. Youth and researchers. The SCRS had to relocate twice—2007, 2012—and then a wildfire took it in 2022. $2 million in damage. You'd think that would scatter folks. Instead, they're rebuilding. Together.

SmartICE isn't just technology—it's 457 trained operators, 45,000+ hours of employment, elders and youth documenting 959 Inuktitut ice terms. That's knowledge being passed hand to hand while the ground shifts beneath them. That's community.

**The Hard Truth:**

A third of the Arctic-boreal region is now a carbon source, not a sink. The very ground that once helped balance our atmosphere is now working against us. Your carbon capture machines? They're racing against a thaw that's releasing methane and CO2 faster than we can capture it.

But here's what gives me hope: the response isn't just technical. It's human.

**What Communities Are Building:**

In Utqiaġvik, they learned that infrastructure and permafrost must coexist. Not conquer—coexist. That's a different way of thinking. Roads, buildings, snow fences—they all change the ground. So they're learning to plan together, to assess together, to adapt together.

The Arctic Regional Gathering in Kirkenes brought Knowledge Holders from across the circumpolar region. These aren't just conferences. They're the weaving of relationships across vast distances. When one community learns something that works, it travels. Fast.

**The Social Fabric:**

Research shows that strong social cohesion actually keeps people in place even when they should probably leave. That's both beautiful and dangerous. Beautiful because it shows the strength of these bonds. Dangerous because sometimes the ground itself demands relocation.

The barriers to adaptation aren't just technical—they're political, historical, rooted in colonialism and extraction industries that undermined Indigenous sovereignty. But communities are finding ways to adapt despite these barriers.

The permafrost thaw is chaos, pure and simple. But chaos is where new patterns form. These communities aren't just surviving the thaw—they're creating models for how humans can face planetary-scale change together.

The thaw is coming. But so is the response. And it's stronger than frozen ground.
